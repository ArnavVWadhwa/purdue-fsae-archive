Fuel Tank Design Task

Purpose:

This for new members to learn some of the software tools used on the Powertrain sub-team. 

Overview:

One of the main components to an internal combustion powered car is the fuel tank. The fuel tank serves as an energy storage compartment for the vehicle. In Formula SAE, fuel tank design is mostly open ended, with constraints built into the rule book for safety. Your task will be to take data from one lap around the endurance track in order to estimate the amount of fuel the car will need to complete an entire endurance run (which will take several laps). When choosing a target size for your fuel tank, do not forget to add a factor of safety! We do not want the vehicle to run out of fuel on the last lap. If you make the fuel tank too big, it will weigh the car down. However, if you make it too small, we will not be able to finish the endurance race. Keep in mind that the fuel tank will need to fit within a small, tight location on the car and it will need to pass all of the rules in the rule book.

Some other things to consider here are: Fuel slosh when the car is turning (slosh is bad, how can we reduce this?), Leaking (how many welds are we making if any? less welds = less leaks), how easy is this to make? (Is it many parts? are the sheet metal bends reasonable?), how is it mounted? (will it flop around or vibrate a lot?) How will the tank be filled? Where will fuel be taken out or re-introduced? (see image above for example) What material would you make this out of? What shape should it be? any any other things you can think of to consider when designing a fuel tank for a racing application, that you can assume you would have to actually make in real life and rely on to function properly 100% of the time.


The FSAE rule book will be your main guide to designing the fuel tank. It can be found HERE.

Task:

1. The endurance race is approximately 22 kilometres. For this specific track, 22 kilometers translates to 11 laps. Attached below is Power vs Time data for a single lap. Calculate the amount of Fuel Required to drive the entire 22km. The energy density of gasoline is 34.2 MJ/L. Assume a 40% energy efficiency. (Hint: you will need to use Excel or Matlab to estimate the area under the curve of the data. ) 

I recommend using MATLAB and the built in functions, but if you don't know MATLAB you can find the area under the curve in excel like this.


2. Now that you have calculated the estimated amount of fuel required. Add a factor of safety to the amount of fuel required, and use this target to size your fuel tank. 


3. Review the FSAE Rulebook and constantly reference it while you are designing. The next step will be CAD. Open the file attached below. This file will be your main assembly in which you will work out of. It has critical reference points that you will use to design the fuel tank. As this is file is your main assembly, you have the option of making a sub-assembly for the rest of your parts. Organization of the parts within assemblies will be up to you. Potential Parts that you might have include but are not limited to: Fuel tank body(s), baffles, fuel pump brackets, fuel pump, filler neck, fuel cap, fuel lines, hydramat, connectors, chassis mounted tabs, rubber mounts, sticky foam, etc. Purchased parts, like the hydramat and fuel pump CAD will be provided to you in the file. Attached below. Additional connectors or caps you may need can be designed by either you, or downloaded from an online source such as McMaster-Carr. Don't forget to assign proper mass properties to each component. We don't want a small fitting to weigh 60,000 pounds! Treat your CAD tree with care. You are going to be spending lots of time with it, and when we check it you will want it to look nice. It is significantly easier to work with well organized, properly named parts and assemblies compared to a mess where everything is named part 1,2,3,4 in one single assembly.

4. The overall geometry of the fuel tank is up to you. However due to space constraints some of the fuel tanks we design look like this: (pictures are for reference to see how the tank sits within the rest of the vehicle assembly)

 

Things to keep in mind include:

  • What is the best material to use? You might consider weight, corrosion, and complexity to manufacture (Sheet metal bending complex? Welding difficulty?)
    • Different alloys have differences in bending capability. 
  • Slosh. How can you design the fuel tank to eliminate harsh slosh of fuel while the car is turning?
    • Baffles? What shape should they be? How will the baffles be installed.
    • Shown below is the current baffling setup, feel free to be creative in your own design. Copying is easy but doesn't teach you much- especially if you copy something without understanding it.


  • Installation of the Hydramat. The Hydramat will need a opening of about 10" x 4" on the top of the fuel tank in order to be inserted in. (See the rectangular weld on the picture of the fuel tank? That is the 10" x 4" hole.) The Hydramat will sit 3.5 amount of inches below the inside of the top plate. Screenshot shown below. The hydramat should sit near the bottom of the fuel tank, in order to be able to pick up fuel from the lowest point in the tank. (The top of the inside of the tank, and the bottom of the inside of the tank should be 3.5in for the hydramat to fit correctly.)


  • Routing the Fuel Lines
    • You may need to download CAD files from McMaster for individual connectors you may need. 
    • In the Main CAD file there is already a connector to the fuel rail (where you will connect the output of the fuel pump), and the fuel pressure regulator, (which you will connect to a return on the fuel tank.)
    • Hint: Fuel Lines are routed by creating a coordinate system datum at each end at the fitting, and then drawing a 3d spline between the two fittings. 
    • Use at least two points at each fitting to start, to force the spline to start colinear to the fitting. 
    • Add points on the spline to adjust curvature, move points only one axis at a time to make it easier. Keep in mind line material and flexibility, braided steel cannot bend a sharp 90 degrees. When the spline is created, use the "tube" command to create a hollow tube with the same OD and ID as the line, don't forget to assign proper mass properties. 


5. Have fun and work with other new members on calculations and designs. Teamwork is essential in creating a successful Formula Team. Some of the new members you may meet throughout this process may become team leads themselves in the future! This project is designed to acclimate you to the design process of parts for the Formula SAE car. Hop on the discord if you need help and a Team Lead or returning member may be able to assist you. In order to successfully complete the project, your fuel tank must pass all rules stated in the FSAE rulebook.
