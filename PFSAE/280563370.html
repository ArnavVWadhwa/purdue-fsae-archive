REDDIT R/PRINTEDCIRCUITBOARD FEEDBACK

  • Robust future proofing & redundancies for extended use in future applications (General data acquisition, secondary ECU for light tasks, etc)

    • DC/DC buck converter 6.5V - 32V input with built-in short circuit protection with no heat sink required
      • 12V and 5V power OK LEDs
    • micro SD slot for onboard data logging, 2x non-voltaile SRAM (16Mb total) for high-speed onboard storage
      • Assuming each sample size of 16 Bytes with a sampling rate of 500Hz and 12 strain gauges, 16,000,000 / (16 x 500 x 12) = 166 seconds, or over 2.5 minutes of SRAM sampling
    • 32Kb EEPROM for storing config and calibration settings
      • can quickly recall info on boot-up
    • exposed connectors for easy access to critical comms lines
      • CAN, UART, I2C, etc.
    • 2.54mm connectors for easy expandability with "shield" type add-on boards
      • flexible and easily add more features for future use
    • Ultra high-precision RTC with battery backup
      • ~3 seconds drift per month, temperature compensated, 10 years battery with onboard coin cell
    • Twin RGB Neopixels for status indication

BOARD STACKUP (4 layers):

  1. Signal & ground fill
    1. All signals + power lines
  2. low-speed, low-priority digital signals & ground fill
    1. Added later in the design process, almost like an afterthought (but not really)
  3. Power plane (3.3V)
  4. Signal & ground fill

Going clockwise from the top-left corner:

  • ARM Cortex standard debug connector 2x5 1.27mm pitch shrouded male headers

  • Micro SD Card slot

  • XT-30 battery connector, 6.5V-32V input range
  • 2x LEDs for 3.3v and 5v OK status
  • Mini-USB connector for data and power

Plans for when boards arrive:

  1. Check for shorts in all critical traces with multimeter in continuity mode
    1. all power lines, CAN bus, SPI/I2C devices
  2. Solder all parts
    1. Solder in reverse height order!!! This means soldering the shortest components first. Resistors, capacitors, etc. Thin components
    2. Thru-hole parts should go last!!!!
  3. Test again for shorts with multimeter. Run back step 1
  4. Clean with alcohol and toothbrush, cotton swabs.
  5. Test again for shorts.
  6. Plug in power. Test with multimeter all power lines.
    1. Test both USB power and battery input. Test if switchover works seamlessly.
  7. Test USB comms. See if code upload works. Reset/Boot0 mode check as well
  8. Test CAN comms.
  9. Test everything else + start writing firmware

One CRAM board assembled and tested.

CONFIRMED WORKING: PSU, power management circuitry, STM32, DFU. Yet to test other peripherals.

Fabrication took about 2 hours.

Debugging took about 3.5 hours.

At first, didn't work. problem was in a diode connected to USB 5V that I think knocked input voltage down too low for board to function.Â Replaced diode with a short and board works fine.

However, PSU is a little weird. When powered by USB, the microcontroller and surrounding peripherals get supplied only 3.2V instead of 3.3V. Not a big deal but annoying.

PSU area of the board gets slightly warm with USB power. Can be felt to the touch but nothing to worry about.

Everything is nominal when running off battery power. Tested up to 12V 1A. So the issue above shouldn't be a problem when running on the car.

Battery/USB switchover works perfectly! CRAM automagically switches over to USB supply if both USB and battery is connected. However, USB LED doesn't light up when powered by USB. I mean it does just very faintly.

2.048V precision voltage reference outputs 2.046 volts, very annoying but not critical for most applications

LEDs are too bright but whatever

1/21/2024: CRAM PSU benchmark

Above are oscilloscope measurements of 3.3V out from POTR262's lab bench power supply. It's just one signal zoomed in at three different levels. There is noise, but not a whole lot.

Above is the 3.3V output from CRAM's power supply with an input of 12.5V. It is visibly quite a bit noisier, but measurements on the left read similar values. I'm a bit concerned about the higher Peak-to-peak on CRAM.

These were measured with x10 attenuation, so measurements read in millivolts when they're actually volts. i.e. 330.77mV means 3.3077V.

The two were all zoomed into identical levels. 38us, 530ns, 49ns per horizontal division for each zoom level. No idea why the bench power supply is spread out wider than CRAM PSU even with the same horizontal zoom level.

This is all very new to me so I have no idea if the differences between the two are significant or negligible. My target was to make the power supply as noiseless and clean as possible. Obviously there is some noise in CRAM's PSU, but overall I think it's still a solid stable power source. No concerns, at least not until we start measuring sensitive analog stuffÂ cough strain gaugesÂ cough

So yeah the USB power issues were cuz of my laptop's shitty USB ports, not CRAM. CRAM works fine for the most part.

2/5/2024

CRAM takes about 2.9us to take 4 samples via DMA. 2.9ms for 4096 samples! 175kHz sample rate for 4 samples per sample. That's 700kSps!!!!

However, we need to take into consideration the time it takes to transmit data over CAN. That will take up a significant amount of time, so transmitting over CAN after each sample is not the way.

Currently testing with UART in place of CAN as Hunter has the CAN dongle. I get approx. 115sps with CRAM sending each sample over UART. Good enough for dash switches, waaaayyyyy too slow for strain gauges!!!


2/6/2024

Made simple Hardware-In-Loop (HIL) test jig. Small PCB plugs on top of CRAM. It has a bunch of switches and pots to simulate car dash.

2.048V ADC Vref removed, shorted Vref to 3.3V supply. Now Vref is 3.307V. It seems pretty stable, so no huge worries here.

Added resistors to make WHAM EXC+ 3.315V instead of original 2.5V.

CRAM can now spit analog readings in millivolt values. 0-3307.

Added 100uF capacitor for LT1054, IT DOES NOT WORK! WHAM PSU is not working!!!

*** BENCHMARK RESULTS: ***

  • VDD-tied analog Vref seems more stable than I thought! Stays at a nice 3.307V with no observable sway. around 11mV pk-pk, 3-4mV when zoomed in. Not sure which is real. Overall nice results!
  • Analog measurement is also somewhat precise. CRAM's analog measurement and real-life DMM measurements are 2-4 millivolts apart. Great for switches, bad for strain gauges!!!
  • I haven't done much testing for drift, but there seems to be slight drift. I had CRAM read a constant voltage (its own power supply, 3.307V) for about 10 minutes and plotted an Excel graph. Slope was -4E-6, so slight negative drift

FINAL THOUGHTS: CRAM analog measurement precision is solid but needs to be much better if we want to get serious with strain gauge stuff. Currently it is only good for rough strain measurement. I think we can make it better with some post-processing either on CRAM or i2, but that will slow down sample speed. Speaking of, sampling speed is currently capped at 330-ish SPS and idk how to make it higher.
