Installation of the Rekluse clutch pack, would come the most easiest by removing the engine from the car. 

The removal of the drivetrain and exhaust connections would be the efficient way of having enough space to input the pack.  

When it comes to tuning adjusting the slave cylinder is sufficient enough, but the amount of space that is given to work on it may not be enough, So to tune we would 

have to make the engine not only accessible, but have a enough room to at least adjust the slave cylinder.

How it works: 

 

  • Note that the system is purely mechanical and uses rotational inertia alongside wedges to expand two disks to then engage the clutch. This system is kept in place by fasteners (in the top left of the image you can see one with a flathead insert on top), that are connected to springs. These springs act in opposition to the expansion of the disk.  
  • Step By Step Operation: 
    • Engine is in Gear 
    • Engine is revved, rotating our EXP disk 
    • Wedges begin to move outward as a function of engine rpm/torque 
    • Clutch is then engaged when wedges reach a certain radial distance, connecting to the drivetrain 
    • Power is transmitted 
    • When engine rpm/ torque decreases the spring fasteners will then bring the disk back together, disengaging the clutch and cutting power to the drivetrain 
      • This is how the auto clutch avoids engine stalls 
  • System is implemented into transmission case within engine itself which means that tuning/maintenance must be done with engine outside of vehicle 

 

Pros of System 

Cons of System 

  • Makes driving the car much easier as it’s practically impossible to stall 
  • System is entirely tunable 
  • Offers consistency in clutch actuation 
  • Still need to manually shift 
  • Hard to tune on the fly, need to remove engine to get to transmission casing 
  • Less expensive models don’t allow for normal clutch operation 

 

Tunability: 

  • You can tune the auto clutch with a couple different methods all internal to the disk and transmission case 
  • Varying the spring stiffness via OEM kits, this will increase the force/engine rpm required to engage the clutch and will make the clutch disengage sooner at the top end of our operating rpms. Similarly, you can tune it the other way to decrease engagement rpm 
  • Varying the weights of the wedges via OEM kits, this will increase rotational inertia and essentially give you more force at rotation. 
  • Easier to do this after maxing out tunability with springs 
  • Tuning will allow for a few things: 
    • Variation of clutch engagement rpm 
      • Most noticeable into and out of a corner 
    • Variation of fuel consumption 
      • If only engaged at higher RPMs will be consuming more fuel (dependent on RPM changes)
    • Smoother/Harder Clutch actuation 
      • Softer Springs will engage smoother while stiffer ones will engage harder (dumping clutch essentially) 
  • NOTE: In further research I have found that engagement can vary from slightly over idle to about 3,000 rpm

 

Integration Process to PF22: 

  • Integrating the Auto Clutch should be done in this process to ensure accurate research and quality engineering-based decisions 
    • A Matlab sim which charts engine rpm vs spring rate for customizability for data to use in the lapsim handled by Suspension 
    • The use of the lapsim to determine the possible gain of throttle on time as a result of clutch engagement rpm 
    • Use of the auto clutch during testing sessions, typically this would result in the tuning of the clutch which would require removing the engine and disassembling the transmission case either out on the track or at the end of the day. 

 

Analyzing the use case of Accel event runs: 

  • The auto clutch system can give you consistent clutch engagement during an accel run however you also must realize that launching the car and travelling through a low-speed corner are two very different operations which would have their own desirable engagement rpms. This means you either have to change the springs in the auto clutch between events or go for a configuration that allows for the use of a clutch handle for accel runs. 

 

Overall Conclusion: 

Everything about this clutch system points to “ease of use” for newer drivers and motocross enthusiasts to eliminate the thought of clutching in and out while driving their bikes. In the realm of Formula SAE it comes out to be something similar as it would make driving a lot simpler and easier but wouldn’t offer many performance benefits given current data (the lapsim could tell us different). Regarding acceleration runs we would need to tune the clutch alongside a torque curve however you would then run into the issue of having the clutch engage at the same rpm as it would during acceleration runs. 

Personally, I’d recommend that we focus on tuning and integrating the WR450F before we investigate an auto clutch for performance as we currently have drivers that can handle using the clutch during normal operations. However this does not mean that integrating it for ease of use is a bad idea, it ultimately falls to the Powertrain Lead and the Chief Engineer to determine whether they want the clutch system.
