ANSYS tests on overhead intake:

After much delay, the overhead intake (the second design) was run through Ansys. Meshing was very similar to before, but care was taken in making sure that the thin tabs had multiple elements along with their thickness so that results were not misleading. The small wall thickness in the main plenum, 0.1 inches compared to 0.22 for pf20, meant that the mesh along this area could not have mesh sizes too large. For right now a sweep element size of 0.015 was used. The next run, which will include the updated intake model coming from these results, will use shell meshing, which should help in this area with accuracy and computation time. Mesh quality and jacobian ratio looked very good, with problem areas either caused by hex dominant mesh (tabs) or localized on areas not important to structural results (diffuser and t.b. only present to accurately replicate real-life weight distribution and are especially dummy solids). Averages for each are shown below with j.r. being well above the set average goal of 0.66. One other issue with the setup was the location of the XYZ vectors, not lining up with the principle directions of the intake in the car (front, rear, left, right, etc.). This posed a problem with defining inertial loads corresponding to acceleration, braking, cornering, and shock loads. However, since these values (see confluence page on designed maximum values) are fairly close together and the vectors were not off by too far, they were just left as is and each acceleration value was assigned to the closest one. This will obviously be fixed for final structural tests.

Results looked very good, especially in the region of the tabs. The tab ring behaved very well, and tab layout led to considerably less stress concentrated on the bolt holes than before. There was a presence of stress concentrated on the end bulkhead of the plenum, however, this could have been partially due to the lack of edge bends on the interior of the plenum in this location. The way this was made in cad was through a rudimentary extrusion and unite command, rather than capping the ends off using the shell command. This jank way of doing it could have not been the best structurally and was replaced with the aforementioned capping method. The wall thickness here may also be increased if this problem still persists. Another issue around here was that the small flange on the runner was modeled as a solid element as opposed to two parts. This may end up being the case depending on how material selection goes since the runner is a problem area for potential failure (will be investigated through dyno testing and ANSYS random vibration testing), however, the runner still may be printed using aluminum SLS. Either way, the geometry will stay the same since the flange doubles as support (due to its orientation along the runner's cross-section) and as a way to mount tabs and fuel accessories.

Tab results are shown below. Note that the final tab design for the diffuser section will likely be carbon fiber rod + rod ends instead of fixed flat tabs to increase strength, inertia, and flexibility and decrease weight. This is supported by the slight increase in strain on one of the tabs circled below. A greatly exaggerated image showing deformation shows the slight lack of rigidity in the tabs, especially on the ring assembly. One corner of the ring will be adjusted based on results to have more of an edge bend as opposed to a corner (shown below).

The last image is a comparison with past intake design tab results showing how much better this method is.

varying plenum pressure from an actual run (note: came from R6 engine)

After this run, necessary updates will be made to the intake, as well as a CAD "clean up" to make the model more top-down. The plenum will be also slightly increased in size as it is on the smaller end. Where this size increase happens is shown above. Note that it has to happen at the end since the plenum flanges are already close to the chassis. After this, the model will be updated in Ansys and the computing will be run again. Directions of acceleration and cornering loads will be reversed to see if that changes results. Also, pressure loading will be made cyclic to better mimic actual conditions. In the workbench, you can define a sinusoidal function with an amplitude (101325 pa + testing value since gauge pressure has been used). Cyclic loading has a more negative effect on potential failure than just the ramped loading currently used. The period will be taken using engine rpm/4 since that is the time that the intake valve is open. An arbitrary rpm value will be picked for now (around max target rpm), however after random vibration tests are conducted and the intake's resonant frequency is found, an rpm that matches this frequency will be tested to see if this worst-case loading scenario will put the intake over its structural limits.

Intake Updates:

Updates were made to the lower side of the intake adding edge bending to hopefully decrease stress in this location. For right now the cap thickness will remain unchanged. The ring was also updated with edge bends and correct-sized holes on the right side tab pieces. The plenum was increased in size by widening the final cross-section located at the plenum runner met point). Size increase can happen here with no issues with clearances. Since the plenum is a simple shape, more cross-sections can be added if the volume needs to increase more (will come from Wave tests next week), like what was used on the experimental larger plenum intake. Ribbing could be added between the plenum and the runner flange if tests show that it is necessary, however, it would have to be made sure that the fuel rail and useless engine tab could fit with these installed. The clearance in here along with the edge bend radius is set so that bolts can be accessed in this location, ribs would only make this issue worse, but they can be added if need be. Additionally, the flange is in the place that it is so that as little runner as possible needs t be on the plenum 3d print if it is decided to print the runner separately, along with its ease of use by other subteams at its current location.

Note: The start of the plenum is made to mimic the shape and angle of the diffuser to further help pressure recovery (which has the action of making the diffuser longer). This effect can be also used more adaquatly if space constraints mandate a shorter diffuser

Before and after re-tooling start of the intake plenum


Other updates were made using the new ideas through up during the 3rd intake's design. The one major one was changing the start of the plenum. Before, the cross-section for the sweep at the start of the intake was the same size as the inner diameter of the plenum at this location. This was changed to be slightly wider to avoid the effect of the cross-section angles not matching up (see image to the left above). The discontinuities that this caused also allowed for edge bends to be added here to have much better control on the shape at this location. Conic edge bends can also be used to change the radius at locations with plenum shape causing adverse pressure gradient, to allow the wall angle to not increase as rapidly, helping the airflow not separate immediately. A chamfer was also added in the flange location so that the diffuser exit angle could be matched perfectly. This is better seen without edge bends since the way they are in CAD rn almost covers this up. The runner's inner and outer diameter was also changed to match up with the 450f gasket and intake ports, which are wider than the R6's. The weird shape at the end of the runner which was in place for the r6 intake gasket assembly is also gone and replaced by a simple circle.

RICARDO WAVE TESTS:

Setup and Background:

Until now, only ballpark intake sizing had been done. Different intakes had been optimized for certain configurations, but nothing size-wise was set in stone and only intake runners had been slightly tuned. Now using Ricardo, that will change. Sweeps will be done for plenum size and runner length at different fixed RPMs to see which yields the highest volumetric efficiency. The max and min plenum volumes and runner lengths for each intake choice will also be found using CAD data, which will be the basis for the sweep vectors in Ricardo. After this was done, the overhead intake was found to have a runner length of 5-9 inches and a plenum volume of 150 in3 to 215 in3. The newer, larger, and less developed intake was found to have a min runner length of 3.8 inches with a max of 5 inches. Its plenum sizes could be 180 to in3 250 in3. Although a smaller plenum size could facilitate longer intake runners and the plenum size could be pushed to 270 in3 (around 10x displacement). Choice of rpm to tune for will be determined mainly by dyno testing and plugging in tuning and Ricardo intake results into the lap time simulator. Online research has shown that tuning the intake to a higher rpm (away from the low peak torque of ~6000-7000 rpm) would result in a flatter torque curve with the intake supplementing the higher rpm range. This remains up to discussion. The goal will be to have a flowchart pointing to what intake to choose based on rpm choice. The results of the tests are shown below. After these tests are conducted the optimal sizes for each intake will be plugged into CAD and the models will be exported and ran in Ricardo to give more CFD level results for the intake to make sure those correspond to the initial results below. Data collection of other teams from competition will also be used. Note: exhaust runner length being held constant for these tests. Sweeping this as well can be done once harmonic and packaging information is better known and once intake size ranges are narrowed.

The best case could be any of the peaks but it looks like the highest overall is case 98 (8 inches 3.38 L), case 95 (8 inches 3.05 liters), or case 117-120 (9 inches 3.16-3.5 liters). The lower end for runner lengths goes along with the Helmholtz runner length theory as runner length decreases with increasing rpm. Like before the overall case vs VE plot has become flatter, however, unlike before VE stayed about the same, and delivered efficiency increased slightly. As the past three results have shown, VE prefers the higher case number range which correlates to longer runner lengths. 9000 rpm will be tested to see if this keeps dragging down due to shorter runner length being needed. Additional tests will be down narrowing in plenum and runner size ranges since these ranges were fairly large once final tests have been done. 

9000 RPM

winning case: case 45 (5.5 inches, 2.94 liters), case 75 (7 inches, 2.94 liters)

This case was interesting as the highest rpm had an ideal plenum size smaller than that of the ideal case at 7500 rpm. Plenum sized stayed constant for both cases and runner length dropped dramatically compared to 8500 rpm. RPM was similar to that which was calculated using the Helmholtz equations, which was 6.3 inches. Volumetric efficiency and delivered efficiency dropped around 0.01 compared to 8500 rpm. This was not due to the plenum size being too small because of what was described above. There is an interesting dead zone between case 80 and case 105, which corresponds to runner lengths 7.5 - 8.5 inches, which was not seen with other cases. If choosing 9000 rpm this case could be filled easily by the overhead intake as the sizes are well within its parameters. 

Conclusions:

Looking at pure volumetric efficiency, 8000 rpm has the best results with a peak VE of 0.912. In terms of plenum volumetric efficiency 8500 rpm is the way to go with a peak VE of nearly 0.96. Since this is so much higher than standard VE, additional tests will be run with a finer tuned ranger of intake runners. The test will also be run on cases showing an ideal plenum size of 3.5 L, which was the maximum entered to see if VE increases still with larger plenum volumes. This will be done on 7500 rpm - 8500 rpm cases with increased plenum size sweep to include up to 10x displacement. 2.94 liters and 3.5 liters showed up the most for plenum sizing. 3.5 is the largest size that was entered thus showing that a larger plenum is generally better for a wider range of RPMs. Further testing will be done testing only larger plenum sizes. Runner length does not correspond terribly well with the theory used for excel calculations using the WAVE model, but a decreasing runner size with increasing rpm correlation was found. The intake design choice should correspond to the one that gives the most flexibility for potentially changing tuning RPMs. I.e. if we choose 7000 rpm, the intake package should be able to support 9000 rpm to allow for increased modularity. This larger than expected runner length and plenum means the first intake iteration comes back into play and will be updated using the latest CAD techniques developed over the months. Plenum size will need to decrease slightly to help with packaging and install but this gives us the best options for design. This will need to be confirmed with dyno testing and running tests with exhaust lengths changing as well to make sure chosen value for all (17 inches) didn't bias one configuration. Since runner length was the deciding factor for intake choice and since the WAVE model does not exactly follow the Helmholtz calculations, physical dyno testing will need to be done in order for me to feel comfortable with the choice we make. The overhead model will be updated maximizing plenum size. 3rd design will be modified increasing runner length and trying to keep plenum size, although this will be done after the overhead design and only if necessary since the overhead is much more tested and does not have interference issues with other subteams like with cooling lines to the engine and with fuel line packaging.

ANSYS tests on overhead intake:

After much delay, the overhead intake (the second design) was run through Ansys. Meshing was very similar to before, but care was taken in making sure that the thin tabs had multiple elements along with their thickness so that results were not misleading. The small wall thickness in the main plenum, 0.1 inches compared to 0.22 for pf20, meant that the mesh along this area could not have mesh sizes too large. For right now a sweep element size of 0.015 was used. The next run, which will include the updated intake model coming from these results, will use shell meshing, which should help in this area with accuracy and computation time. Mesh quality and jacobian ratio looked very good, with problem areas either caused by hex dominant mesh (tabs) or localized on areas not important to structural results (diffuser and t.b. only present to accurately replicate real-life weight distribution and are especially dummy solids). Averages for each are shown below with j.r. being well above the set average goal of 0.66. One other issue with the setup was the location of the XYZ vectors, not lining up with the principle directions of the intake in the car (front, rear, left, right, etc.). This posed a problem with defining inertial loads corresponding to acceleration, braking, cornering, and shock loads. However, since these values (see confluence page on designed maximum values) are fairly close together and the vectors were not off by too far, they were just left as is and each acceleration value was assigned to the closest one. This will obviously be fixed for final structural tests.

Results looked very good, especially in the region of the tabs. The tab ring behaved very well, and tab layout led to considerably less stress concentrated on the bolt holes than before. There was a presence of stress concentrated on the end bulkhead of the plenum, however, this could have been partially due to the lack of edge bends on the interior of the plenum in this location. The way this was made in cad was through a rudimentary extrusion and unite command, rather than capping the ends off using the shell command. This jank way of doing it could have not been the best structurally and was replaced with the aforementioned capping method. The wall thickness here may also be increased if this problem still persists. Another issue around here was that the small flange on the runner was modeled as a solid element as opposed to two parts. This may end up being the case depending on how material selection goes since the runner is a problem area for potential failure (will be investigated through dyno testing and ANSYS random vibration testing), however, the runner still may be printed using aluminum SLS. Either way, the geometry will stay the same since the flange doubles as support (due to its orientation along the runner's cross-section) and as a way to mount tabs and fuel accessories.

Updated Spherical Intake:

Concept Drawings and Design Review:

 Over the summer, an intake similar to the second overhead intake was studied, with a simplified shape. The plenum would be derived from a sphere, not a spline, which could be freely moved around to an optimal location in the car. The spherical shape would allow for the Coanda effect to take place, making the air spin around on the inside of the plenum, effectively making the diffuser and plenum longer from the air’s perspective. The design process was super straightforward. A cutout was added to allow for a smooth flow transition between the diffuser and the plenum. The runners had to snake around in order to meet the plenum on an axis to match the diffuser so the air would have the most straight shot in making it into the runners. Snaking the runners also allowed for good clearance from the coolant outflow, and oil catch can tubes, which was a problem on the last intake design. Finally, it allowed for a flange to be added on the runners, increasing strength in the weakest part of the intake, allowing for a different printing material on the section of the runner that saw the most inertial loading, vibration, and heat. It also allows for easy replacement of the runner if it breaks as opposed to the entire lower section of the plenum costing past 1000 dollars. An attachment scheme was not added initially to the design, but it was planned to be much similar to the previous intake made. 

Several new design practices were added during this intake’s development, these included an updated bell mouth and a new flange location. The flange was proposed to be moved/oriented such that it offered the most clearance to the vehicle. This had been done before, however, it was extended so that the flange would be at a different angle in the car 45 degrees in between the side and front/back of the car. Again this could be done due to the simple shape of the intake unlike the more complicated ones before. Finally and most crucially, the bell mouth was changed to be fully elliptically shaped, as opposed to just having an arbitrary curve to it. It was sized according to Gordon P Blair with curve radii and opening diameters set based on the runner diameter. This produced a much larger and more tuned bell mouth that will hopefully have a positive impact on VE and discharge coefficients. Again it will decrease the number of things on the intake that is chosen arbitrarily, which is one of the goals of this redesign.

Reaction to this intake was lukewarm, to say the least, mainly because of how simple it was and how so many teams go this route. Another bad thing was how it threw smooth airflow out of the equation being just a simple cylinder. The airflow would separate and the only effect it would have to improve engine performance was its resonance and wave echo chamber effects, which is something that does not carry much tangible research behind it. Because of this, it was decided to go back to the previous intake and heavily modify it to mimic the upsides of this design, while including better steady-state airflow characteristics. Nonetheless, this design, the smaller overhead design, and the large Loch Ness monster-shaped intake all will undergo CFD steady-state analysis to study airflow behavior and mass flow rates. This will be fed back into each design and modifications will be made to each to allow for better steady-state behavior. All solver inputs will be the same as the 2018 intake report to keep variables to a minimum. Results are shown below.

Location Changes:

Based on feedback, this side intake was heavily modified. To start, the updated bell mouth was added to the design from the last intake. Next, the Plenum runner interface was updated to be less tight. The radius on the last plenum cross-section was reduced to prevent one side of it become too warped. The way this intake changes shape and location is through moving the diffuser/filter/throttle body to the desired location. Next, a coordinate system is updated which brings the plenum to the updated location. The diffuser was moved more forward (looking down in view) to bring the intake away from the engine, increasing clearance, from the oil catch can and coolant outlet port. This decreases heating on the intake, which reduces the strength of the design. It also decreases the intake being cluttered where it turns near the engine, helping with the fit and finish of the cad features, having more room for install and ribbing, and making the runner not bend at as extreme of an angle. Looking from the side view, the intake was tilted downward a few degrees, reducing its overall height (helping with the rear wing) and allowing more plenum space.

Finally, from a front view, the intake diffuser was tilted away from vertical, which has the effect of further reducing its interference with the rear wing and helping with airflow (mitigating flow separation). Initially, the intake airflow turned almost 90 degrees when going into the plenum. This is shown in the image below. Not only did this cause a point where there was flow separation into the plenum, negating the effect of the diffuser but it also made the height of the intake almost past the height of the rear wing airfoil. Since one of the requests of the aero team was to move the intake (for the R6 it was above the wing) below for better performance, the intake plenum size (already over 10x displacement), was reduced and the diffuser was tilted down and into the car. The one thing that had to be noted was staying in the clearance zone. To help this a sketch was made and a clearance factor from this sketch was defined to have a cushion. Thus, all adjustments were made to match this metric. According to the aero team, it was better to have the intake stick out more than up, however, driver clearance (during egress) was still something to look at as a constraint. In the end, the intake ended in a position where the aero team was happy and we weren’t facing any issues with packaging intake plenum configurations. Plenum volume right now will be 270-290 in3 or 10x displacement. I do not want this to change unless we either a) get Ricardo transient working or b) get good dyno data (the first thing tested will be plenum). Going the maximum allows for all possible intake configurations to be accounted for. If the plenum size does need to be reduced the runner interface spline will get smaller to again help with packaging in that area and to give the flow a better chance of getting to the runner. This should help with the discharge coefficient as well but CFD will be needed to validate this as well. 



Diffuser:

The diffuser was substantially overhauled as well. To start off, it had to be made compatible with the new throttle body. The exit diameter of the TB was much less than the previous throttle body so the overall height was reduced. For now the same converging and diverging angles are staying the same. After more important tests are run the diffuser/TB/air filter will be run through CFD to validate flow and pressure drops. The design can be further iterated using this method. One of the main issues with CFD discussed above is that the intake is not symmetrical about a plane, meaning that doing a center flow analysis is more difficult than before. Simulating the top end of the intake will mean this will not be the case. The flange for the TB was redone as well since the new tb only has two bolts. Since it was much smaller, the ribbing was eliminated from the design giving it a much more simple appearance. 

For the bottom diverging section the profile was redone to be more elliptical shaped. The tangent function was used to make the exit geometry match that of the start of the plenum, helping with the flow by a ton since the plenum sweep path is complicated. The center part stayed at the 6-degree diverging angle while the first section after the diffuser was made started out cylindrical and then curve to meet this 6-degree angle. For now, the restrictor will stay the same as what we have for manufacturing simplicity. In terms of manufacturing if the 3dpl at Purdue has working SLA printers we will print the diffuser there. There were initial issues with height on the past designs however since the diffuser is shorter this should not be an issue.

Chassis Tabs:

The chassis tabs were also redone. The design pioneered the overhead intake with several modifications. Since the diffuser flange was not centered between the roll hoop and the rear chassis tabs the design was not as eloquent as before. The design still retains the ring however the tab count has been reduced from 3 to 2 with each being parallel to each other on each side. Additionally, two tabs were mounted off the center flange on the plenum. This design was reviewed with the chief engineer to make sure they were constraining all degrees of freedom. Another major change is the elimination of the center thin tabs and their replacement with aluminum turnbuckle rods + rod ends. This will allow for much more strength and less bending at the cost of a slight increase in weight. The rod ends will also have ball bearings on them, allowing for a little bit of movement and shock absorption if the car goes over a bump. The rules need to be checked to see if this counts as not a direct connection from intake to chassis. One tab on the throttle body and on the diffuser will keep the same design due to their small distance to the chassis, however, all long tabs will be converted to the rod end design. 

Another additional will be a locator scheme to the engine to help in loading and passing rules stating we need a hard connection to the engine. On the R6 since there were 4 runners, attaching it to the engine constrained all movement and locked everything in place. However, with only one cylinder the intake can still freely rotate during install, so to make sure it’s being installed in the right place in the car and within all clearance zones, a tab will be designed to attach to a point on the engine, which is considered the datum/origin of the car. The point where the tab attaches to the intake will be depressed so that the tab can only exist at one angle. Therefore, there would be only one place where it could like up with the engine. This could only be used for loading or it could be used as a dummy rule passing tab as well.

Gasket design is also being looked at. Before we used a single o ring which never worked for sealing the intake, thus electrical tape was always used to make sure there was a seal between everything. However, there exists SLA resin’s that have properties really similar to rubber. Thus we could CAD up a really thin gasket sharing the same geometry as the flange to have in between the intake section. Additionally, the Parker O-Ring guide stated to use the sealing paste to increase the sealing results, which can be added to both sides of the gasket. Manufacturing should be super cheap and easy to implement. The curvy nature of the plenum will also mean that the flat part of the flanges will never be flat to the printing layers, which will require sanding.

Whiffling is still being looked at to boost performance. There is still no validation that this will work, but if there is time CFD testing will be done (after torque curves and FEA) with whiffling on the bottom part of the diffuser extending out to the point of maximum adverse curvature on the plenum. Previous CFD has shown flow separation in this region with airflow being better on the favorable pressure gradient side of the plenum (we are looking at moving the runner exit slightly off-center for this reason to get more direct airflow). Whiffling may help this negative side effect of the intake. Another idea is diverting flow right after the diffuser down here to create a blown effect. Channels could be built into the diffuser to direct flow, with the inlets coming just after the restrictor before a boundary layer has time to flow. The port was also contoured to minimize any vena contract effects as pictured.

As discussed above the bellmouth port was sized according to the Gordon Blair report (images shown above). The end result is a bellmouth that is much larger than before with the bellmouth being sized according to the diameter of the runner. There has been some discussion on effective plenum volume, trying to determine the optimal height of the bellmouth above the base of the plenum and if the plenum space behind the runner is usable. The Gordon Blair report stated the bellmouth length must equal the exit diameter (3.6568 inches in this case) with exit diameter being 2.13*runner diameter. Forum posts state that runner height has little effect on Helmholtz resonance as long as there is enough room for air to slow down but has a large effect on intake ram theory. 

In terms of plenum volume, the same forum included a chart showing the effect of plenum volume on torque shown below. In addition, a Matlab script will be written that calculates runner length based on Helmholtz theory (using the engine as the air tank) and ram charging theory. The strength of the plenum was also increased as well. After tests showed vibration along with condensation from pressure drops in the plenum, the wall thickness was increased from 0.125 inches to 0.175 inches. This is probably not necessary but doesn't hurt until we get actual physical testing data. The ribs on the plenum/runner transition section were also improved from before. CFD tests on a previous iteration of this intake showed that this point was the weak area of the design. The number of patterned ribs was decreased to allow for easier placement of the fuel injector. Tree-like supports were then added to the top part to give additional strength, without getting in the way of the intake runner. SpaceX does something similar to this on their DMLS printed Super Draco thruster.

Another strengthening measure taken was the doubling of the number of fasteners attaching the two plenums pieces together. This will allow for less preloading per fastener, which is important due to the brittleness of the SLA material used. The varying pressure loads the plenum will see also make it critical that the sealing is set for performance and the preload isn't at a value where it will yield the material at a certain pressure value. It should also help with leak prevention along with the updated o-ring grooves which are sized for a 0.07-inch diameter o-ring. O-ring cord can be used and then sealed together to make the custom shape needed for the plenum. This same approach will be used for the plenum/diffuser and the diffuser/tb interface.

Several changes were finally made to the intake runner. The first is that its base length was increased slightly in order to match the 2021 engine vs. the 2019 engine. The second was the sealing surface to the rubber engine gasket. The old o-ring design was eliminated being replaced by a contoured revolve that matched the rubber gasket sidewalls, allowing for a more rigid connection and better sealing.

The fuel injector was moved 180 degrees to allow for a better spray pattern into the engine valves. This was something discovered during dyno-tested where the current runner position was causing idling issues. The updated design uses a 45 degree mount epoxied into housing (shown below). The injector is placed much closer to the airflow than before eliminating the contoured injector section seen on the R6 intake. The placement is partially due to the large spray pattern angle of the selected injector whereas the old design had a much smaller angle, meaning the current design has a tendency to hit the cylinder walls and lose atomization effects. The 180-degree location change also allows the fuel flow to be better mixed with the air flow, which also pushes it more towards the valves than before.

One last run of analysis was done to determine at what backfire pressure would the plenum fail. Backfire are common for single cylinders and with this being our first year using the engine, we want to make sure a backfire does not structurally compromise the engine. Backfire occurs when fuel combusts in the intake runner/manifold. Finding results for in-cylinder show a max pressure of 60 bar. Using first-principles (ideal gas law, adiabatic expansion) assuming the plenum volume is 10x displacement, the pressure for a full load of fuel igniting would be roughly 6 bar. To test this a ramped pressure load of 30 bar was applied. Mesh size, displacement, and stress are shown below. The stress plot is scaled to where anything over red is yield strength. For displacement elongation at break was used. Any displacement greater than 5 percent of the original size would cause failure. For the plenum (R = 3.61 inches) this would correspond to 0.181 inches or 0.004 m. For the runner (R = 0.87) this value is 0.04 in or 0.0011 m. Note that the 10 percent surface thickness rule was not done for these (allowable strength thru 10 percent of surface thickness corresponds to failure).
