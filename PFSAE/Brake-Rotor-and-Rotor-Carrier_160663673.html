Purdue Formula SAE : Brake Rotor and Rotor Carrier

Created by Unknown User (xia107), last modified by Kurisko, Keith Gregory on Jan 05, 2021

Design Constraints/ Requirements

Separate Rotor and Carrier Design, Fixed with Bobbins Overview

  • Aluminum carrier, steel rotor
  • Rotors convert kinetic energy to thermal during braking events
  • Bobbins allow for rotor and carrier to expand at different rates, helping manage warping characteristics
  • Carriers carry the whole thing

Constraints

  • Must fit the designated calipers: (Willwood GP200 front and AP Racing CP 4226 rear)
  • Must use the stud pattern on wheel hub
  • Rotor and carrier assembly must fit properly with calipers to maximize swept area

Design Requirements

  • Braking deceleration of -2.81g from 70 mph
    • Make sure to take into consideration drag and possibly tire friction
  • Safety critical component
    • Safety factor of 1.4

Loads: Refer to

  • Coefficient of thermal expansion
  • Compressive Stress

Rotor  cutouts (slots, drilled, combination of those) impact grip and cooling

Good basic overview of drilled vs slotted: https://www.brembo.com/en/company/news/disc-drilled-or-slotted

  • Drilled
    • Provide better heat dissipation from the rotors
    • Remove layer of gas that causes fade at high temperatures
    • Prone to cracking depending on metal type and use case
  • Slotted
    • Far greater "mechanical resistance" during braking
    • Far greater resistance to cracks 
    • Used far more often for race applications
    • Does not affect heat dissipation at all

From: https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/119947/1080312700-MIT.pdf?sequence=1&isAllowed=y

  • MIT team used 17-PH stainless steal carriers and 1430 steel rotors
Brake Rotor Style (all are floating disc)Thermal ResistanceVerdict

"Holes and slots"

  • Highest thermal resistance of the three
  • Reaches higher steady state temperature
  • slots are NOT very effective for heat dissipation

Hole radius = thickness of plate

  • Thermal performance is similar to largest cutouts
  • Similar performance to large cutouts

Largest cutouts possible (minimize mass)

  • Best thermal performance of the three options
  • Lowest thermal resistance
  • Used on MIT 18' car

(it's hard to discern between the different styles, but from the article: "The model affirms that the "Lite" rotor will result in a lower steady state temperature compared to
the other rotor concepts. The model also reveals that the "Lite" rotor, with its reduced mass, will get to the steady state temperature in a shorter amount of time".)


From: https://ideaexchange.uakron.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?referer=https://www.google.com/&httpsredir=1&article=1149&context=honors_research_projects

  • University of Arkon
    • Ranked order of importance: Thermal conductivity, heat capacity, then capacity*density
    • Drilling through rotor is optimal to reduce fade caused by thin layer of gas created between rotor and brake pad
    • Rotor design needs to wear pad uniformly
    • Hub and pin design is superior:
      • Less rotor and hub material required
      • Stress of braking is spread amongst rotor, pin, and hub assembly

VERDICT

  • Most teams employ drilled rotors at a minimum, and rarely use a combination of drilled and slotted
  • Drilled rotors are most effective for the sae car, as the highest priority issue is cooling, which drilling is best for
  • For reduction of mass, simulations need to be run based on rotor and hub size to best determine how to model rotor
