  • Exercise 
    • The most effective way to get comfortable with CFD is to start running your own CFD, without any automation assistance, and without a pre-existing simulation environment to throw things into. Both the tutorials above were for 3D geometry, with access to reasonably high compute. You're going to be running a 2D simulation for a single aerofoil with a mesh density low enough to run on a laptop. The idea is to generate a lift polar (lift coefficient vs angle of attack in this case) that can be compared against experimental data (which is why we're going to use the NACA 2412 airfoil). 
    • Requirements (some of these exist to guide you in the right direction)
      • Use the NACA 2412 airfoil at 0,3,7,12 degrees of angle of attack. i.e you'll be running 4 simulations 
      • To make it a bit more interesting, all 4 simulations must use the same exact mesh. You cannot rotate the airfoil. Need to find a different way to induce an angle of attack. 
      • Physics 
        • Steady state 
        • 2D
        • Turbulent
          • Justify your own selection of turbulence model
      • Reynolds number must be 3.1e6 for all 4 cases. 
      • Find a way to change the angle of attack through physics/boundary conditions, rather than through geometry. 
    • Mesh
      • Must use a prism layer mesh with reasonable wall y+ values
      • Cannot have a prism layer around the domain walls.
      • Performance mesh independence study to ensure your result doesn't vary 
    • Reports
      • Cl and Cd reports
      • Surface wall y+ distribution plot
    • Visualization
      • Must have a velocity scene with LIC
      • Must have a pressure coefficient scene
        • Pressure coefficient must be correctly normalized
      • Both scenes must have sensible scaling. 
    • Notes 
      • Cell count can be found under representations > volume mesh on the tree
      • Prism layer is an option when you create your automated mesh, it needs to be manually configured. 
      • Wing and reports must be set up in an ‘airplane’ configuration. You want to be making positive lift. Not negative lift. 
    • If you actually get through this, hit all the requirements on and off the list for accurate CFD simulations, and are able to justify your decision, you'll be in an incredibly good place to make the jump to running and setting up your own full vehicle 3D simulations.
