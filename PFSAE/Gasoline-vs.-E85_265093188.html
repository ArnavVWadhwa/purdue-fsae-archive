PROS
  • High octane
    • Resistant to knock
    •  More stable overall
  • Increased torque and horsepower 
    • ~ 14-20% increase [1]
  • Cleaner emissions
CONS
  • reduced energy content
    •  15%-27% fewer miles per gallon [2]
    • More fuel required, thus adding weight for more fuel/larger fuel tank
    • Correction factor of 1.4 applied during comp
      • (Volume E85 consumed) / 1.4 
  • Cold start issues
    • Higher heat of vaporization (HOV) - 2.5X than gasoline
    • harder to start engine cold
  • Highly corrosive
    •  Has corrosive impact on the fuel pump internals, rubber fuel lines, aluminum fuel rails, and fuel injector internals
    • Impacts piston rings, thus increasing engine wear
    • Ethanol resistant components required
  • Seasonal inconsistencies and storage issues
    • E85 becomes E70 in winter, to aid in cold starting
    • Storage can become an issues, since certain algae can grow on alcohol and clog filters


"The result of increased heat of vaporization and increased octane ratings that, while there is 1.4 times less energy per volume in E85, an engine set up specifically for this fuel will be able to extract more useful work, to the point where the amount of fuel used would only be about 1.3times as much throughout an endurance compared to gasoline. Depending on the competition, this can yield a points gain of between 10and 15points [7]."  


According to: https://eflexfuel.com/us/blog/does-e85-eat-or-corrode-fuel-lines

  • The ethanol in E85 doesn't eat or damage fuel injectors. In fact, ethanol actually cleans fuel injectors.
  • Ethanol doesn't corrode aluminum. It doesn't corrode anything unless the fuel is water logged. That shouldn't ever happen in a normal vehicle.
  • Ethanol doesn't damage catalytic converters. If anything, ethanol will help keep your catalytic converters clean.
  • Ethanol doesn't eat plastic, at least not any of the plastics found in a fuel system.
  • Ethanol doesn't eat rubber. So the seals and rubber hoses within your fuel system will remain safe.

According to: https://www.in.gov/idem/tanks/files/tech_guidance_ethanol_compatibility.pdf

  • Ethanol is not compatible with soft metals such as zinc, brass, copper, lead, and aluminum. These metals will degrade or corrode in contact with ethanol and possibly contaminate a vehicle's fuel system.
