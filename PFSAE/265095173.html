Purdue Formula SAE : Hub PF24 | Design

Created by Stallings, Simon Tibor, last modified on Aug 14, 2023


What We Would Change

  • Look into bearing properties and different types of bearings to eliminate hub compliance.
    • Could be caused by the hub yielding
  • Tolerances within the floating brake disc design (if that is what we go with) so that they don't seize up again
    • Towards the end of the season, they would get tighter and tighter 
  • Look into making the entire hub lighter so that we can decrease the rotating mass of the car
  • Tolerances for the half shaft shelves (should not be able to hand press them)
    • Coming up with a new system with John is the only way to get this to be gone
    • We ended up using green Loctite 

What Was Done Well

  • I believe that the design for accepting the half shaft into the hub was adequate even tho the tolerance needs to be looked at
  • Mate surface for the wheel center was adequate
  • The attachment point of the hub to the upright (hub nut and threads)
  • Cutting threads for the hub nut. It is important to make sure that the threads are not cut too large so that the fit is sloppy.

What Would We Plan To Do Moving Forward 

Moving forward I would develop an entire list of design constraints that should be followed to cover all bases that the hub needs to meet. Once that is completed, I would begin my design by looking at the properties of different bearings. Even though the bearings are within the upright rather than the hub, I believe this will be a crucial point to both designs as it should help in eliminating the compliance issue that we faced with the prior year. From there I would then move into determining the necessary sizes for hub nuts, the circumference of the hub based on the bearing and tolerances for the floating disc design.

Questions to answer:

  • Why do we need a hub?
    • Hubs are necessary as they are the connecting point between the suspension setup and the wheels. 
  • What forces go through the hub?
    • Torque (rotational force) from the half shaft to the hub.
      • Produced by the engine.
    • Torque (rotational force) from the brakes to the hub when the car slows down.
    • Normal Force absorbed from the tire due to different moments creating suspension travel.
    • Lateral forces 
      • Cornering left and right and skidding
      • Force of friction created by the tire.
      • Coefficient of Friction.
  • What subsystems work through the hub?
    • The entire suspension system will work through the hub as other than the wheel center, the hub is the farthest outboard part that is manufactured by the team.
    • Drive Train, specifically the half shafts will also work through the rear hubs as the half shaft connects to the rear hubs.

Constraints

  • Thermals 
    • Provide adequate strength in the hottest thermal environment/try to reduce heat transfer 
      • How Hot does it Get?
        • Roughly (no recorded data) 250 F or 120 C.
      • What is the maximum heat that the material can take before yielding?
        • Quick Search
          • Aluminum tends to lose strength around 150 celcius and really show degrading around 200 celcius (https://firesciencereviews.springeropen.com/articles/10.1186/s40038-015-0007-5)
    • Thermal expansion has been considered and is not a problem with interfacing components. 
      • Hub and Upright interactions (bearing meets hub)
      • Floating Brake Disc Design 
        • How to make sure the brake discs stay floating and don't lose their intended play
  • Structures 
    • Mass as low as possible 
    • Margins as close to 0% for all load cases
      • Margins are the % stress you are away from your design load case
        • Ex: if we were to have a 15% margin, then a 15% increase in load (from what was originally designed) would not meet the design criteria.
    • Hub is able to survive the seasons (duh)
      • Hubs will not need to be replaced throughout the entire season 
      • Define what the cycle of the hub is.
        • The cycle is basically the expiration date for the hub. 
          • How much mileage can the hub take till it needs to be replaced?
        • In order to determine this we are going to want to run an FEA model putting the proper forces on the hub for x amount of cycles until it fails. We can then determine a miles-to-cycle ratio and then based on that determine the maximum mileage that we want our hub to see.
  • Component Interface
    • Provide as little play as possible between the hubs and bearings.
    • Proper mounting features for the wheel center
    • Inadequate part stiffness doesn't interfere with the kinematics of the suspension
      • we assume all components are rigid when doing kinematics.
    • Fit inside the wheel shell.
    • Must interface with the brakes and not hit the caliper while rotating. 
  • Manufacturing
    • Keeping in mind that some things are impossible or very difficult to perform on the machines available to us, therefore, using simplistic techniques to create the necessary product.
  • Serviceability
    • Hub is able to be removed from the bearings with ease.
    • Toll access has been considered for assembly and disassembly.

Criteria 

  • Keep tolerances to more manageable numbers so that it is easier to manufacture.
    • This is a common practice in all designs as we want to make the product as simple as possible rather than needing ridiculous tolerances to function correctly.
