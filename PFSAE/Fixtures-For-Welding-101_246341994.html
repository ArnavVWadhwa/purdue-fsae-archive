Purdue Formula SAE : Fixtures For Welding 101

Fixture Material Selection

Fixtures should generally be made of the thickest steel available. This is usually an old frame jig plate. Steel has a lower coefficient of thermal expansion than aluminum (4130's CTE is 1.23E-5 C-1, 6061's CTE is 2.36E-5 C-1) so it expands less for a given temperature increase. Steel also resists temperature increases better than aluminum due to a lower thermal conductivity (Steel k=40-60 W/mK, Aluminum k=170-240 W/mK). This is the reason that aluminum heats up quickly when it's welded. This is also the reason that a fixture made from aluminum is extremely prone to warping.

Design for Manufacturing / Assembly

This is important when designing every part but cannot be overstated here. It is your responsibility to familiarize yourself with your method of fabrication. Refer this page to get an idea of how welding works: TIG Welding Training Resources. With a basic idea of how welding works, below are some minor recommendations for designing welded parts (weldments).

  • Use locating features as shown in the image below to improve the fit up / assembly process. This removes any guesswork from the assembly process. Essentially, you want your parts to fit together like a puzzle.
  • Oversize the hole by ~0.005-0.020" depending on the sheet thickness.
  • Add a chamfer or edge blend for any male part when it needs to interface with a hole, as shown below. This will make assembly of the fixture far easier.

Welding Torch / Filler Rod Access

This goes hand in hand with thinking through the complete fabrication process of your part. It's a good practice to weld as much of a part while it's constrained in a fixture to mitigate any risk of warping. When possible, provide access cutouts for the filler and/or torch. In the example for the exhaust jig, notice that one of the most notable changes was the addition of two large cutaways from the first to the second revision. This allows your welder to complete many more welds while it's properly constrained. Another example is the bulkhead jig. Welding Procedure

Think through the welding procedure and design your fixture to meet your needs each step along the way. For example, with the exhaust jig, the welding procedure is as follows:

  1. Fit up the four primary tubes to the collector disc, tack each primary together.
  2. Weld as much of each primary as possible while constrained by the jig.
  3. Remove the primaries then complete any remaining welds.
  4. Re-fixture the primary tubes with the collector disc.
  5. Weld the primary tubes to the collector disc from the inside.
  6. Fixture, tack, and weld the collector and secondary tube.

This welding procedure should be determined from a conversation between the design engineer and the welder. The design engineer may envision a welding procedure that is unrealistic or impossible, so these conversations are great learning experiences. With a realistic welding procedure, it may be determined that intermediate fixtures are required. In the exhaust jig, the addition of a locating plate to a flange on the collector disc was necessary to ensure the primaries are in the correct positions for steps 1-4.

Tips While Welding The Fixture

Proper Fit Up

Fit up - the specific arrangement of two or more pieces of base metal in preparation for joining (welding) - is everything. It doesn't matter how well your CAD is done if your fit up has your fixture looking cross-eyed. Having extreme attention to detail in the design stage (by following the guidance laid out earlier in this page) will help avoid issues with this, but sometimes you will simply need to deal with warping or other defects. This is where the proverbial "jig brain" comes in handy. Use all available resources to bring your reality as close to CAD as possible. C clamps and spare jig stock become your best friend when you need to align something.

Mitigating Risk of Warping

Try to keep the fixture from heating up inconsistently to avoid warping. Take your time whenever you're welding. Warping is the enemy of a useful fixture and rushing through welding will leave you with one ineffective jig. Below are two effective methods to keep your fixture cool:

  • Placing aluminum blocks underneath the fixture to act as a heat sink. This works well but you should monitor the temperature of the blocks and run cool water over them if they get too warm.Â 
  • Forcing air over the fixture between welds. Pretty self-explanatory, just do not do this while welding because the airflow will disrupt the shielding gas.
