Tune To Win

Page 106:

  • “The big payoff of a well sorted out braking system comes, not from any increase in braking power itself, but in the confidence, consistency and controllability that it provides to the driver.”
  • Racing disc brake system has been developed to a very high level and there isn’t a lot of room for practical improvement
  • Energy required to decelerate is .0335 x [(mph max)2 - (mph min)2] x gross weight (lb) [Very Large]

Page 107:

  • “Need braking system capable of developing enough braking force to exceed the deceleration capacity of the tires”
  • Braking effort needs to be linearly proportional to pedal pressure from driver
  • Need to get balance of force between the front and rear brakes correct (bias bar)
  • Complete reliability

Brake Pedal:

  • Obviously needs to be well mounted to chassis
  • Most brake pedals have advantage of 3:1 - 8:1
  • Master cylinders must be well mounted

System Force Ratio

  • Mechanical advantage of the system is determined by the mechanical advantage of the brake pedal and the mean diameter of the discs.
  • Hydraulic force ratio is determined by the relative area of the master cylinder bore and the total piston area of the calipers
  • The softer the compound of the brake pad, the higher the friction and the less force required.  
  • Typically adjust master cylinder vs calipers due to cost
  • Since brake fluid is not compressible, the amount of hydraulic pressure developed by given pedal pressure is inversely proportional to bore of the master cylinder.  Thus, a large cylinder will require more foot pressure per unit of hydraulic pressure generated but will require less pedal travel to exert the same force.
  • Author leans towards firm brake pedal with little travel, using large master cylinders 

Front to Rear Braking Force Balance

  • If all 4 brakes were equal, during straight line braking rear would lock due to forward load transfer
  • Optimal arrangement is front tires lock just before rears under heavy straight line braking
  • Brake effort proportioning is determined by the ratio of the area of the front master cylinder bore to the total front caliper piston area compared to the same factors at the rear.

Bias Bar

  • Changes mechanical advantage of the bar and proportions more of the driver foot pressure to the cylinder closest to the pivot.

Brake Pads

  • 3 requirements, stop the car controllably without fade, last long enough and don’t chew up discs.
  • The coefficient of friction between the pad material and the disc is a function of operating temperature.  Normally it increases pretty steeply until the threshold of the design operating temp is reached.  Once it hits limiting temperature the pad fades.
  • More mass in the brake discs = more heat sink

Pad and Disc Modifications

  • All new pads arrive with a radial slot in the lining to give dust a place to go other than between the pad and the disc, this slot often fills up with fused lining dust.  If this happens make another slot at right angles to the stock slot.
  • Most discs come with some number of tangential slots milled into the operating surface which wipes the boundary layer of dust from the rotating disc as it comes into contact with the leading edge of the pad.
  • Drilled discs do the same as slotted discs except better and remove mass from the disc, decrease pad taper at the expensive of increased pad wear, also prone to cracking around the drilled holes.

Conclusion

  • “Once you have the braking system properly set up and sorted out, there really aren't that many meaningful improvements to be made, it's basically a question of getting everything right and keeping it that way. About all that you should have to change from track to track is a slight amount of brake bias and, maybe, the pad compound.”




FSAE Braking & Major Parts in Braking:

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hT4oMXaQzlE&ab_channel=DhyanPatel%40BITSPilani

Overview:

  • Brake pedal transfers force through braking fluid to stop car
  • Brake pedal utilizes lever to gain mechanical advantage, master cylinder applies pressure to brake fluid
  • Front brakes are disc brakes rear are drum brakes
  • Disc Brakes clamp on disc that spins with tire
  • Drum brakes have a pad inside that pushes up into the drum and stops the wheels

Pedal Assembly:

  • Bias Bar is able to have bias between amount of force sent to rear and front brakes (if car is back heavy, put more force on rear brakes)
  • The Master cylinder is a reservoir where the brake fluid is, when force is applied to the cylinder it travels through the brake lines to the brakes.
  • Need a spring or hydraulics as a return mechanism to return pedal to upright position when no pressure is applied

Rotor Assembly:

  • Rotor is the brake disc mounted on hub which rotates with the wheel.
  • Brake Caliper clamps down on the rotor

Drilled, Slotted & Vented Brake Rotors:

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=78wbht355R8&ab_channel=EngineeringExplained

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gZmZ3J1Zy-w&ab_channel=TheCarizon

Vented:

  • Increased heat rejection, angled vanes act like pump that evacuates heat from rotors

Drilled:

  • A bit obsolete due to improvements in pad materials, holes can lead to stress points and cracking, not ideal for racing

Slotted:

  • Same purpose as drilled but wipes pad clean and can provide additional biting edge, can increase pad wear.

Summary:

  • Professional Racing applications almost always use vented and sometimes also slotted but rarely ever drilled brake rotors.
  • Tires and Pads are much more important than the rotors when it comes to braking

MIT Design of a FSAE Braking System:

https://dspace.mit.edu/bitstream/handle/1721.1/119947/1080312700-MIT.pdf?sequence=1&isAllowed=y

How they picked Calipers:

  • Chose GP200 Wilwood calipers due to low cost, low weight
  • Tested maximum pressure they can produce and noted the maximum operating pressure from manufacturer

How they picked Brake Rotor Radius:

  • Maximizing rotor radius lowers required driver effort
  • Thus they picked largest possible size given the sizing of the Wilwood GP200

How they picked Rotor Material:

  • Primarily picked based on availability and trustworthy material property data (They used MMPDS-11 database)
  • Tested 3 candidate materials on dynamometer and looked at pad-rotor friction coeff v temperature for each
  • Want consistent coeff of friction through wide temperature range

How they picked Rotor Geometry

  • Need adequate cooling, minimize mass without compromising  cooling effectiveness or max braking torque, must provide means of allowing for radial and axial thermal expansion of the rotor without causing internal stresses
  • Used Floating rotor design (I didn’t know what this was so I looked it up):

Floating Rotor vs Solid Rotor: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VrUQr7BAyLA&ab_channel=RowanLewis

  • Floating Discs: More Heat Resistant, less likely to warp, less likely to crack
  • Rotor is secured to the Bell rather than being all one piece like a Solid Rotor
  • Tested 3 candidates for rotor geometries on dynamometer, but before that testing all 3 were refined using FEM simulation
  • Used a “Lumped Parameter Model” because the brake rotor is a “ thermal system with a Biot number much smaller than one” (no clue)
  • Chose drilled holes design based on testing

Race Car Vehicle Dynamics:

Components:

  • Flexible brake lines should be replaced with metal braided hose to minimize volume change
  • Brake discs rotors must be machine to run true on the wheel bearings (no clue what this means) Runout will knock the pads back and cause a low pedal

Ventilation:

  • Air must be ducted directly to the eye of the rotor and optionally air blast may be directed at the caliper if fluid boiling is a problem
  • Brake cooling air should be taken from a high-pressure location for ex. Chin spoiler

Brake Distribution:

  • Vehicle will not achieve the max braking decel unless all four tires are brought to the friction peak simultaneously.  Otherwise one end of the car will lock up first.
  • Brake balance is a function of the loads on the wheels which is in turn a function of the deceleration
  • Either fit a brake pressure regulator in line or use 2 master cylinders with bias bar

Brake Bias Research:

Forward Brake Bias Shift:

  • Larger Front Rotors
  • Larger Front Calipers
  • Higher friction front brake pads
  • Lower friction rear brake pads
  • Raised suspension, more weight transfer forward
  • More Sticky Tiers

Rearward Brake Bias Shift:

  • Opposites of above
  • More weight on rear axle


7/9:

Brake Bias Video: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6chttotuw6s&ab_channel=HighPerformanceAcademy

Basics:

  • Many people falsely think that master cylinder size is not important and that braking tolerances can all be fixed with bias bar
  • Having a bias bar that is biased significantly to one side leads to many other issues

Brake Pressure Inconsistency:

  • The balance bar can pitch when brake is pressed hard, altering the braking pressure by as much as 10%, this variation can lead to many problems like locking up brakes before they are supposed to

Choosing Master Cylinder Size:

  • Start from the tires and COG, figure out what force of braking you need at the front and rear for the maximum braking application
  • Design the whole system front and rear at the right proportion to suit what overall brake bias you want (ex. Same brakes front and rear makes no sense)
