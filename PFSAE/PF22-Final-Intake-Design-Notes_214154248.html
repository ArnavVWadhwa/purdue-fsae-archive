Purdue Formula SAE : PF22 Final Intake Design Notes

Created by Unknown User (kinsellj), last modified on Apr 21, 2022

Throttle Body:

The selected throttle body stayed the same although a custom air filter along with a a housing for a secondary spring.

The spring setup is combined with the chassis tab that attaches to the car. This was more difficult from a GDT/tolerance standpoint, but the overall look was much cleaner. The tab wrapped around and was attached using both bolts on the throttle body. This was to make sure it was locked down, especially given the loading that it would receive from the spring. The whole thing was secured with jet nuts for good secondary retention. Mockups were done with printing a 1:1 model on a piece of paper to get the design close and then 3d printed to double make sure things fit. It took three 3d print iterations to get the design down perfect. Also, holes for the grommet and the bolt size were sized up from tight screw clearance holes to give more slop in the design.

Overall tab setup with sized up holes

According to rules, there needs to be two springs on the throttle body pulling it closed, so that in case one breaks, it will still be able to spring back into the closed position. As it sits, there was just one torsion spring on the throttle shaft. While the easiest solution to the problem would be to add another concentric one, there was no good way to disassembly the throttle body without potentially damaging it. Thus, another solution was needed. The design that was decided upon was to bend part of the chassis tab up so that a extension spring could be looped around it as pictured. A small pin hole was drilled on the throttle cam so that the other end of the spring could be put on it. Note that the dimensions for the TB in cad are slightly off so the real thing is not as tall compared to it. The spring and the tab height was sized so that the spring was tangential to the cam through most of the process, to maximize the force applied. 

The initial design used a threaded shaft and rod ends instead of bending the metal. This was abandoned since the final design was much simpler. Also material properties for resisting bending improve dramatically with bending the metal up. Initial and final designs shown in images below.

Design iterations, and spring/closing force calculated

Final Design

The spring was selected so that the base length was slightly shorter than the geometric closed length so that there was preloading in the spring. This is so that it will always fully close and lock if the other spring fails. The max extension length was also slightly longer than the WOT length from hole to hole to reduce yielding/fatigue life. The spring constant was selected as the highest possible to have the force needed to close it. Maximum diameter that was allowed for the spring was a quarter inch without the spring rubbing against any part of the TB. Final mcmaster part file is shown below.

Air Filter:

A custom air filter was also 3d printed to use the tea filter air filters that were selected. The design uses the natural draft angle of the throttle body to stay on (locking it in place), along with screws to increase friction and clamping force. Several iterations were done just to get the geometry correct since everything was being referenced off of a CAD model from grab cad. Adjustments to the air filter cutout were done along with the surface toughing the throttle body. Around 1mm diametrically was added to this profile to accommodate for 3d print tolerances. The top of the design also wrapped slightly up on the filter to prevent it from moving. Finally, some geometry simplifications were made to make printing easier and so that less supports were needed. Plenum/Runner:

The plenum and runner was redesigned slightly. It was realized after ordering that the flange for the screws clipped a section of the chassis by roughly 0.2 inches. While the intake could be re-clocked and rotated around the rubber intake gasket, this would have made it so the fuel injector was not spraying straight onto the valves. Re-clocking also only gave minimal clearance, and the goal was to have at least 0.5 inches due to the seatbelt harness. Thus, a redesign was needed. Keeping the flange and moving the spine controlling the plenum over was considered, but this would have changed plenum size/geometry invalidating all tuning done to this point. So, the flange was eliminated with only a flange surface kept to attach the truss tab to.

Initial Design's Clearance Issues

Rapid fire FEA was done to make sure that this would not structurally cause any issues. Loading was 2022 design values for inertial loading found on confluence (plus a bit more), and full vacuum pressure in the plenum. Inertial forces for braking, a left turn and a vertical load of going over a bump were tested since these values were the highest on the confluence page. Results showed that this modification did not pose any issues, and are shown below. Two mesh elements per thickness was not ideal (3 is the best) but this was done to save time as design modifications were decided upon and out the door within a week for scheduling. 

FEA results

The modifications to this design were the elimination of the flange, and the redesign of the truss tab for the new screw holes. The fuel injector port was also adjusted since we changed from the old injector that had an off axis spray (see image below for example). Because of this, the injector cutout angle was decreased to angle the injector spray more towards the values. This was a balancing act since the plenum's position made it so decreasing angle too much would mean the injector would hit the plenum tree supports. This was done until the profile looked good enough. Besides this, the boss for the FPR pressure port was changed so that the diameter was smaller, so that the port would fit snugger.

 

CAD images and image showing off axis spray from old fuel injector

Final Intake as installed on car (note clearance from far left image)

Finally a jig was made for the metal fuel injector holder so that it could be clamped when the epoxy was curing. The first intake had issues where the holder became angled over to one side, which is not good for fuel injection onto the valves. There needs to be clearance between the holder and the hole for the holder to give the epoxy room to cure. A jig was 3d printed that clamped onto the inner runner surface. A screw was then passed from the holder down to this section and the bolt was tightened, holding it in place. CAD abd drawn images are shown below. This design proved to work pretty well.



For a spare intake, CraftCloud was used, which allows you to upload a file and specify materials, and finishes. It then contracts sub-companies to print it for a set price, similar to xometry but jankier. The material was glass-filled nylon, which has the durability of nylon with material properties above that of accura 60. We will be receiving the part raw and unsanded, so we will have to do the post-processing, painting, and sanding. The price was 400 dollars, much lower than what xometry or realize would quote so fingers crossed the quality will be decent.

During testing, there was a failure of the flange on the intake, where it cracked across 3/4 of the length with roughly a third separated, and the rest cracked/delaminated. It was looked at to use epoxy to cure the crack back together, but calculations showed that this would be 60 percent as strong as what it would be without a crack. Thus, this was rejected with this intake set to be covered in carbon fiber to increase strength and fix the crack. The cause for the failure was both over-torquing the bolt, and not having a set value for the torque of the bolts. This is critical since resin is super brittle, unlike aluminum or steel. Hand calculations should be done with the nut factor and clamping area (washer/bolt face) along with the compressive yield strength of the resin. Load value should be tight, but under the yield strength with the pressure loading being taken into consideration for the plenum/diffuser flanges. The other cause of the failure was due to fatigue. Analysis of the crack made it appear as fatigue had something to do with it, as the crack went up the flange through multiple bolts. This flange was the closest the intake was to the vibrating engine, especially at idle and during skidpad when the car has the tendency to buck up and down. While Accura 60 is strong, its hardness and tendency to be brittle make it not ideal in cases where the is continued vibration and loading. The R6 did not have this problem due to its engine running much smoother. It was decided to run late-night FEA using SLS nylon 12 instead of accura 60, which has much better durability properties with slightly less yield strengths and a slightly increased young's modulus. Material properties were taken from these websites:

1) https://www.cableorganizer.com/learning-center/articles/materials-nylon12.html

2) https://www.matweb.com/search/datasheet.aspx?matguid=0e37a459c4eb452faa9d92659f9a0ccc&ckck=1

FEA results are shown below. A mesh convergent study was done looking at mesh nodes vs. maximum principle stress. The first run had half of the nodes of this final one (not shown), but stress did not increase. Results are very good, with only a marginal drop in safety factors. Thus it was decided to order this from xometry with nylon. Results are shown below.


As a stopgap solution, the old intake was coated in carbon fiber with a wet layup, placed in a vacuum bag, and left to cure overnight. The final product looked less than ideal (since there's no good way to do this due to the intake's complex geometry) but was much stronger, especially in the broken region. The flange holes were found and redrilled carefully and the intake was attached to the car after a 4-day turnaround period.
