Suspension / Brakes Problems

Solution(s)

Front lower control arm failures

  • Increased bolt length = increased moment

  • Spherical bearings coming out of cup

  • Analyze safety factors on all bolts in system

  • Reduce bolt length

  • Confirm spherical bearing retention, add bearing capture feature (internal snap ring?) (not tack welds)

  • Confirm bearing cup orientation in CA manufacturing

Hub assembly issues

  • Press fit tolerances of studs into hub

  • Possible to put wheel on without engaging wheel studs

  • Brake rotor float

  • Very little clearance between hall sensor plate and brake components (in fact they interfere in CAD)

  • Wheel nut locking holes on hub is integer multiple of wheel nut, requiring specific position to insert ‘springy bois’

  • Ream all stud holes in hubs

  • If necessary, make unique studs to each hole that has correct press fit

  • Incorporate locating features into hub/wheel centers to prevent misalignment 

  • Machine features into hub that transfer torque, eliminate need for separate studs

  • Redesign trigger to not hit things: shorter and / or has outer ring. Possibly go to hub not mounted trigger wheel

Design Judge Feedback

From Gene Lukianov (former head vehicle dynamics guy at FCA. glukianov@vradengineering.com / 248-770-1995)

  • Make free body diagrams (FBDs) of tire, then bicycle model, then full vehicle to predict outcomes of simulations. Then you can trust your simulations.

    • Specifically, failed to understand why lateral migration of roll centers matters. This could be discovered with a sweet FBD

    • Had somewhat rough idea of why pro-ackermann is better despite not “creating optimal slip angle for lateral force”, but could also use some FBDing

  • Front ARB is cantilevered

  • Rear toe compliance

  • Do chassis stiffness test with front suspension, then rear suspension. Measure chassis stiffness and suspension stiffness independently to reduce tests required

  • Take measurements of kinematics values (this is probably one of the easier ways to pick up design points. This is about 6 hours of easyish measurements that doesn’t require a working car -BR):

    • Camber curves (easy)

    • Motion ratios (easy)

    • Bump steer (kinda hard, max bump steer should be about 0.1 degree. That’s beyond the measuring capabilities of the stick and ruler method i think)

    • Toe vs. steering (easy)

    • ARB stiffness (easy)



From Benjamin Pohl (Brembo)

  • Teams often just reuse the same brake system and fail to make sure anyone understands it. Need to just make sure you can walk through the calculations with a design judge. (This is probably the easiest 5 points in design we could pick up. Need to just write out all the equations in the spreadsheet from start to finish. Need to find good source for braking force target -BR)

  • Check your pressures against calculated pressures. (We have pressure transducers in both brake circuits. I did check pressures with last year’s car and they did not match. Need to find root cause. Last year’s events were on a wet track which could explain some of it. Gotta look at data from this year. -BR)

  • Should make all cockpit control forces similar. You shouldn’t have light brake, heavy accel, light clutch, etc. A driver gets used to some level of input and will try to apply it to all controls.

  • Run hardline everywhere you can run hardline. It has negligible expansion. Will reduce required travel, be cheaper, and maybe a bit lighter.

  • Next steps for going beyond the linear calcs required to just get a car to lock all four with acceptable pedal force:

    • Friction v. temperature data would be ideal, but requires a brake dyno if not provided by the pad manufacturer.

    • However, even without this data, there is generally information regarding what temperature a pad material wants to run at. Make a simple thermal model of brake system and tune for this via material selection and rotor mass.

    • Then validate model by doing repeated stops and measuring brake temperature.



Questions for Gene:

From Ben Reynolds:

  • Request general feedback based on presentation with notes returned...

  • Steering force:

    • How much of aligning moment comes from contact patch migration and how much comes from moment made directly by twisting about contact patch.

      • Could use this info to determine how much more steering force can be reduced by tweaking mechanical trail

    • Does scrub radius matter beyond potential for steering force due to mismatched grip during braking (should look into this before asking cause it might be a dumb question)

  • Springs and damping (was quite lucky that we didn’t go to far into this, cause my knowledge is limited)

    • Determining spring rates: on smooth road simulation, spring rates won’t matter so long as total transverse load transfer distribution (TTLTD) is unchanged. With aero you would want super hard springs to keep ride height and rake constant. But on a rough road, soft springs give better adhesion. How do you compromise here? Is the only answer to test? Can you set targets based on wheel hop, ride, pitch, and role natural frequencies?

      • Hitting all these frequencies and aero map stuff would dictate roll center absolute heights, anti-dive / anti-squat geometry, spring rates, and arb rates.

      • My guess is that you want to be as soft as you can be before absolutely destroying aero performance, but how do you quantify road bumpiness effects??

    • Determining damping baseline: so far have just gone off of rule of thumb for percent critical damping. This is ride damping only. Should also look at pitch and roll damping? Would adjustable RCs be useful to decouple transient handing characteristics from damping? Or is it fairly true that handling is from low speed damper settings and ride damping is high speed?

  • General breadth vs. depth trade off.

    • Major gripe from presentation was over-reliance on Adams Car. however, if i made manual calculations of events simulated, there is plenty of stuff I just wouldn’t get to. How do you strike this balance? What depth of hand calculations is necessary before trusting lateral steady state simulations, step stear simulations, combined lateral longitudinal simulations?

  • Lap time simulation:

    • I feel like this is the most important thing to chase down for next year. Need to provide points sensitivities for downforce, drag, weight, torque, differential settings (may be more a transient thing). Does adams car for tire-limited  gg-diagram generation and then Matlab quasi-steady-state sim with powertrain specs make sense?? 

    • For autocross/slalom, I think we really need to understand the transient response of the car more. It’s hard to simulate, but there are simple experiments we could run in the car to help validate a sorta-simple mass supported by 4 springs model that reacts to a steering input. Idk what I am saying tho




Projects that need owners:

  • Brakes deep dive: need someone to own the system. It is currently an acceptable minimum viable product (MVP), but not understood or optimized.

    • Understand and document current spreadsheet used for initial design

    • Take a look at other options for calipers / master cylinders. Either prove we made the right decision, or change to different system

    • Determine front and rear pad materials for minimal bias change as system heats during operation.

    • Make simple thermal model

      • Need to work out energy dissipation front vs. rear. Would be cool to integrate with lap time simulation and extra cool to validate on track.

      • Model would assume all braking energy is dumped into disc

      • Would need to figure out brake cooling rates likely as a function of vehicle velocity

    • Use thermal model to select pad material, disc material, and disc mass. So that system has constant bias as it heats up, and then operates in acceptable temperature range

    • Find sensible rationale for braking force target

    • Decide on need for adjustability. Justify decision. If non-adjustable, make system for quickly identifying dry vs. wet settings

    • Gather brake temp data on track - I think there is some extra heat being dumped into brakes due to rotor float issue

  • Structures calculation spreadsheets:

    • There are some things that we either don’t analyze or rely on FEA for that are actually fairly simple hand calculations. The two that come to mind are:

      • Lug calculator. This may already exist for free via the interwebs https://www.abbottaerospace.com/whats-new/lug-analysis-spreadsheets/. This will help us quickly analyze and document all suspension tabs. And this can hopefully be forced onto the other subteams that make some lugs

        • Calculations layed out in Nasa Astronautics Structures Manual 1: https://www.abbottaerospace.com/reference-data/nasa-astronautic-structures-manual-volumes-i-ii-iii/

      • Bolted joint calculator.

        • Nasa-STD-5020 and NASA-RP-1228 should have everything necessary. I think we can neglect temperature effects in our application. -BR

  • Adams Car part database work.

    • There are many standard bits that we could make in Adams Car that would make analysis go faster. This includes making all of our damper, spring, and arb adjustment settings. Kinda lame busy work, but could help someone start familiarizing with Adams Car.
