Purdue Formula SAE : PF24 Chassis Onboarding -- FEA Bulkhead Analysis

Created by Cole Henry Kniesly, last modified on Oct 06, 2023

Subteam Introduction: 

As a sub team, we are responsible for designing, building and testing the chassis, all cockpit controls, as well as other parts of the driver interface. As a designer you will be responsible for the research, design, manufacturing, and assembly of your assembly. 

Design Projects:

Chassis Design:

This project is mainly done by the team lead. It is a very involved project that is driven by the needs of every subteam, driver preferences, and rules. It is constantly iterating as design season progresses due to the requirements of each interfacing assembly starting to mature.

Shifter and Clutch Handle Design:

This project designs and specs out the driver interface with the shifter and the clutch, the interface with the engine, and all supporting mechanisms. A large part of this project is optimizing the air reservoir capacity and determining the dynamics of the system by coordinating with powertrain. 

Pedal Box Design:

This project is both mechanical design heavy and calculation heavy. Encompassed in this project is the design of the break and throttle pedals, the pedal mounting assemblies. Alongside the mechanical design, is the creation of all associated Excel/ Matlab Calculators to help define the system and to drive the geometry. 

Steering Assembly Design:

This project is mainly heavy on the mechanical design aspect but expect there to be some complex calculations for the design of the steering rack (We love designing gears). The three main parts of this project are the design of the steering wheel, the column and associated supports, and the steering rack and associated supports and hardware. 

Seat, Firewall Design, and Headrest:

These projects are smaller and less design and calculation heavy. The seat, being the most complex of them all may require a full study into driver ergonomics. if a new seat is going to be designed. The firewall and headrest are relatively straightforward, but are a great introduction into solid modeling, some composite analysis, and some mechanical design and analysis.

Project Introduction:

This introductory project will guide you through the design of a front crash structure that meets or exceeds the pre-defined goals/ design targets. Along with designing your first part, this project will continue to test and teach you the CAD skills required for this team through a complex design problem. This project will also provide you with opportunities to learn simulation techniques in ANSYS (the FEA software used by the team) as well as get familiar with the documentation and hand calculation tools provided. 

This project will be done by all new members to the chassis team alongside any current members who wish to participate (this is not required but highly encouraged).

As a general rule for this intro project, we don't expect you to have any knowledge of car design, CAD software (other than concepts covered in the introduction), or ANSYS. The goal of this project is to let you get comfortable with the software and design process used by the team through a challenging design problem. The purpose of this project is NOT to be frustrating or extremely difficult or time-consuming, because of this make sure you are asking as many questions as you need to (I would rather you ask than progress in the wrong direction and get frustrated and stop working on the project). See contact at the end of the page

Resources, including the drawings, Formula SAE rules, and tutorials for processes in this project are included throughout the page by their relevant steps. Please make use of these.

The project is expected to take around 10 hours to complete. Plan accordingly.

The Project: 

Through the course of this project, you will be designing the Front Bulkhead. The Front Bulkhead takes load from the impact attenuator, which is the "crumple zone" of our car and distributes the load to the chassis. This is why it is imperative that our Front Bulkhead meets the design targets defined in the Formula SAE Rule Book in case of a crash. This is where the FEA comes in handy. It allows us to see what happens to the Front Bulkhead during impact and to see if it meets our targets. For this project we will be guiding you through the design so if you follow the directions correctly, your design should meet all required specifications. b. Model Bulkhead

Use the following 2D drawings to model the bulkhead. 

If you need some help with the solid modeling, please refer to this video:

Steps: (you can follow these steps if you need hints on how to do this)

i. Create wire diagram of bulkhead (e.g.: sketch a square using dimensions from figure 4)

ii. Sketch cross section of single bulkhead tube (see figure 4). Hint: Center of sketch should be on the wire diagram (line created previously)

iii. Create diagonal (45deg) datum planes on each corner of the wire diagram

iv. Extude crossection sketch (on both sides) onto the diagonal datum planes (do a single bulkhead tube)

v. Make the two 0.5in holes

vi. Use mirror tool to add the remaining three bulkhead tubes (Remember to mirror all sketches and necessary parts!)

vii. Create a diagonal datum plane in the center of the bulkhead (this is to create the cross-tube from figure 5)

viii. Sketch figure 5 drawing on datum plane 

ix. Extrude until the edge of the bulkhead (you might need to trim the body if you decide to extrude by a distance instead)

x. On each corner of the bulkhead, sketch the bulkhead supports and extrude as a sheet

Figure 6: 2D drawing of cross-tube (+cross section of tube)

Figure 7: Bulkhead supports - These circles are on the bulkhead, opposite the anti intrusion plate. They represent where the bulkhead connects to the rest of the car. Create an extrusion of the supports. It doesn't matter how far you extrude, they will be removed later.


c. Model welded inserts

Model the welded inserts. Then, create a subassembly with 8 welded inserts and the bulkhead. Mate the welded inserts with the holes of the bulkhead

Figure 8: Welded inserts


d. Model AIP (Anti Intrusion Plate)

Use the following 2D drawing to model the AIP

Figure 9: 2D Drawing of AIP


The rectangle in the center represents the impact attenuator. It does not matter how far out you extrude this rectangle, as it is only relevant as a surface. For the purpose of this analysis, the input force works through the impact attenuator, and the effects of this force on the rest of the structure are analyzed.

Note: Use wavelink geometry to create the holes in the AIP from the bulkhead subassembly - see  Tool Specific Tutorial for additional help

This tool allows parts to interact with one another, meaning you can copy geometry from one part onto the other. Therefore, you can make the holes on the bulkhead tubes, and then project them directly onto the anti intrusion plate. This means that if you were to change the dimensions of the bulkhead, the changes would carry over onto the anti intrusion plate, and your holes would still be aligned. Good practice is to link one body at a time. 

A tutorial for this is in "NX Tool Specific Tutorials", which can be found in the resources section at the bottom of this page.



e. Extrude as a step file

After this step you can export your top level assembly as a STEP file and import it in Workbench. When doing this, make sure to export solid bodies specifically. This can be found in the menus in the export screen. We don't want our sketches transferring over to Spaceclaim.

FEA Preprocessing in Spaceclaim

f. Open the file in Spaceclaim and remove any duplicate bodies.

Here is a helpful intro on how Spaceclaim works: https://youtu.be/bd5Tz8ti6o8

Next, we will be using a new software called Spaceclaim. Open Spaceclaim, and open the STEP file you created in the previous step. Pro tip: Click "all supported files"

Expand the part tree completely, and mouse over all the parts in your assembly. As you do this, the corresponding body should highlight on the 3d model. If any part is highlighted several times, remove duplicates by right clicking on the part in the part tree, and clicking delete. Duplicates show up because the Wave Geometry linker creates a copy of a body in a new part, and both copies are exported in the STEP file.

g. Combine Bodies

In this step, we want to combine bodies that we are treating as a continuous object for the purposes of the Finite Element Analysis we will be doing later.

By the end of this step these bodies should remain

  1. The Anti Intrusion Plate
  2. The Bulkhead Cross Tube
  3. The other Bulkhead Tubes (Combined into 1 body)
  4. The 8 Welded Inserts (Each is a single separate body)
  5. The Impact Attenuator
  6. The Bulkhead Supports (Each is a single separate body)

Instructions:

  1. Select "Combine" from the "Design" tab
  2. Click on the first part you want to combine with other parts.
  3. Click on the "Select Bodies to Merge" button on the top left.
  4. Click on the other bodies you want to combine into one. 
  5. Hit Esc to exit the "Combine" tool

h. Share Faces

here is how this step looks on screen: https://youtu.be/8kJ2Xl3fH38

Next we will use the share face tool to communicate to Ansys that it should treat the face between two bodies as if it isn't there. This will allow the software to create a mesh that crosses between the two bodies. We want to do this with the Bulkhead cross tube instead of combining it with the rest of the bulkhead because we want to keep the option of doing the simulation without the cross tube. Using share face will allow us to suppress that body separately from the rest of the bulkhead if we wish to do so.

Instructions

  1. Select Share" from the "Workbench" tab
  2. Check the "Faces" box. (Faces between touching solid bodies should highlight in red on the assembly, and shared faces are green)
  3. Click the red faces between the bulkhead and the cross-tube (They should become green)
  4. Hit Esc to exit the "Share Faces" tool (DO NOT HIT THE CHECKMARK IT SHAERS ALL FACES. YOU DON'T WANT THAT)

i. Project the bulkhead supports and impact attenuator

Projecting faces 101: https://youtu.be/8DDWa9Khg3s

Now we will use the bulkhead supports and impact attenuator we made in NX. The idea of this operation, is we are projecting the shape of these objects onto the bulkhead and anti intrusion plate respectively, so that those faces are selectable in Workbench.

Instructions

  1. Select "Project" from the "Design" tab
  2. Click on the face you want to project. (It should highlight in orange)
  3. Click the "Select Direction" button on the top left, and click on a plane normal to the direction you want to project the geometry in. (It should highlight in dark blue)
  4. Click the "Select Target" button on the top left, and lick on the face you want to project your geometry on to. (It should highlight in a pink/peach color)
  5. Click the Check mark, and hit escape to exit the "Project" tool
  6. Delete the geometry you used for the projection (Bulkhead support/Impact Attenuator)

j. Model Nut and Bolt Contacts

We want to model the fact that there is a bolt through the welded insert, and it is held in place through friction with the bulkhead, AIP, and welded inserts.

Instructions

  1. Click "Sketch Mode" in "Design"
  2. Select the front face of the AIP
  3. Draw a circle with a diameter of 0.7" centered at the middle of every insert
  4. Click end sketch
  5. Project the resultant surface onto the AIP and Bulkhead

k. Open Workbench, import The Spaceclaim File, and open the editor

Now we are beginning the actual "Finite Element Method" part of the project. ANSYS Workbench is a FEA (Finite Element Analysis) software that simulates complex physics problems by breaking them up into small finite elements and solving each individually. We want it to tell us whether the AIP will deflect more than an inch if we crash the car.

Instructions

  1. Open Workbench 2022 R2. It is a software on the lab computers. (If your computer doesn't have it, go to another one)
  2. Double click on "Static Structural" 
  3. Right click "Geometry" and select "Import Geometry". Choose the Spaceclaim file you saved earlier.
  4. Right click "Model" and click Edit. 

l. Assign Materials

Because the problems we are working with are statically indeterminate, the material of each part will affect the result of our calculation (You will learn about this in Statics and Mechanics of Materials). As a reminder, the Anti-Intrusion plate is Aluminum 6061-T4, while all the other parts are AISI4130 steel. (You can use any steel or any aluminum, the results will be the same because the exact alloy does not change the young's modulus of the material)

Instructions

  1. In the tree on the left, select the geometry you want to assign a material to. (You can select several by ctrl or shift clicking)
  2. In the details menu (bottom left), click on the box to the right of "Assignment"
  3. Use the search bar to select the material you want to use.

m. Create Contacts

Unfortunately this video only covers making contacts in ANSYS, for advice climbing the corporate ladder, ask someone else: https://youtu.be/Iae8I07ZrEk


Contacts tell workbench that two parts are touching each other, and the nature of the contact surface. In this simulation we will be using two frictionless contacts, but you can also model frictional, bonded, and other contacts using the contact tool.

Before we create any contacts of our own, we should delete all the auto-generated ones. In contacts there will be a bunch of them. Delete them all. If you ever update the geometry, these will be regenerated and you'll have to delete them again.

The two contacts we want to assign are:

  1. A frictionless contact between the front side of the bulkhead tubes and the back side of the Anti intrusion plate. 
    1. This contact simulates the fact that the bulkhead will support the anti intrusion plate as it is forced backward by the impact attenuator (we will get to that later). If we didn't have this contact the AIP would fly off backwards through the bulkhead (suboptimal).
  2. A bonded contact between the outside surface of the welded inserts and the inside surface of the holes in the bulkhead.
    1. This contact simulates the fact that the welded inserts are prevented from moving relative to the bulkhead because they are welded to it.

Instructions (General instructions for how to make any contact)

  1. Right click "Connections" or "contact" select "insert", and then, "Manual Contact Region"
  2. Click the yellow box next to "Contact", and select all the faces on one side of the contact. When you are done, click apply.
  3. Click the yellow box next to "Target", and select all the faces on the other side of the contact. When you are done, click apply.
  4. Select the type of contact. (bonded, frictionless, etc.)


n. Create Joints

Joints tell workbench that two parts that are not touching have some sort of physical relationship. Such as a hinge constraining a door to pivot about it. For us, joints will simulate the effects of the bolts that attach the welded inserts, bulkhead, and AIP to each other.

The two joints we want to assign are:

  1. A fixed joint connecting the front face of the welded insert to the 'bolt surface' on the AIP.
    1. This joint simulates the fact that the bolt is holding the welded insert to the AIP. If this wasn't there, the inserts and the AIP could float away from each other sideways.
  2. A fixed Joint connecting the back face of the welded insert to the 'nut surface' on the Bulkhead.
    1. This joint simulates the fact that the bolt is holding the welded insert to the Bulkhead. If this wasn't there, the inserts wouldn't transfer force to the bulkhead.

Instructions

  1. Right click "Connections" or "Joints" select "insert", and then, "Joint"
  2. Click the yellow box next to "Scope" under "Reference", and select all the faces on one side of the joint. When you are done, click apply.
  3. Click the yellow box next to "Scope" under "mobile", and select all the faces on the other side of the joint. When you are done, click apply.
  4. Select the type of joint. (fixed, revolute, etc.)

o. Create Supports, and add the Force

These are the boundary conditions for the simulation. The fixed supports enforce that the selected region can not deflect or deform, and the force applies a force at a certain surface in a certain direction.

In our simulation, the fixed supports will be the bulkhead support surfaces you projected onto the bulkhead in Spaceclaim.


In our simulation, the force will be applied at the impact attenuator you projected onto the AIP in Spaceclaim. The force is derived from the crash data shown below.

Support Instructions

  1. Right click "Static Structural", and click "Insert" and then "Fixed Support"
  2. Select the surfaces you want to be Fixed Supports 

Force Instructions

  1. Right click "Static Structural", and click "Insert" and then "Force"
  2. Select the surfaces you want to have an applied Force
  3. Select the direction. If the direction shown by the red arrow is wrong, the arrows in the bottom left corner of the screen will let you flip the direction.
  4. Select the magnitude of the force. 

p. Mesh and Solve

The simulation we are creating uses a mesh to turn a complex physics problem into many simple discrete physics problems. This means, it literally splits our cad model into thousands of small solids, and solves a physics problem for each. (Ask Emil if you want more details on how it works) Luckily for us, ANSYS is able to automatically generate a mesh for our CAD, unluckily, it is stupid and we have to give it some pointers so it knows what to do. The smaller the mesh, the better the approximation, but the longer it will take to solve. Generally, you want to use a large mesh to troubleshoot the simulation, ad a fine mesh to get accurate results.

Instructions

  1. In the mesh menu, select "Sizing"
  2. Select the geometry you want to give a mesh size to. (For this project, select everything)
  3. Select an element size. (For a light simulation, 0.5" is good. If you are looking for accurate results, 0.1-0.2" is preferable. (Ideally, give the most critical geometry the smallest mesh))

Finally, you want to select what to output once you solve. In this project, we want to know the stress and deformation in our assembly.

Instructions

  1. In the "Solution" menu, select "Deformation" → "Total", and "Stress" → "Equivalent Von-Mises"
  2. Click Solve in the top left
  3. Once the program solves. Look at your results, and try to determine whether they make physical sense. If they do, re-do the simulation with a finer(smaller) mesh, and observe the results. Then, IRL you would redesign your part to be more optimal (Mass Down vs. Strength)

q. Parametrize The mesh size and max deflection

How to parametrize a variable: https://youtu.be/66t0Rj4EB5I  

You can make various variables in your simulation into parameters. This means you can try different values and see the results that you want to see for each value. I will give the instructions for doing this with the mesh size, but you can do it with almost anything in the simulation. We tend not to do this, it is generally easier to just run the simulation many times in the design editor and iterate what you are doing based off of every new result.

Instructions

  1. Click on "Mesh" in your design tree, and click the white box next to "Element size". The box should now have a blue "P"
  2. Click on "Total Deformation" under "Solution", and then click the white box next to "Maximum Deformation". The box should now have a blue "P"
  3. Go out into workbench (Out of the design editor). You should now see a box that says "Parameter Set". Double click it.
  4. In the menu that is pulled up, you can fill in the table with various different mesh sizes. Try 0.75", 0.5", and 0.25".
  5. To solve, and have the program fill in the dependent variables, click the button on the top left that says "Update all design points"
  6. Wait a very long time, and then see your results.

r. Final questions

I want to make sure you know what you are doing. When submitting the project, be able to tell Emil the answers to each of these.

  1. What things could we vary to decrease deflection in the AIP?
  2. How could we decrease weight without increasing deflection in the AIP?
  3. What design changes would you make to the TLA based off of your results?
  4. How would you make this project clearer/easier to understand?
