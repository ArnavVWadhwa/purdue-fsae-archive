Hunter's Notes:

Chapter 4: Taking Measure

  • Throttle Position Sensor (TPS)
    • Tells the ECU where the throttle blade is so that it can be determined whether the driver is attempting to idle, cruise at steady state, accelerate, or decelerate
    • Rotary potentiometer
    • 0-5V
      • 0.5-1.0V = Closed throttle
      • 4.5V+ = Wide open throttle (WOT)
    • Many systems consider WOT to be anything greater than 70% because the effective flow rate changes so little at higher percentages
  • Coolant Temperature Sensor
    • Engines have a narrow temperature band in which they operate most effectively
      • Too cold and fuel has trouble atomizing before the combustion process
      • Too hot and preignition might occur
    • A slightly cooler engine will allow for a few extra degrees of spark advance
    • Idle speed and enrichment increase at colder temperatures
    • Thermistor (typically NTC)
      • 5V Input
      • Increasing temperatures are read as higher return voltages (resistance drop)
  • Air-Inlet Temperature Sensor
    • Air temperature has a direct effect on density and burn rate
    • Colder air temperatures allow for more spark advance whereas hotter temperatures require reduced timing to avoid knock
  • Mass Air Flow Sensor
  • Manifold Pressure Sensor
    • Calculates amount of air going to the engine through pressure (higher pressure = more air)
    • Pressure measured through a diaphragm that acts upon a normal strain gauge
    • Manifold pressure is directly proportional to engine load
  • Crankshaft Position Sensor
    • Used to adjust injection timing and ignition timing
    • Primary source of engine speed (RPM)
    • Missing tooth on triggerwheel gives offset angle
    • Used in conjunction with the camshaft position sensor to determine position in combustion cycle (necessary for engine start)
  • Camshaft Position Sensor
    • Operates the identically to crankshaft position sensor but with smaller trigger wheel
    • Adds layer of verification for information coming from crankshaft position sensor
  • Fuel Pressure Sensor
    • Injector flow rate increase as pressure along the injector increases
    • Must be modeled to correctly calculate the amount of fuel making it into the intake port
    • Used in non-return fuel systems for fuel pump control
    • Pressure measured through a diaphragm that acts upon a normal strain gauge
  • Oxygen Sensor
    • EFI systems can adjust themselves to correct for errors between desired and delivered air/fuel ratios through monitoring the oxygen content of exhaust gases
      • More oxygen means running leaner assuming no leaks
    • A leak will show the engine as running leaner as exhaust gases will flow out and oxygen will flow in due to non-constant output cycle
    • Basically, a battery that changes potential based on the level of oxygen present
      • Layer of zirconium oxide changes the sensors output voltage based on the partial pressures of the oxygen molecules
        • More oxygen means less output voltage
    • Closed loop means that actual injection is adjusted based on lambda reading while open loop is used just for data
    • Cold oxygen sensors have inaccurate readings so the ECU ignores them for a small period after start in a closed-loop system
      • Heated Exhaust Gas Oxygen (HEGO) sensors are made for this purpose
        • They have heaters so that they work on engine start as startup is critical for emissions as engines run slightly rich when they are cold
    • Increased CAM duration can cause issues as some unburned intake mixture can flow through the exhaust increasing the oxygen reading
    • Sensitive to exhaust backpressure (rich will read richer and lean will read leaner)
      • Sensors should be installed after turbochargers for this reason
      • The closer the sensor is to the engine the greater the transient accuracy will be due to delay time
  • Knock Sensor
    • Essentially a microphone with a filter to isolate the range of frequencies associated with knock
    • Attached to engine block or head to avoid the sound having to travel through the air
    • Most engine knock occurs in the 10-12 kHz range


Akash's Notes:

Chapter 4: Taking Measure

  • Throttle Position
    • Throttle position sensor (TPS), very important for EFI systems
    • Tells computer position of the throttle blade
    • Lets computer determine if car is in idle, cruising at a steady state, accelerating, or decelerating
    • Rate of change of throttle blade position and change of direction tells computer if driver is trying to change states
    • Most TPSs are rotary potentiometers
      • Read output in a 0-5V scale
        • 5-1V closed throttle
        • 5V>= wide open throttle (WOT)
  • Coolant Temperature
    • Too cold -> fuel unable to atomize before combustion
    • Too hot -> preignition may warp sealing surfaces
    • Most OEM systems are controlled at ~200F
    • At 180F -> few extra degrees of spark advance -> more horsepower
    • Cold engines are normally only used in drag race situations where emissions and cylinder wash from excess raw fuel are not a concern
    • Amt of enrichment to be added depends on actual temp
      • Temp reading comes from a sensor in the coolant path or mounted in the cylinder head
      • Most ECT sensors are of the negative temp coefficient type (NTC)
        • NTC thermistors have lower resistance as temp ^
        • With a steady input voltage (5V), temp is rea as higher return V from the sensor
        • If sensor is in coolant path, make sure no air pockets
  • Air-Inlet Temperature
    • Air temp has direct effect on density and burn rate
    • Air temp rises -> air volume rises -> air density down
    • Air temp, vol, density reading needed for determining # of oxygen molecules available for combustion
    • Inlet temp directly proportional to allowable spark advance
      • Assuming constant fuel delivery
      • Colder inlet temps -> more timing advance can be used -> more available horsepower
      • Hotter inlet temps -> reduced timing (avoid knock that comes from increased burn speed)
  • Inlet temp sensor design similar to NTC coolant-temp sensors
    • Sensor should be placed as close to the cylinder-head intake port as possible
  • Manifold-Surface Temperature
    • Some systems have a sensor to monitor manifold-surface temp
    • Lets computer model heat transfer from intake manifold to or from the intake charge immediately ahead of the cylinder
    • Identical design to NTC coolant-temp sensors
  • Mass Air Flow
    • Mass air systems use sensor to measure air mass flow into engine
    • More air molecules move past the sensor, more the signal changes
  • Manifold Air Pressure
    • Key element in speed-density systems
    • Charge density calculated by measuring exact pressure in the intake manifold and knowing the accurate temp
    • Air mass can be calculated by system efficiency * density * displacement
    • Manifold pressure measured by a diaphragm
    • Manifold pressure directly proportional to engine load
  • Barometric Absolute Pressure
    • Similar to MAP
      • Not connected to intake manifold
    • Helps mass-air-based systems detect changes in altitude
  • Crank/Cam Position
    • 2-piece system
    • Part 1:
      • Wheel installed on the crankshaft or camshaft
      • Wheel has teeth (1 or more will be offset)
    • Part 2:
      • Hall effect sensor
      • Provides output that fluctuates when each tooth passes on the signal wheel
    • CKP gives:
      • Instantaneous engine speed
      • Indicate actual rotational position
    • Fuel rail delta pressure
      • Used in returnless fuel systems to give the computer feedback for fuel pump control
  • System Voltage
  • Oxygen Sensors
    • Lets EFI systems correct errors between desired and delivered air/fuel ratios
    • Like a battery -> changes potential in the presence of oxygen
      • The more oxygen -> the lower the output voltage
    • Computer uses oxygen reading to trim the command fuel delivery -> closed loop operation
    • Computer ignores sensor readings till sensor is at an optimal temp
    • Installed as close to the cylinder head as possible
    • Most EFI systems switch to open loop operation at WOT
    • Wideband oxygen sensors
      • Accurate range: 8.0-30.0:1 air:fuel ratio
      • Lets computer actively correct fuel delivery at ratios outside the normal range
      • Operate between 0.8-1.3 bar at reasonable accuracy
      • Installed after the turbocharger
  • Knock Sensors
    • Detect detonations
    • Microphone that isolates a specific range of frequencies
    • Attached to the engine block
    • Range: 4-20kHz
    • Most engine knocks occur from 10-12kHz
