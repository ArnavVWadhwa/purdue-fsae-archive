Purdue Formula SAE : Upright FEA

Created by Unknown User (dewey2), last modified on Apr 26, 2021

This page will mainly detail the setup process of the FEA for the uprights. Alongside the FEA, the bolted joints on the uprights and the chassis tabs and the turnbuckles should be analyzed.

  • Bolted Joints (on uprights and tabs): Bolt Margin Calculator
  • Tabs: Lug Margin Calculator
  • Turnbuckles: Two-Force Member Margin Calculator

If you aren't well-introduced to FEA, check out the FEA basics page: ANSYS Structural FEA Basics

Force Model / Loads

The loading on the uprights is calculated based off the following Confluence page (numbers presented in the page are an example): Upright Force Model

The loads for each upright are calculated in the Drivetrain_structures_2022.xlsx file (for PF22) located here: Z:\2021\6_Drivetrain\Design

The Ul and Ur forces are used in ANSYS for the separate models. For the combined model, the Net Force X and Z are used.

FEA Model Setup

The upright FEA involves three different setups:

  1. Upleft
  2. Upright
  3. Combined Upleft- Upright model

These can all be accomplished by importing the uprights assembly and suppressing the irrelevant components for each model. With this, there will be three different static structurals in workbench. This is what gets imported into ANSYS:

One important step is to create a simplified bearing model to ease the meshing and contact difficulty. The outer race of the upleft and upright bearings were replaced by cylinders that extend to the inner diameter of the actual race. Below is an example of the upleft and upright bearings (which are also available in the 2022 drivetrain CAD)

Upleft Model

The upleft model includes the upleft upper tabs, upleft, and upleft bearing. The tabs are in simply to locate the turnbuckle location and will be suppressed before running the FEA.

A body to ground spring is added to represent the turnbuckle. The body side is on the upleft bolted joint that connects to the turnbuckle rod end. The ground side is placed on the two tab holes (ANSYS will automatically place it in the center of the two holes). Once placed, the tabs can be suppressed. The spring stiffness is set to a high value where it effectively does not have deformation (2.9E7 lbf/in) and the behavior of the mobile selection is set to deformable. If rigid, there may be high stress reported due to the rigidizing of the upleft holes.

Combined Model

The combined model is essentially adding both the upleft and upright models together. Both uprights, bearings, and upper tabs should be the only things in the model initially. Then, the springs can be set up and the tabs can be suppressed afterwards. This is what the final combined model should look like:

The only additional item in this is a beam element serving as the stability bar which is connected to the stability bar hole on each upright.

The beam material is set to the upright aluminum (all aluminums are going to have essentially the same stiffness) with body-body. The reference and mobile locations are rigid as there's no expectations that the hole locations will have high stress so it shouldn't impact any results. Rigid will allow the simulation to run quicker than deformable.

Uprights Material

  • Upleft/Upright - 7050 T7451
    • The upleft/upright material is typically 7050 or 7055 depending on what is available at Arconic 
  • Bearings
    • The exact material and properties of the bearings are unknown, however they are a high alloy steel in which case the Young's modulus really won't change significantly across alloys.

Boundary Conditions

The boundary conditions are slightly different between the upleft/upright and the combined models.

Upleft/Upright

The upleft and upright require a cylindrical support on the lower mounting point with rotation free, frictionless support on the bearing side face, a bearing load onto the inner bearing surface with components from the force model, and a bonded contact between the bearing and the upleft/upright.

Cylindrical Support:

The cylindrical support acts as the bolt supporting the upright. The tangential (rotation) is set to free since the clamping force on the uprights by the tabs (due to the tension in the bolt) is not great enough to actually stop rotation entirely. You'll get an ANSYS error saying the model is under constrained, but it doesn't consider the spring's effects to counter the rotation.

Frictionless Support:

A frictionless support is placed on the inner face of the upleft/upright to make sure the upright doesn't twist in the simulation. Because the single simulation doesn't account for the factor it's connected to the differential, the upright will just twist excessively which really would not happen. The twisting is removed with the frictionless support.

Bearing Load:

Bearing/Uprights Contact:

The bearing contact for the current FEA is bonded as it is the simplest form of contact with the quickest solve time. A more realistic model would have this be frictional. however there have been significant difficulties in getting the model to actually work with it. Because all the deformations are rather small, it shouldn't have a huge impact on results, but it would be good to have a frictional model working to compare. This will show the bonded contacts.

Be sure to not select the bearing lip as a surface for this, only the radial surfaces as shown.

Combined Model

The combined model still uses the cylindrical supports in the same fashion as shown previously. The springs should also already be in the model with the steps already discussed. A remote force and revolute joint will be added.

Remote Force:

For the force is a remote force located at the center of the sprocket to induce the twisting that is actually happening on the drivetrain. The coordinates for this can be pulled from NX. Both bearing surfaces are selected with deformable behavior.

Revolute Joint:

A body-body revolute joint will constrain the 2 surfaces to remain aligned axially (for curved surfaces like this) and can only rotate about its own axis. The revolute joint in this is serving as the differential which is modeled as rigid (i.e. no deformation) such that the 2 bearings remain aligned at all times. This couples the 2 uprights together.

Meshing

The meshing will be detailed for the upleft. The upright and combined models use the same methods since they're using the same parts.

All the elements used are quadratic. Ideally, all the geometry could be meshed with quadrilateral elements, however only the bearing was meshed with quad elements.

This is the final upleft mesh:

Global Mesh Settings:

For anything that doesn't have a further refinement, the mesh adheres to these parameters. Namely the element size is defined and adaptive sizing is turned off.

Bearing Swept Mesh:

The default meshing on the bearing results in distorted and unordered elements. To fix this, a swept mesh is used.

Bearing Face Meshing:

To have an ordered mesh on the bearing side faces, face meshing was used on all faces of the bearing.

Contact Sizing:

Contact sizing is used for the bonded contact between the bearing and the upright.

Body Sizing:

Body sizing, specifically a sphere of influence, was used on the upright at the location of maximum stress. This was used to ensure further mesh refinements do not influence the maximum stress.

Results

In the results, the max stress may be located at a bolt hole where the cylindrical constraint or spring is located. If that is the case, it is an anomaly and the max stress is located elsewhere. For example, at the bottom of the upleft in the combined model, the equivalent stress shows this:

Other Suggestions

It would be worth getting the bearing contact to work with a frictional/rough setting rather than bonding to truly capture what is happening in the model. Rough would ensure no slippage.
