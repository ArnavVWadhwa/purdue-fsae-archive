Purdue Formula SAE : Hub Nuts

All done on Manual Lathe. You'll need to know the thread parameters you need, likely that is what you did on the CNC lathe for the hubs. This info can be found in the Mastercam file for that part.

High Level

  1. Turn stock to part's OD
  2. Drill and Bore out ID
  3. Thread Turn
  4. Face Relief
  5. Part Off
  6. Deburr and Clean Up
  7. Mill Flats and Side Holes

Approx Time: 6-8 Hours

1. Turn Stock to Part OD

Use the manual lathe (both 3 jaw and 4 jaw setup) to turn down stock to outer dimensions of the hub nut.

If you are confident with your speeds and feeds, you can take up to a 0.200" turning pass. If not, go lighter or ask shop staff for help.


2. Drill and Bore out ID

The goal here is to be about to drill out the majority of the hole, while leaving some material on the wall (0.050-0.080) to be able to bore and hit your diameter.

Start with center drilling and work your way up to your target size. Keep in mind you cannot go straight to the largest bit as you cannot cut with the web of the drill.

For example, if I am trying to get to a 2 in hole, I go with: Center drill > 0.5" > 1" > 2". Those are just recommendations, it really does not matter as long as you know you have made room for the web of the drill.

After drilling, use a boring bar to get to the target ID (should be right around the minor diam of the thread).

To make life easier in thread turning, you should drill and bore all the way through the stock. This way you have run-out space for your threading tool.

3. Thread Turn

This gets a bit tricky, check out this page and attached video: Thread Turning

The important thing here is to not take off too much material. Test fit the hub with every pass and it's ok if its still a bit snug when you are done turning. There are thread files you can use to make it fit nicer afterwards.

4. Cutting Face Relief

With all of our designs up til now, there is a relief cut into the face to ensure contact at the bearing inner race. The only significant notable detail of this is the radius. The easiest way is to find an insert (or grind a tool) with roughly that radius and cut the relief using that tool. The alternative is to make this a CNC part which is a bit ridiculous.

5. Part Off

At this point, if your stock was long enough for multiple hub nuts, the difficult part is over, just part off and repeat step 4 for each hub nut.

6. Deburr and clean up

This is especially important as the threads could leave a not-so-nice finish. You can fix this by putting a slight chamfer on the ID. This is also when you could put the hub nut back in the lathe to clean up with a thread file. I also did a very light facing as I didn't like the finish from the parting tool

7. Mill

This part should be pretty trivial.
