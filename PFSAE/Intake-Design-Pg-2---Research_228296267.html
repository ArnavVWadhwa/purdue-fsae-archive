  The Scientific Design of Exhaust and Intake Systems was the first book that was read. It was most recently updated in 1972 making it a good choice as a starter.


Chapter 1 - Basics

The point of the exhaust is to guide the out flow in an efficient way. Optimally, there should be no exhaust gas present in the cylinder after the exhaust stroke. 

Both volume and temperature of remaining exhaust gasses hurt performance.

Overlapping timing is used to help achieve this. Fresh air passes through both the intake and exhaust valves.

If the exhaust manifold is designed poorly, high pressure from one cylinder's freshly opened valve could impact the last phase of an adjacent cylinder's exhaust stroke:

Chapter 2 - Simple Problems

The volume of the exhaust manifold should not be too small. This would cause pressure to be built up too easily.

Design exhaust to absorb high pressure events when valves are opened and allow smooth flow.

Average pressure in the exhaust system matters, BUT the most important thing is exhaust port pressure near the end of the exhaust stroke. We want maximum 'scavenging.'

Basic fluid dynamics must be considered. Boundary layers, major and minor losses (ME308).

The exhaust can be tuned to utilize pressure waves and time the low pressure to arrive at the port at the end of the exhaust stroke, increasing scavenging.

For multi-cylinder engines, this effect is smaller, but more opportunities for gains are possible if the exhaust kinetic energy from one cylinder can be used to amplify the low pressure at another.
