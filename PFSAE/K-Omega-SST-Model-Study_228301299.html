Purdue Formula SAE : K-Omega SST Model Study

Created by Zhang, Chenkai, last modified on Jul 22, 2022

Background:

We used to to use K-w SST model up until 2019 when a rear wing flow attachment test was conducted. The rear wing was fully attached on the car, but K-w SST showed separation, whereas Spalart Allaramas model agreed with the test. Since then, we switched to S-A model and never looked back. We never tried to mess around with the settings in the K-w SST model to make it reflect the the test result. So this is what this study is about.

The K-w SST model is based on K-w BST model with an extra viscosity limiter. Since K-w BST model tend to overestimate wall shear stress, which leads to overestimation of flow separation, the additional viscosity limiter was designed to correct that. The limiter term lives the turbulent viscosity equation, which take the form of Â . The a_1 term is what people usually play around with. A large a_1 term delays the activation of the viscosity limiter and will cause a higher turbulent viscosity when it's active. A lower a_1 term achieves the reverse.

Geometry Set:

Geometry set used in the study was the 22_07_13 base geometry, which includes:

  • FW-8-0
  • RW-1-8
  • UT-EF-17
  • PF20 Chassis with 1 inch bulkhead raise and flat front roll hoop to main roll hoop floor pan
  • PF20 Powertrain

Sim Configuration

  • Same set of mesh for all sims
  • Yaw 15
  • FRH/RRH: 0/0

Both a1=0.2 and a1=0.25 case failed to converge after 7 hours of iteration. Therefore, their values are rather meaningless. And that explains their extreme Cl values.

In all cases, turbulent kinetic energy residual failed to drop below 1e-1. Which is bad even for our extremely low residual requirement.

Comparison:

To identify areas where viscosity limit is active, a field function of the following equation was created in STAR:

(a_1 * ${SpecificDissipationRate}) < (${StoredStrainRate} * ${MenterKwTurbF2})

This field function returns 1 when the limiter is active and 0 when it's not.

From left to right below, the a1 values are 0.2, 0.25, 0.31, 0.35, and 0.4 respectively.

At low a1 value, viscosity limiter was activated in large regions. As a1 value increases, viscosity limiter activation reduces. This matches the expectation.

Looking at pressure scenes, it's apparent why a1=0.2 (left) and a1=0.25 (right) case did not converge.

The a1=0.2 produced very messy front wing suction distribution, and the a1=0.25 case predicted excessively early flow separation. From now on, the unconverged cases will not be discussed anymore, since they are clearly bad.


Using the wall shear stress separation indicator, we can compare the Spalart Allaramas simulation (left) and the default a1=0.31 case (right). Note that because the separation indicator is based on the direction of wall shear stress, flow close to normal to the wall can be falsely identified as separation regions. Both cases present very similar flow separation characteristics. S-A predicts more flow separation in the leeward side side tunnel. The separation region is expected due to the local geometry and flow field. Both models predict the existence of small separation bubbles in the main diffuser.

Looking at rear wing, both models predict minor flow separation in the wake of the headrest and the engine intake.

Image below the image delta between S-A and a1=0.31. With dark areas mean the pressure in S-A simulation is lower and vise versa. Looking at the bottom view (left), S-A model predicts more suction in most places, which is consistent with the performance values shown above. Looking at the top view (right), both models predict a similar amount of pressure.

Comparing a1=0.31 case with a1=0.35 (left) and a1=0.40 (right) case. The higher a1 cases both predicted more separation in the diffuser, which is expected, since viscosity limiter is activated much less at higher a1 values. Due to the similarity in the amount of viscosity limiter activation, it is also no surprise to see that a1=0.35 and a1=0.40 cases produced very similar results to each other. On the rear wing, the flow separation characteristics are very similar to that of the S-A model.

There is practically no difference in pressure distribution between a1=0.35 and a1=0.4. So comparing both to a1=0.31 (left), there is some minor differences. This time, darker color means lower pressure for a1=0.31 case. The a1=0.31 case predicts higher pressure at locations where vortices created by inwash break down. a1=0.31 case predicts more severe vortex breakdown than a1=0.35 and a1=0.40 cases.

Conclusion

Both S-A model and K-w SST model with higher-than-default a1 settings produce very similar results. There are only 5 points of Cl and 1 point of Cd in it. Without more data from wind tunnel or on track testing, no conclusion could be drawn as to which turbulence model or which settings produces a more accurate results. However, I don't think it would be the end of the world to choose either since the results are so similar. Since we have a lot of historical data from running sims with S-A model, I would say before more correlation data is gathered, we keep using the S-A model.
