Purdue Formula SAE : Welding Exhaust Headers

Created by Unknown User (jpeerbol), last modified on Jan 17, 2022

For PF22 a 304 stainless steel header was designed. 18 ga 1.5" tube was used because it was the thinnest wall we could purchase off the shelf mandrel bends from SPD exhaust, which provides us an FSAE discount.

A jig was CNC machined from some high density foam that aero had as spare, anything would really work, you just have to make sure you keep the steel grounded while welding.

304 stainless steel gets welded with 308L filler rod, in a pinch you could use the regular 4130 filler we use for the chassis, the welds will rust and material properties might be a little off.

I used the regular 2% ceriated tungsten in 1/16" at about 55 amps, the largest gas cup I could find (7) and about 17 cfm of argon and 10 seconds of post flow. You probably could benefit from a larger gas cup.

I had a lot of difficulty welding the m5 EGT bung with 5/32" 308L filler, I was having issues with too much heat and melting / deforming the threads. 

I decided to try some thinner filler rod, the only thin 308L filler I could find locally. I think ideally you would roughly match the filler rod thickness to wall thickness (0.45"ish for 18 ga stainless), but I think it's easier to be precise with thinner filler than thicker.

After switching to thinner filler the results were much better.

Notes:

I would recommend practicing before welding the car exhaust, I ruined the first exhaust because I didn't practice and and was impatient with too thick of filler. I ended up having to splice in a new section. 

Make sure to purge the inside of the tube with argon when welding a tube, it really makes a difference. I just used some aluminum foil to cover the ends, plugged the lambda and egt bung. Took a spare argon tank with an extra regulator and tube and filled the tube with argon. I poked 3 small holes in the opposite end with 1/16" tungsten so the air could flow out. 

Make all your gaps as small as possible, it's worth the extra time getting everything tight, it will make your life much easier.

This video was helpful when I was struggling, you can search "tips for welding lambda bung"


This is a good example of why you want to purge the inside of you stainless welds.

https://www.instagram.com/etsfab/p/CYsFUPcL4Q5/?utm_medium=copy_link

"The top weld is purged, and the bottom one isn't, so whats going on here?
Reactive materials such as stainless, inconel and titanium can have real performance and strength issues if the backside of the weld is left to react with the atmosphere. As we strive for full penetration into the material this will be evident on the inside, and this penetration reacts with the atmosphere inside the pipe and fails to create a sealed seam or worse, create a jagged mess of ugly buildup that could even be dislodged and cause damage to internal engine components as a result.
To shield the backside of our weld, we need to use the same argon that we’re using on our TIG welder to fill the inside of our pipework, and this process is called purging. In this context, purging refers to the removal of atmospheric air and the cleansing of the weld. Every pipe we make in motorsport applications will have some form of entry and exit hole, so to fill this pipework with Argon, we need to cap all holes and create an entry point for the argon, and an exit point for the atmospheric air to be pushed out.
When the backside of the weld has sufficient argon coverage, it can be as smooth as the external weld, creating a perfectly strong seam that passes gasses with optimal performance. By having a perfectly sealed weld joint, we reduce potential stress raisers and weak points, and increase the longevity of the part. If we don't purge the pipe, then we create an avenue for carbon to build up inside the weld seam. This carbon, mixed with expansion, contraction, and vibration, can cause breakage of the weld joint over time."
