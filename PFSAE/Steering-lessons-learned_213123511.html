Purdue Formula SAE : Steering lessons learned

Created by Unknown User (mgal), last modified on May 02, 2022


List of some things we ran into, things to consider for further design

  • A lot of the prior documents are in the sections above steering lessons learned
  • Jig and dry fit steering well
  • better if welds are away from bearings (grease is not meant to heat up to the temperature of 4130 welding (400-500F), ours were fairly close but we used the heat sync compound that was also used on u-joints
  • It is important to make the proper bearing choice within the design phase of your subassembly. There are many types of bearings out there but in our steering assembly, we used needle roller bearings, originally we went with open needle roller bearings without realizing that open bearings start to bind when exposed to the outdoors (Rust). We had to redo a whole steering column to change to a sealed bearing design since the car drove in the rain and seizing become noticeable.
  • Make sure to properly lubricate the bearings since lubrication is important for fatigue of bearing and it is hard to lubricate the bearings when the column is assembled
  • Use heat sync compound for all welds, each weld in our assembly was close to a component with grease
  • Try to redesign the bearing supports and steering assembly so that maintenance can be done without having to totally lose a steering column (and spline which is very expensive)
  • One way this might be possible is if the bearing sleeve holders were not full enclosures. This would have let us service the steering without having to order brand new parts. Most likely with a more serviceable system, the structures are also more complex
  • Make sure to account for electronic wires throughout the assembly (Steering rack cover will need to be designed after all plumbing, etc is in cad.)
  • A lock collar was used on the front face of the first bearing sleeve to prevent linear movement of the steering column tube.

welding....

  • tighten bolts to torque 
  • measure measure measure, the linear position of rack (needs to be centered for multiple on car welds as well as steering wheel)
  • weld slowly
  • use paste


One issue that was run into was roll pin walking. This means throughout cyclic loading the roll pins in the steering rack adapter started to walk off-center. This pdf goes into depth about why roll pins and some ways to prevent it. The reason our pins walked was due to uneven loading happening from the twisted bearing sleeve support. An angular force vector is created from the bending and ends pushing roll pins.
