Purdue Formula SAE : Aerodynamics Basics

Created by Zhang, Chenkai, last modified on May 30, 2023

Airfoil Nomenclature

It's important to understand what the words means so you can refer to things accurately.

Leading edge: the forward most point of the airfoil

Trailing edge: the rearward most point of the airfoil

Chord line: the line connecting the leading and trailing edges

Camber line: the line that divide the airfoil in two halves (top and bottom). Camber is usually expressed as a percent of chord length. E.g. 10% chamber means the max chamber is 10% of chord length away from the chord line

Thickness: self explanatory. Thickness is usually expressed as a percent of chord length. E.g. 20% thickness means the max thickness is 20%

Angle of attack (or alpha): the angle between the chord line and relative speed (velocity vector of the airfoil)

Suction side: the side of the airfoil that produces lower overall pressure.

Pressure side: the side of the airfoil that produces high overall pressure. This is content is convenient for use since we constantly switch between airplane airfoil and race car airfoils.

Lift and Drag vs. Angle of Attack

 

As angle of attack increases, both lift and drag increases up to a point. The point where lift stops increasing is called the critical angle of attack, where flow separates off of the suction side of the airfoil. This phenomena is called flow separation or stall. We will go much more into this later.

For 2D airfoils below critical angle of attack, the lift curve slope, the slope of lift coefficient vs. angle of attack, is approximately linear.

Airplanes care much more about efficiency, as defined by lift to drag ratio, so they operate at low angles of attack of a few degrees in cruise. During takeoff and landings, they operate at a higher alpha, closer to critical alpha, but far enough away so that a gust of wind or a slight piloting error does not cause the plane to crash.

Though racecars also care about efficiency, the efficiency required to achieve the best on track performance is much lower. This is especially true for FSAE. Therefore, you want to operate very close the critical angle of attack, but not too close that you are sensitive to ambient conditions.

Aerodynamic Forces

Aerodynamic forces are generated in two way, pressure and shear.

Pressure force is the probably what you think about when you think about aerodynamic forces. Note that pressure force always acts normally into the surface. It's the difference in pressure on an object that generates a net force. In the context of a racecar, you want high pressure on top and low pressure on the bottom, so that the the net force points down. Drag force generated by difference in pressure is called pressure drag, or form drag.

Shear force is generated by friction between the fluid and the surface of the car. Shear force is the direct results of viscosity, or the stickiness of the fluid. Shear force is directly proportional to wall normal velocity gradient. The higher the gradient, the higher the shear force. Unlike pressure force, shear force is always tangential to the surface. Therefore, shear force plays a very small role in lift generation, but can play a big role in drag generation. The drag generated by shear force is called skin friction drag or parasitic drag.

Different Types of Pressures

There are three types of pressure people usually talk about. Static pressure, dynamic pressure, and total (or stagnation) pressure.

Static pressure (P) is caused by molecular collisions with the surface of an object. It is the pressure that you "feel", it is the pressure that generate pressure force, it is the pressure that generates downforce and drag.

Dynamic pressure (q), which we have already touched on, is the additional pressure caused by motion of the fluid. 

When you bring a stream of air to a stop, like when you put your hand out of the window of a driving car, you get total pressure (stagnation pressure). It is static pressure and dynamic pressure summed together. When you bring the stream of air to stagnation, all of the total pressure is in the form of static pressure since the flow is not moving. However, if you don't do anything to the moving flow, it is comprised of ambient pressure as static pressure and dynamic pressure. Total pressure is a very important concept to understand, since it represents energy in the airflow. If you lose total pressure, it means works has been done by the flow. That's why it's important to keep total pressure high, since high total pressure flow has a lot more energy and by extension the ability to do work (aka generating force).

It's time to get into rant that I should have gotten into early, and it is about classification of drag. People like to classify drag as pressure drag and skin friction drag as indicated earlier(and induced drag, but that's another topic, the rant isn't about induced drag). To some extent, this makes sense, because the drags are generated by two different types of aerodynamic forces. I personally disagree with the classification, since they are two sides of the same coin. Neither pressure drag nor skin friction drag can exist without viscosity. In other words, neither exists without skin friction. Moreover, both can be qualified by total pressure loss after freestream flow interacted with a geometry. There is nothing fundamentally different about these two different types of drags. Think about drag as total pressure loss is much more useful when it comes to design as well. If reducing drag is your goal, you know the only way to achieve it is through minimizing losses (as opposed to think about "is the tradeoff of wall shear stress worth it for the increase in wake size" or whatever). 

Bernoulli's Principle

Bernoulli's principle is a touchy subject simply because the shear number of misconceptions out there. So I think it's good to clarify it first.

For those of you who don't know, Bernoulli principles operate on the basis of conservation of total pressure (aka conservation of energy). As flow velocity increases, dynamic pressure increases, static pressure must decrease to conserve total pressure.

Now, people think Bernoulli's principle applies everywhere. This can't be further from the truth. In rotational flow (which is always the case in the real world), Bernoulli's principle only applies along a streamline. This is the case for both viscous and inviscid flow. In the real world, energy can be dissipated into entropy due to viscous effects, therefore, energy is not always conserved.

Bernoulli's principle does apply everywhere if and only if you make the irrotational assumption (i.e. potential flow), which we will get into in the next page. So, be really careful about how and where you are using Bernoulli. 

Governing Equation of Fluid Mechanics

You can think about fluid mechanics in terms of control volumes. Control volumes are imaginary volume in space that contains geometries of interest. For example, if I am interested in a converging diverging nozzle, I can put a volume around it and analyze fluxes into and out of the control volume to calculate thrust generated by the nozzle. Control volumes are enclosed by control surfaces, and these are the surfaces that flow variable flux into and out of.


So what equations do I apply to the control volumes? The answer is the governing equations of fluid mechanics. Fluid mechanics is governed by 3 conservation equations: continuity, momentum, and energy. The equations can either be in integral or differential form. Integral form is easier to solve by hand, but CFD prefers differential form.

 

Continuity equation is the mass conservation equation. Continuity equation ensures the mass flow (change in unit mass per unit time) into the control volume is equal to the mass flow out of the control volume.

Momentum equation is the, well, the momentum conservation equation. It ensures the momentum is conserved in the control volume. Why do we care about momentum? Because force equals change in unit momentum per unit time. Momentum equation has a special name: the Navier-Stoke equation. 

Energy equation describes conservation of energy. Conservation of energy is of the least interest to us, since we run incompressible flow (flow density doesn't change) and don't take any temperature into account, energy equation is trivial and doesn't need to be solved. 

Fluid Particles

Fluid particle is a term that I will use a lot in the reset of aerodynamics fundamentals as well as CFD fundamentals, and it is important that understand this term.

As much as fluid particles sounds like air molecules, they are not. Fluid particles are much bigger. This point needs to be stressed since you cannot say a fluid particle has random motions like molecules do.

A fluid particle is a control volume small enough to resolve the physics. In other words, fluid particles are of different sizes in different problems. So in a way, fluid particles are analogous to mesh cell in CFD, if you are familiar with them.
