Purdue Formula SAE : Perimeter Shear

Created by Unknown User (mkotur), last modified on Apr 21, 2020

The main importance of the ANSYS Perimeter Shear simulation is that we are able to predict the shear strength of our sandwich panel before testing them within a reasonable margin. From past experiences, perimeter shear samples have not represented any difficulty when passing rules as the failure forces generated from these panels are usually well above the minimum requirements. The information brought from this simulation is essential when filling SES to make sure equivalency is met. Before delving into the perimeter shear simulation, read the 3-point bending document as it goes into further detail of how to set up your sandwich panel’s layup schedule using ACP (Pre).

Similar to the 3-point bending simulation, the sandwich panel is split into three parts: the top laminate, the honeycomb core, and the bottom laminate. The following figures represent the way all three parts are made in NX 12.0. I will not go into much detail on how to CAD them because they are not complex parts. All parts are 100x100 mm as are the minimal requirements specified by SES.

Figure 1. Top Perimeter Shear Laminate

The top laminate, shown in Figure 1, is a surface, and the “divide” tool was used to get the division lines seen on the figure. These lines are important because they help with the meshing of the sandwich panel as well as the set up of boundary condition. The first two inner circles represent the surface where the punch (25.4 mm diameter) applies a load as it punches through the panel. The outer most circle serves as a border to indicate the surfaces that are not affected by the punch, and thus do not see any displacement in theory. This will be clearer when we set the boundary conditions in the next steps.

Figure 2. The Honeycomb Core

The honeycomb core is an extruded solid. In this case we have a CAD representation of a half inch core. Notice how in real life the core has cells, however, it is not possible to CAD a 1:1 scale core with all the cells because it would make meshing impossible and the part would turn out to be larger than 200MB (trust me I have tried). Just make sure to extrude towards the -z direction so that all the parts line up correctly.

Figure 3. The Bottom Laminate

Similar practice used for the top laminate was used for the bottom laminate. The horizontal and vertical lines were used to split the surface up such that the meshing was organized, and the circle represented the metal plate that the sandwich panel sits on. The circle had a diameter of 32 mm as specified by SES. Also make sure that the bottom laminate is projected in the -z direction to the same thickness of your honeycomb core (12.7 mm in this case).

Once all the parts are made, they are ready to be imported into ANSYS. Figure 4 shows the project schematic for the simulation. 


Figure 4. Project Schematic for Perimeter Shear simulation

Notice how the project schematic for the perimeter shear simulation is very similar to the 3-point bending simulation. As a result, I will not go into detail for setting up material properties and the layup schedule. For those steps please refer to Figures 7 through 19 to set up your sandwich panel correctly. I will, however, go through the meshing of the top and bottom laminates as well as the core. Furthermore, since the “divide” tool was used instead of the “trim sheet” tool, the surfaces are not individually separated, which means that there is no need to open Design Modeler to merge the surfaces into one part.

Figure 5, below, represents the meshing of the top laminate. A face meshing tool was used to make sure that all the elements on the surface remained quadrilaterals, and two face sizing tools were used on the middle of the panel and the outer surfaces of the panel.

Figure 5. Meshing of the Top Laminate

As shown in Figure 5, the outer surfaces were given a larger element size than the inner circles because the part would fail in the center, where the punch was applying a load. Figure 6, below, shows what your final mesh should approximately look like. I would recommend going through the same meshing process, but take off the horizontal and vertical lines in Figure 1 to see how different the final mesh is. It would be a better way to understand why these divisions are helpful.

Figure 6. The resulting mesh for the Top Laminate

The honeycomb core was meshed in a similar way to the core structure in the 3-point bending document. A simple body sizing tool was used to obtain the result seen in Figure 7, below. An element size of 2.5 mm was used.

Figure 7. The resulting mesh of the Honeycomb Core


Figure 8. Meshing of the Bottom Laminate


Figure 9. The resulting mesh for the Bottom Laminate

Once the meshing is done, and the material properties have been set, you can now set the boundary conditions for the sandwich panel in the static structural module. The objective of the boundary conditions is to recreate the experiment to be as close to the real set up.

To begin with, use a displacement tool to fix the four sides of the core structure such that there is no movement in the X and Y directions. During the real experiment, the panel does not move side to side, as a result, the same should apply in ANSYS. Figure 10, below, shows the core with the displacement constraint.


Figure 10. Fixing the Honeycomb Core

Once the honeycomb core is fixed, you can go ahead and hide its geometry and show the bottom laminate geometry. To hide/unhide geometry, just expand the geometry tree and right click on the geometry you want to hide/unhide and find the option. For the bottom laminate, you should apply a displacement tool to fix the surface solely in the Z direction as shown in Figure 11. Notice how the middle circle is not fixed. During the experiment, the panel is able to expand into the 32 mm hole that the metal fixture has. As a result, the middle circle should remain free.

Figure 11. Fixing the Bottom Laminate

Once you have fixed the bottom laminate, you may hide its geometry and unhide the top laminate surface. The first step for the top laminate consists in fixing the outer surfaces that will not be impacted by the punch. Figure 12 shows the surfaces that are to be fixed.

Figure 12. Fixing the Top Laminate

Finally, another displacement tool is used to represent the punch going through the sandwich panel. Figure 13 represents the surfaces used to embody the punch. Notice how the surface between the outer most circle and the middle circle is not constrained. This is because in reality, there is a small area of the panel that is also displaced whilst not being in direct contact with the punch. If you do not account for this area, the results will output a reaction force that is much higher than the real force because you are constraining the same edge to be fixed in the Z direction, on the one hand, and move in the Z direction, on the other hand. In reality, you can view this phenomenon by looking at the perimeter shear testing document.

Figure 13. Applying a displacement force to the Top Laminate

Also keep in mind that the punch punches into the panel, thus the displacement must be towards the -Z direction. Each layup schedule will also have a different failure displacement; it will be your goal to input that displacement such that the composite failure tool gives a factor of safety close to 1. The two main solution tools you will want to use are, as mentioned previously, the composite failure tool, and a force reaction tool. I will not go into any detail for setting up the composite failure tool, instead check the 3-point bending document for more information. However, for the reaction force tool, you must use the displacement tool used to embody the punch to get a failure force linked to the displacement of the panel. This is shown in Figure 14, below. To find the reaction force tool, you must right click on “Solution (D4)”, go to probe, and click the “Force Reaction” option.

Figure 14. The Force Reaction tool

For a two-ply layup schedule using Toray 300 2x2 3k fabric, the failure force was estimated to be 7720 N. This value should then be used as the first peak failure and maximum failure when inputting theoretical results into SES. The resulting factor of safety, using the composite failure tool, was the following:

Figure 15. Composite Failure tool Factor of Safety

When using this simulation to find a value for the failure force, consider using a factor of safety of 1.15 to 1.20 to have a conservative result.
