Research and Development:

Engine Selection 

We know that PF20 was designed with all the right fundamentals. The stage is set and the time is right to make a large architecture change. The next best architecture change that would yield the most points would be an engine change. 

Engine Research

Dom and Ben narrowed down engine selection by compiling both torque curves and component weights for several engines. A page investigating several FSAE engine options can be found here: Engine Choice.  Torque curves, gear ratios, and estimated component weights were "stolen" from each of the restricted engines of interest to be used in the lap sim. Attached below is the excel sheet with all of the necessary information for each engine. Constant ~10% knockdowns were applied to E85 torque curves to simulate a plausible 93 Octane Torque Curve. 

EngineOptions_V2 excel page. Additional less important pages can be found here: Z:\2021\5_Powertrain\12_Engine_Choice\engine choice stuff


Lap Sim Results

Engine Selection started with a review of successful engines. The 600cc 4-cylinder and the 450cc 1-cylinder made up all of the 2019 top 14 teams. The 1-cylinder wins 8:6. Between the 4 cylinders, the Honda CBR600 and the Yamaha R6 are popular. Between the 1-cylinders, the KTM 450 SX-F and the Yamaha WR450F are popular. There are occasional 690cc 1-cylinder and 675cc 3-cylinders.

The 1 cylinder engine was predicted to score approximately 17 more competition points (running 93). The lap sim results also align with 2019 competition points results.

Engine Overview 

The 2019 WR450F we got from Yamaha USA weighed in at 64-65lbs dry.

It came stock with the following electrical:

  • crank position
  • gear position
  • starter positive
  • single phase stator

The 450 did not come with many sensors we need in order to monitor the engine to our standards. 

Overview on the engine we received, it is the 025 model specifically 


Sensors we needed to add required custom machined parts and or welding. 

  1. Oil Pressure and Temperature - a custom machined oil filter element adapter measures high pressure oil right after the internal wet sump.
  2. Cam Position - a hall effect sensor was required to measure cam position so we can get engine synchronization before the engine can start.  
    1. Note the valve cover is Aluminum so we can machine parts and weld to it. The R6 valve cover is part magnesium so welding the sensor bung from that onto the 450 failed. 

Additional Machined parts required for the engine also include a custom machined coolant inlet adapter piece.

Here is a page tracking spare part inventory: WR450F Team Part Inventory


Another challenge faced was electrical power consumption. We needed to track power consumption averages from all components on the vehicle due to the reduced stator output of the single cylinder, and the further reduced output of the 2019 WR450F engine from Yamaha's engine refresh for this model. 

Power Consumption Tracking can be found here: Power Accounting | PF22

  • Dom may attempt to design a larger stator housing to put an older higher output stator into the 2019 WR450F engine. 
    • This would benefit active aero and other external systems.


In hindsight, we are trading a heavier engine (R6) with oil system complexity to a lighter engine with engine synchronization complexity and sensor location complexity. (It has been a frustrating dead / finals week.)

Dyno

Load Control

The team's dyno was back up and running by the end of the Fall 2020 semester. We have full control over the dyno from at least 4000 rpm and at least 20% throttle. This is made possible via a special "Laura Valve" (Laura is an alumni who was the last to run the dyno successfully before us, 2012 era). The laura valve provides a good range of head to control the waterbrake perfectly for the torque output of the R6 in 6th gear. Thanks to Connor, a PI controller was implemented and we have the capability of +/-50 RPM load control. More information on the control system can be found here: Stepper Motor Load Control. 


Log and random notes on getting the dyno working can be found here:  Dyno 2020 Reborn. An explanation explaining the load control phenomenon is summarized in the attached slides. Testing the dyno load control took almost an entire semester and around 6 total dyno days.  

We will need to experiment with the 450 to see which gear it operates best in. The less rotating mass as well as the uneven power stroke due to the single cylinder will also play a role we may or may not need to worry about. 


Dyno Team

Running the dyno requires at least 2 people, however 4 is preferred. 

  1. MoTeC Operator / Tuner
  2. Clutch Operator / Fire Extinguisher Person / Water pump controller (if not using stepper motor) 
  3. (optional) Electronics Person to diagnose and troubleshoot harness
  4. (optional) Extra set of eyes for safety

It is also going to be nice to use the loading dock to move the dyno in and out, but it is possible to go out through the side big door closest to walc. 


Dyno Operation (R6)

  1. Setup
    1. Watertub - requires 5/16 wrench to open up access panel to get hose water from ME
    2. Priming the waterbrake waterpump - loosen small hose going into stepper motor valve. Hold hose to the ground and prime the pump. Reconnect.
    3. The electrical outlets outside of ME do not work. You will need to get power from the EE Building loading dock
  2. Running the engine
    1. Warm up in neutral, it is the easiest
    2. To shift into 6th don't, just start the engine clutch in and release the clutch into 6th
      1. I recommend to release it slowly but we have dropped the clutch to regulate RPM when starting the engine at full throttle
  3. Tuning the Engine
    1. Tuning Walkthrough Williams, Carson Kenneth link motec videos people need to watch

Oil

Design: WR450F Oil System Design Notebook

There is no oil pressure or sensor locations on the block unlike the R6. In order to solve this issue, the internal oil system routing was analyzed. The easiest, safest, and most accessible location (incase we need to swap sensors etc.) was located on the oil filter element cover. The oil filter element is located directly after the internal oil pump. This allows us to measure the high pressure oil as well as the corresponding temperature.
