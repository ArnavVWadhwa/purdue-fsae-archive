Week 2 Work:

Current Dyno Plenum CAD

shown below is the current design for the variable size plenum with the diffuser mounted on the side. The part in the middle can be moved to simulate different volumes. The main body is currently sized to vary volume from 6 to 10 times the size of the engine. There is also a cysm on the diffuser so its location relative to the plenum can be changed when making sure it works on the dyno setup. The design also includes variable intake runners where are accomplished by adding different length aluminum tubes. The plenum model ends with the intake runner neck and additionally, the section containing the fuel injectors will also be 3d printed. The different length tubes will be placed in between these two using hose sections and hose clamps to attach them. This will ensure that no potential damage occurs to the brittle 3d printed components. One of the main things that need to be determined is the wall thickness of the plenum so that it will not fail under vacuum testing conditions. ANSYS structural will be used for this. Another concern is making sure that the design will fit in the layout of the dyno. Furthermore, the intake ports on the engine are at an angle so the layout and angle of the runners may need to be changed so that the plenum can be adequately supported by sitting flat. This can be accomplished easily using either section of flexible tubing.

Initial design

Since creating a large 3d printed plenum would be difficult, using a large stainless steel piece of square or a piece of circular tubing would be ideal. Stainless steel would be used for ease of welding. These can be ordered from McMaster carr in foot long sections. Doing a circular section would allow for a stronger seal for the movable diaphragm but creating the adaptors for the runners and the diffuser would be more difficult. However, since the sealing is the main issue with this design going this route would be better. For the diffuser, a metal section with a flat piece and a rounded side could be machined. The end of the tube would be closed by welding a circular piece of steel to it. A hole would then be cut in this to weld a smaller circular piece of tubing to which the runner lengths would be attached to. The lip at the start of the runner can be 3d printed from CAD and either stuck or glued to the bottom of the plenum. The movable diaphragm would be made from stainless steel or aluminum and would be made with a radius slightly smaller than the plenum so that a rubber seal can be glued to it in order to hold the vacuum generated in the plenum. This could be braced using a clamp or a rod attached to the end of the plenum effectively creating a plunger. Going this route would dramatically decrease ANSYS testing time, machining time, and cost. Additionally, stainless steel is easier to weld but would cost 50-70 dollars more than using aluminum. This design would also allow for no changes to the variable runner length design. 

the potential option of a circular section for the main plenum and a metal end cap which would be used to secure the movable diaphragm. The second tube size has the benefit of working with an end cap that can be ordered online and not fabricated in-house

To secure the movable section an end cap would be added to the other end of the plenum tube meaning that the diaphragm downward movement would be prevented. A threaded shaft would be run from the diaphragm to this end pice. This shaft would be screwed or welded onto the diaphragm and would have bolts on each side of the cap, attaching the diaphragm to the cap. To move the section up and down to change plenum volume the shaft could either be turned left or right or the entire thing could be adjusted manually. If this design gets the go-ahead a BOM for ordering the large tube, small runner attachment tube, the bolts, the diaphragm, the end cap, and the shaft can be created. All parts would be ordered on McMaster carr. An initial drawing showing the theoretical design (including that of the diffuser adaptor is shown below). CAD to come soon.


Throttle Body Sizing

The equations above come from: Design and optimization of a throttle body assembly by CFD analysis and are used to determine the optimal throttle body diameter. Current calculations in Matlab are ongoing using this method. Additionally sizing is also being determined by determining the choked mass flow rate provided in the figure below. This value was set to the product of throttle body air velocity, density, and cross-sectional area. Additional work is being done to make sure the correct velocity and density values are being used but the current diameter using this method is 1.78 inches. 

Runner Length Sizing

Using the Helmholtz runner length tuning equations some runner length approximations have been made using the equations above. The graph above is for the first harmonic and shows that in our rpm range, the optimal runner length is between 6 and 13 inches. This matches up well with previous testing and tuning using the r6 engine and for values presented in other published FSAE intake reports. IVOD which is the duration at which the intake valve is open is subject to change as the cam profile is created and adjusted but is currently set to a benchmark of 68 degrees. Currently the harmonic (n in equation 7) is set to one which gives the most inertial supercharging benefits at the cost of an increased length. This is subject to later change.

Comparison between two potential Plenum to Runner design choices

These are two potential options for the base of the plenum. The first one would have slightly higher losses but would take up less room in the car. The one on the right is a smooth transition from the plenum to the runners which would minimize losses but take up much more room in the car. Doing smooth transition on the plenum for the diffuser and the intake runners would also have the benefit of making the runners slightly longer (depending on where the runner would start since there's no hard stop point) and most importantly offer more pressure recovery on the diffuser. The longer the diffuser the better as this allows for the flow pressure and velocity to increase and decrease respectively. The closer these values can return to ambient conditions the better since this decreases losses on total pressure, temperature, and density. Having a plenum that has a smooth transition (having the same diverging angle) will increase the effective length of the diffuser again at the expense of space.

Intake Location and sizing for vehicle:


several locations/configurations were tested for the intake in the vehicle. The first saw it wrap around the back of the vehicle. The was found to not work since not enough length was available and since the entire intake system would have to turn 180 degrees, decreasing volumetric efficiency and creating pressure losses. the bend angle also means that the plenum would have to be a lower volume to keep the x-sectional area smooth. The design is similar to the images shown below. Note that each of these sacrifices in plenum volume as their volumes are much smaller than ideal. This design also usually necessitates a discontinuity/bellmouth between the plenum and the runners, increasing pressure losses. While going that route would save space, it's not ideal. due to the angle of the intake port, the intake would also have to drop down so that it would be level with the vehicle.

the engine placement on the car also creates issues with the intake for all configurations. The intake port is mounted towards the front of the car with the engine moved back with the part of the car from the engine behind is significantly shortened. This means the intake has limited room to move without breaking the box that the intake has to be in according to rules and regulations shown below. If the intake was oriented the way is shown below (same as the r6 engine), it would have a limited length as well as several bends (to maximize length). The goal is to also not mount the intake too high so that it does not disrupt the lift coefficient or flow over the rear wing. Because of this design choice was eliminated pretty quickly. 

The third design choice was a side-mounted intake. This has the benefit of offering a lot more space for the length of the intake as well as an area to contain a large plenum. Side intakes have been used in the past but are less efficient for 4-cylinder vehicles. However, for the single-cylinder setup, orientation does not matter as much since the air is all going to one place. The current setup sees the intake trumpet mounted low on the car and going up to feed into the cylinder. This orientation offers the most room and keeps the intake away from the aerodynamics of the vehicle at the expense of a larger bending angle. Air also does not have much of an issue flowing up since it is so light. Once more parts of the car have been loaded into the car, potentially positioning the trumpet to catch fast-flowing air off the side pod can be considered (effectively blowing into the intake). Also as can be seen the area of the plenum is not smooth and perfect so different techniques of doing this sweep need to be studied in the near future to make it smooth while maximizing the volume. The current volume of the plenum is ranging from 170-240 in3 corresponding to 6.3 to 8.75 times the engine displacement putting it in the middle of the target. The plenum can be sized up and down pretty easily making adjustments after physical testing hopefully straightforward. The intake runner looks shorter but this is because a chunk of the end of the plenum adds length as well as the section of the engine between the end of the runner and the valve (sketched below). This "added effective" length can be tested on the dyno and in Ricardo wave to see how this affects the torque curve. Adjusting the runner length after testing will be more difficult as the entire intake will have to shift orientation slightly but won't be impossible. Another issue is making sure that the plenum does not get too close to the back chassis tubes, which has led to orientation changes. This location however can be used as an attachment point for an intake tab.

due to this layout putting a large moment on the runner connection, several mounting points have to be designed to attach it to the chassis tubes. One of the potential ideas is adding a loop around the plenum which is attached to chassis posts (where it is closest) using a chassis tab. It would also be very easy to bolt the throttle body or diffuser to the side chassis post or the plenum/runner to the top of the engine using a tab/post. For the image with the throttle body, black represents a tab while red represents a loop with an attachment point. These are only preliminary designs as intake sizing and structural FEA needs to be done to know how much support is needed and where. design and o-ring information

Once this was done, the design of the plenum was created. The goal was to make it as circular as possible for lower stress values while also making it able to handle multiple spaces. The current design uses larger circular end caps and a cylindrical section with the interior radius being 3 inches. This value was chosen as it gave a good length to width balance and made it so the volume could be effectively manipulated up to 10x displacement without excessive spacers. The design was made so that with no spacers, it had 5x engine displacement with a 1-inch spacer adding approximately 1x additional displacement. The wall thickness was initially 0.125 but this was increased to 0.2 inches. Design notes, calculations, and formulas for sizing are shown in the drawing below.

wr450f test intake design, Accura 60 properties, specs entered into ansys and boundary conditions

For testing, ANSYS Structural was used with the interior of just the plenum being subjected to -75 kpa of pressure force. This was used for the R6 plenum testing years ago and comes from MAP sensor data showing the lowest pressures experienced combined with a cushion for a factor of safety as the wr450f intake is of a different shape. The runner and the diffuser ends were held as fixed supports while the screw holes and the holes for the MAP sensor were added as compression-only supports simulating screws present. A full image of the BC's is provided below. The material in question, Accura 60, was created in Ansys as a new material using values taken from multiple 3d printing source data sheets with missing information filled in from PP plastic and Resin tables (which are very similar in the compound). Young's modulus was initially entered in wrong being low by a factor of 1000 which skewed results a ton and was not realized until a day later after excessive modification to the plenum (also shown for comedic effect). After this was fixed results are very favorable with the maximum deformation on the order of 10^-5 meters and a maximum principal stress value of 2.39690 mpa, well under the yield stress value of Accura 60, which is around 58 mpa. One area of improvement could be adding a larger bend radius to the flange section to decrease the stress located there, this can be accomplished if need be. After this design, modifications were made to also have the diffuser section be a flange as the material is too brittle to take screws. Besides this, in the future, the mesh will be made smaller and convergent analysis will be conducted (for tests with spacers).

Wr450F dyno placement:

Due to the geometry of the intake section and the angled intake ports placement on the engine, several modifications had to be made for the intake to be able to be secured in the dyno. One issue with securing the intake is that it is going to be changing lengths with the added spacers and longer runners. Thus, a standard tab would be ineffective. To accommodate this, several intake locations were considered. The first was to have it mounted straight up like a chimney. For this, the intake runner would be curved up by 39 degrees (its orientation on the engine) to get it completely vertical. Then a holder could be fixed on the base and a design using clamps being secured to a tower with multiple holes was thought up. One issue with this was the fact that the fuel injector would be taking up most of the location where the bottom support would be located. This along with the difficulty in manufacturing had this choice be eliminated. The concept drawing is shown below.

Because of this, it was decided that the intake would be mounted on the side. Doing so meant the intake would have to be turned by 90 degrees, making the runners slightly longer. One worry is that the runner in its base form with no spacers will be too long to fully test the shortest intake configuration especially with the added length from the port to the valves, although not much can be done about this. The ideal lengths that should be tested are 7-13 inches. Doing it on its side also meant an easier support design could be made consisting of a semi-cylindrical holder with a post that was secured to the table. Multiple holes could be drilled to have support at different locations. The length of the actual holder is rather short (~1 inch) so that it is not too long to hold the cylindrical section with no spacers. For reference, the top and bottom plenum sections have spherical caps and about 1.2 inches of cylindrical length (so that its volume is 5x displacement in base configurations). Rubber/neoprene stripping will be placed on the holder to offer a soft connection for the brittle 3d print material and to damp out vibrations. A slight design did away with the screws altogether and replaced them with t-shaped feet allowing easier movement. The bottom will have a rubber coating to have better friction with the table. These are designed to be made with extra stock with the only constraint being that the intake bottom currently sits about 15.94 inches off the table so the bottom + rubber coating + length + cylindrical cradle must be this high.

Since it was quickly found that the intake runner would have to curve to mount the engine to the car, the entire runner part was rebuilt from scratch. This included making a spline to control its geometry from a 2d sketch and not a 3d studio spline to get rid of imperfections. The final redesign used much fewer features and was very easy to modify with one sketch controlling the runner radius and curvature along with the point at which the fuel injector top was located, and another to control the angle of the injector. Also included in this redesign was space for an intermediary spacer to be placed to better secure the runner section to the injector housing. it will be made of tin steel and slide into a groove at the top of the injector housing and slip on the inside of the metal runner length. This will better anchor the runner to the injector whereas before it was only being secured by the hose and the hose clamp. Testing Throttle Body Size:

Before this, not much effort had been put into the testing of different throttle body diameters besides some Matlab scripts that have used approximated values. To get more quantifiable results of different diameters we want to test a simple Ricardo Wave setup that was created modeling the intake throttle body, bypass (with no screw inserted), and the top of the diffuser up to the smallest part of the restrictor. Testing boundary conditions were set as ambient at the inlet and vacuum at the restrictor, which was done to bring the flow speed to Mach 1 at the restrictor, choking the flow. The choked mass flow rate will be the maximum flow rate the engine will ever receive since after this point no changes in downstream pressure will increase the flow rate. Thus the throttle body must be sized to not choke this mass flow rate, degrading engine performance. However, it is sized too large throttle response will be not good since the engine needs only a set amount of air. This air amount may be achieved with a large throttle body at 20 percent throttle. Because of this, a compromise must be achieved. The current R6 throttle body is sized large at 1.26 inches partially due to poor design and to allow for a safety factor since pipe loss coefficients, discharge coefficients, and volumetric flow rate state that not all of the theoretical air is going to make it through. It would be nice if the TB housing was not changed too much and under the current standards a sleeve allowing no larger than 1.4 inches of TB space can be inserted. Because of this several lengths were chosen to be tested in WAVE: 1.26 inches as a control (case 1), 1.35 inches (case 2), 1.175 inches (case 3), and 1.125 inches (case 4). As can be seen in the charts, a size of 1.175 inches offers the greatest mass flow rate and velocity through the throttle body. Furthermore, the expected flow rate is highest with the largest sized throttle body. These results are purely experimental as kinks are being worked out using Ricardo Wave, but it doesn't give us something to test off of. If these values are correct, we can take them into consideration for seeing the throttle response of each sized throttle body. Since sleeves are easier to machine manually with only two holes needing to be drilled we can make multiple sleeves and test them. Each one will be able to use the same throttle plate and we can have multiple throttle plates water jetted. While the diameters inside are not going to line up perfectly, the behavior of the throttle is much more dependent on the throttle plate than it is on the pipe losses. Since a safety factor was built into the r6 TB which takes in more air 600/4cc vs. 450cc intake strokes, the TB of the WR450F is automatically going to need to be bigger. Therefore, the TB housing sized will be increased to fit larger sleeves. The converging and diverging angles of the diffuser will stay constant so it will be increased only to a point where the lower flush part of the throttle body can have the same converting angle without having to grow larger.

Intake Update 4/7/2021:

Intro

In the last month, many significant updates were made to the intake and the intake design philosophy. Before, the intake setup was designed to be as large as possible (for both the plenum and the runners) to make sure there was enough space for the largest possible configuration. However, math calculations revealed an issue. Intake runner lengths were found to be much lower than what was expected and modeled in CAD. The past design also had cooling lines running underneath where it arched, meaning-making runner length smaller would be challenging to over-complicate the intake and subteam integration. Thus, this intake was suppressed, and a new one was designed. This design works well for a wide range of runner lengths but gets dicey with very large plenum sizes so if large plenum sizes (above 8x displacement) are needed then yikes. However, alongside the previous intake, one other design has been laid out and is suppressed as well. As of right now, there is not much development for the case with short runners and an extremely large plenum. This will be a design task for the summer (if needed)  and may follow the dyno intakes shape as forum (sus) posts have noted that a cylindrical tank-shaped plenum is the best. For single cylinder intakes plenum design is more important than runner sizing, putting emphasis on getting the plenum down perfect.

Suppressed intakes (in blue)

Plenum

The new intake follows some of the older iterations, having it swoop up and over the engine with the air filter coming down on the side. This allowed for runner length to be any value needed, at the cost of plenum size decreasing slightly. The intake does not have a smooth plenum to runner transition like before and uses a bell mouth much like the R6 intake. This has the added benefit of making runner length calculations easier since, with the smooth transition, it was difficult to determine where the runners started. This allowed runner length to be increased without changing the geometry too much. 4 Stroke Performance Tuning states that with this kind of bellmouth, the height of the tube in the plenum should be 1-2 times the diameter of the runner for optimal performance. Right now, it is right in the middle of this value. Right now, the profile for the bellmouth is arbitrary; soon after research, a final profile will be created for the best performance. Also, since the plenum curves, the part of the runner inside is curved along with the spline that controls the plenum's shape to improve airflow and make top-down design changes more manageable. The spline chosen for this plenum is a new and much better method. The bridge curve command was used to connect the spline controlling the runners (same as in previous versions) to a small spline mapped to the diffuser. If plenum geometry needs to be changed, all that needs to be done is re-contain the small spline point to the center of the diffuser and adjust the spline angle so that it is normal to the diffuser exit. The bridge curve replaces using points that were hard to perfect and finicky to get perfect. Tangency can be changed at the start and end of the curve. The plenum does not curve too early after the diffuser, increasing strength, clearance space for bolts and making the package look and function better aerodynamically. MAP sensor placement is currently right above the bellmouth inside the plenum, which is optimum since it needs to be as close to the injectors as possible since it dictates fuel info. In the image below note the tighter angle right at the start of the plenum for bolt clearance. Since increasing edge bend radius, gives the same bolt clearance problem, this will be tweaked in the near future by moving bolt locations. As before this tight bend would be where boundary layer control could be added to control flow separation at this point if Ricardo wave results show that works. To test it, a small bypass duct will be added from the restrictor to the start of the diffuser to see how volumetric efficiency is affected. The current plenum uses a sheet sweep command and then a thicken command, meaning adjustments easier. If need be this can be replaced to make the plenum thicker in places if need be to increase strength and not increase weight too much. Interior profiling can be done too to make the curve angle less severe inside the plenum at the expense of weight increase and volume reduction (see below).

This will be applied in the fine-tuning state after physical testing commences. Since there is no smooth transition (the design instead has a diameter "discontinuity"), a flange could not be used like before.

Tabs

              The intake in its current form went through several slight iterations, mainly updating its overall location in the car. Tab placement was much for complex than the lower intake since there were not many points to mount them, with some locations such as on the roll hoop not being allowed for tab placement according to FSAE rules. Nevertheless, the initial intake had a decent tab placement with one mounted to the engine, side roll hoops, and a chassis piece by the headrest. However, it was found that the intake had to be 350mm (~13.7in) above the ground, a problem since the intake sat low as it was coming down from above. After it was adapted to this constraint, tab placement was nearly impossible to mount them in a way that triangularly "locked it" in place. Thus the intakes were moved again farther back in the car to get the diffuser flange in a place where a tab could be mounted to the roll hoop support. Doing this while keeping the ground clearance above 350mm and preventing it from getting too close to other chassis components was difficult since it was already mounted higher. However, after 12 hours of work, it was set without affecting sizing. The tabs, in this case, would have to be 4-6 inches long and bent, which in the case of using aluminum would not put it up to spec for strength. This is especially the case when trying to mount them off the plenum flange, which is the section of the intake that is the heaviest. Earlier FEA imagery above shows the large stress values on the tabs, pointing to the necessity of a different, stronger tab design. The runners needed to not be in a condition where they were acting as a load member or had concentrated forces on them during acceleration and cornering as this could lead to failure as its the weakest point of the design (due to the nature of single-cylinder engines) Thus, for tab design, a circular aluminum plate was designed and mounted on the diffuser side intake flange to remove and install easier. The plate will be bolted to this flange and is 0.125 inches thick. Three tabs then come out of this plate, with one going to the roll hoop support and two being angled up and down, meeting at the exact location (but with different heights) to the roll hoop. The way this was accomplished was by using construction lines for the flange sketch, shown below. The angles are the same magnitude but opposite of each other (see image below). All bending and tuning were done with the thick plate, allowing stress to be concentrated to this thicker and more "locked-in" location. These bend angles are currently set at an arbitrary 30 degrees since this looked the best visually, and strength-wise. FEA testing will be used to nail this angle down perfectly if the current setup is shown to not work. The tab was designed to concentrate failure on the diffuser and not the plenum or the smaller and weaker runners since the diffuser is a much cheaper part of making (it can even be made in-house) compared to the 1000+ dollar plenum and runner section. Finally, the tab housing on the throttle body was made longer so that there would be no interference with the tab (see the last image in this section).

Intake Whiffling


dimple design main images

The second addition is dimpling to both the diffuser and the start of the plenum. Both location's dimple geometry is the same for right now but since the flow speed (and potentially direction) will be dramatically different, this will probably change if this idea makes it into the final design. It should be noted that this still needs to be tested in Ansys (will start with just the diffuser since that's the easiest model) to fully validate it, as sizing and locations for right now have just been determined by reading literature. ANSYS Fluent results run just on the diffuser last year (while I was working on the E85 case study), showed that a significant boundary layer formed along the diverging section, negatively affecting volumetric efficiency, pressure, and mass flow rate.  The third image is from this test, which was performed and the very low end of flow speeds the intake will encounter, note how large the viscous region is with the rearview. The other images come from other tests done at higher speeds. The first is from the 2018 report showing ANSYS results at a higher speed. Note the much larger boundary layer. The supersonic (very high choke flow RPM conditions), also has the condition after the normal shock in the nozzle. Therefore, if something was in place that could make this pipe flow viscous section smaller, it would increase the overall efficiency of the diffuser. The design for the dimples is based on the fact that a rough surface causes the flow to turn turbulent, which delays its flow separation and boundary layer development since it keeps higher kinetic energy flow closer to the surface. Vortex generators have been tried to be used for this purpose but they have, due to their design, decreased mass flow rate. 

clockwise from top: diffuser CFD at near sonic (high rpm) conditions from 2018 design report (not made by us as diffuser angles different but shows pipe slow conditions), supersonic choked flow with norma shock in the nozzle for a diffuser (our's would never get to this point but b.l. formation image is interesting after shock, bottom two images from CFD last year on diffuser with an inlet velocity of 30m/s (note large location of slow-moving flow at exit which effects performance


For the diffuser, the initial dimple size was set at 0.05 inches wide by 0.01 inches deep. The dimple also includes symmetrical edge bends both on the inside and the outside to give it a smooth finish. These are of a 0.0275 radius. The way that this feature was made was by setting an array of points on the surface face. Thus for the pattern to work correctly, all features for a single dimple must only depend on this point. This sizing was calculated roughly and will be subject to change as more research takes place. For the diffuser, it was initially thought to put dimples along the entire diverging section of the diffuser but looking at the Study of Leading-Edge Dimple Effects on Airfoil Flow Using Tomographic PIV and Temperature Sensitive Paint, it was found that only putting dimples on the first section helps to reduce excessive drag. For this report the initial section was just the leading edge, however, for the diffuser, this value was chosen with previous CFD results above. It was elected to only put dimpling on the first 25 percent coming out of the restrictor. Another report shows pressure distribution around a simple setup with varying size dimples from 0.125mm to 2mm (not that these dimples were sticking out and not the other way around. This report concluded that small dimples had a large effect on flow, medium-sized (~1mm) did not do much more, but then as the size increased to 2mm, the flow pressure dropped due to the presence of recirculation areas.

dimple and point closeup for the diffuser and research onto dimple locations (right)

Another report looked at the actual dimple shape as opposed to the size, which is shown in the image below. Note that TKE stands for turbulent kinetic energy. As can be seen, the standard dimple has higher TKE compared to the smoother teardrop-shaped dimple, but have a higher drag with them. If the teardrop-shaped size is applied it would be for the diffuser only as the changing flow speed and direction in the plenum would necessitate asymmetric dimple shape for them to work adequately.

While diples on the diffuser are mainly a result of skin friction drag and a small amount of pressure drag, the dimples on the plenum would act more like golf ball dimples due to its sharp angle change. While this will hopefully change slightly once the size is better known, the geometric constraints, rule constraints, and sub-team integration have meant that the intake in this configuration will need to change sharply in direction. This will adversely affect the flow and efficiency through the plenum especially since the ideal shape is a cylindrical tank (like what is on the dyno design). Thus mitigating this will be pivotal for the effectiveness AND ease of tuning/sizing of the plenum. The testing plenum is simple for design reasons and the ones on Ricardo are 1D simulations only so making this complex geometry act more like a simple cylindrical tank will align its performance more to the testing models. Update April 27th, 2021:

Building off of the research done above, once again a new design for the intake was drafted up. The goal of this design was to give a geometry configuration that the previous two could not, a large plenum volume (8-10x displacement) while allowing for short intake runners (actual runner length at around 3.4 inches for 8500 rpm). It should be known that there was a slight mistake in the runner length calculations, in which things were being converted to inches twice making everything short by a factor of 2.54. After this was fixed it showed that there was significantly more wiggle room with dyno calculations and intake designs than was thought. For the configuration, the overhead intake could not achieve this due to the constrained areas above the engine (increasing plenum size would cause it to hit the chassis) since it had to be routed closer than usual for optimal tab placement. The first design (which will probably not be used) could have a large plenum but needed a longer runner to match. The main issue with the large plenum short runner is that it has to be designed in a way to prevent the intake from getting too close to the engine both for clearance, subteam integration (fuel and cooling), and to prevent the intake from losing strength due to the increased temperatures. To balance all of these variables, an overhead design was considered. It would be similar to the outgoing R6 design but would be jogged over to one side and then up to save vertical height (to increase rear wing performance). Aero gave a high of no more than the top of the headrest as to how high it could go. While no real constraint was given for how far it would stick out the side, this was to be minimized. This proved to be difficult since volume increased as it would stick out more, so a balance had to be found. 

initial drawings and dyno intake assembly which the new design is modeled after

Views of the new design. Note the use of the trumpet once again. The lines in the front view coking from the chassis are the boundary lines the intake can fit in as stated by rules. Max height is just under the top headrest point

The design was taken from the simple dyno-shaped plenum. This was done to increase the link between dyno results and how the car actually performed. Research also showed that its spherical tank design had better performance although this should be taken with a grain of sand since it was found on the FSAE forums. Of course, the dyno plenum had no chance of fitting in the car since it was designed with practicality and simplicity instead of size as a constraint. Still, the end goal for this latest intake was to make the plenum as similar in shape to the dyno one as possible. Initial sketches kept the same dyno cross-section but had the runner and diffuser exits angled on the sides, however, this still proved to be too big (see leftmost drawings). It was thought that there was enough room to have the intake "s bend" on its way up. This proved to be not the case as volumes found were not significantly higher than the last design. Thus the final design had it bending 90 degrees, and then 90 degrees again to make it almost vertical again (where the diffuser would be attached). These sharp angles were hard to do using the sweep command (geometry would come out messed up) and thus the number of cross-sections used was increased to four (two end pieces and two midsections). The two midsections allowed for the twists to be fully captured and guided in a nice aesthetically pleasing way. The final bend angle for the diffuser was also difficult to perfect as decreasing it (towards 90 degrees) increased volume but caused the height of the intake to increase. At the same time increasing it allowed for a smoother transition but decreased overall volume and caused it to stick out too far. In the cad, it was marked with a sketch of the maximum possible point it would stick out before it broke rules, which mandate that it not break the tangential line from the roll hoop to the top of the tires. Finally, the design could not majorly get in the way of any other subteam components, with the cooling lines being the main parts that needed room to come out of the engine.

highlighted cross-sections for the sweep, the position of plenum closer to the engine (could be an issue), and hole orientation to allow for easy access around other subteam parts/features

Perfecting all of these variables took time but was assisted by arguably the most top-down design out of all of the intakes. Both the runner and the plenum were controlled by a single spline that was a bridged curve between two very small splines. The small spline out of the engine was curved slightly to assist with optimal location, while the diffuser one was not. The location of the diffuser spline was dictated by a datum coordinate system. By moving this any which way the xyz location and angle of the diffuser could be adjusted with all of the other geometry the following suit. This was critical in getting the vertical height just right and location along the length of the car so that it didn't hit the chassis and so it stayed in the area defined by rules for the intake. After much trial and error, a base design was created and matured. Tab design has not been done yet but no major issues are suspected as the flanges are located in a way that makes tab placement easier. The plate/tab design described for the previous intake design will most likely be used again as attaching a tab to the back chassis piece will require it to be 5-6 inches.

Another worry was to make sure there was enough room to allow a cooling line to pass out of the engine and under the plenum. Testing Throttle Body Size:

Before this, not much effort had been put into the testing of different throttle body diameters besides some Matlab scripts that have used approximated values. To get more quantifiable results of different diameters we want to test a simple Ricardo Wave setup that was created modeling the intake throttle body, bypass (with no screw inserted), and the top of the diffuser up to the smallest part of the restrictor. Testing boundary conditions were set as ambient at the inlet and vacuum at the restrictor, which was done to bring the flow speed to Mach 1 at the restrictor, choking the flow. The choked mass flow rate will be the maximum flow rate the engine will ever receive since after this point no changes in downstream pressure will increase the flow rate. Thus the throttle body must be sized to not choke this mass flow rate, degrading engine performance. However, it is sized too large throttle response will be not good since the engine needs only a set amount of air. This air amount may be achieved with a large throttle body at 20 percent throttle. Because of this, a compromise must be achieved. The current R6 throttle body is sized large at 1.26 inches partially due to poor design and to allow for a safety factor since pipe loss coefficients, discharge coefficients, and volumetric flow rate state that not all of the theoretical air is going to make it through. It would be nice if the TB housing was not changed too much and under the current standards a sleeve allowing no larger than 1.4 inches of TB space can be inserted. Because of this several lengths were chosen to be tested in WAVE: 1.26 inches as a control (case 1), 1.35 inches (case 2), 1.175 inches (case 3), and 1.125 inches (case 4). As can be seen in the charts, a size of 1.175 inches offers the greatest mass flow rate and velocity through the throttle body. Furthermore, the expected flow rate is highest with the largest sized throttle body. These results are purely experimental as kinks are being worked out using Ricardo Wave, but it doesn't give us something to test off of. If these values are correct, we can take them into consideration for seeing the throttle response of each sized throttle body. Since sleeves are easier to machine manually with only two holes needing to be drilled we can make multiple sleeves and test them. Each one will be able to use the same throttle plate and we can have multiple throttle plates water jetted. While the diameters inside are not going to line up perfectly, the behavior of the throttle is much more dependent on the throttle plate than it is on the pipe losses. Since a safety factor was built into the r6 TB which takes in more air 600/4cc vs. 450cc intake strokes, the TB of the WR450F is automatically going to need to be bigger. Therefore, the TB housing sized will be increased to fit larger sleeves. The converging and diverging angles of the diffuser will stay constant so it will be increased only to a point where the lower flush part of the throttle body can have the same converting angle without having to grow larger.

Intake Update 4/7/2021:

Intro

In the last month, many significant updates were made to the intake and the intake design philosophy. Before, the intake setup was designed to be as large as possible (for both the plenum and the runners) to make sure there was enough space for the largest possible configuration. However, math calculations revealed an issue. Intake runner lengths were found to be much lower than what was expected and modeled in CAD. The past design also had cooling lines running underneath where it arched, meaning-making runner length smaller would be challenging to over-complicate the intake and subteam integration. Thus, this intake was suppressed, and a new one was designed. This design works well for a wide range of runner lengths but gets dicey with very large plenum sizes so if large plenum sizes (above 8x displacement) are needed then yikes. However, alongside the previous intake, one other design has been laid out and is suppressed as well. As of right now, there is not much development for the case with short runners and an extremely large plenum. This will be a design task for the summer (if needed)  and may follow the dyno intakes shape as forum (sus) posts have noted that a cylindrical tank-shaped plenum is the best. For single cylinder intakes plenum design is more important than runner sizing, putting emphasis on getting the plenum down perfect.

Suppressed intakes (in blue)

Plenum

The new intake follows some of the older iterations, having it swoop up and over the engine with the air filter coming down on the side. This allowed for runner length to be any value needed, at the cost of plenum size decreasing slightly. The intake does not have a smooth plenum to runner transition like before and uses a bell mouth much like the R6 intake. This has the added benefit of making runner length calculations easier since, with the smooth transition, it was difficult to determine where the runners started. This allowed runner length to be increased without changing the geometry too much. 4 Stroke Performance Tuning states that with this kind of bellmouth, the height of the tube in the plenum should be 1-2 times the diameter of the runner for optimal performance. Right now, it is right in the middle of this value. Right now, the profile for the bellmouth is arbitrary; soon after research, a final profile will be created for the best performance. Also, since the plenum curves, the part of the runner inside is curved along with the spline that controls the plenum's shape to improve airflow and make top-down design changes more manageable. The spline chosen for this plenum is a new and much better method. The bridge curve command was used to connect the spline controlling the runners (same as in previous versions) to a small spline mapped to the diffuser. If plenum geometry needs to be changed, all that needs to be done is re-contain the small spline point to the center of the diffuser and adjust the spline angle so that it is normal to the diffuser exit. The bridge curve replaces using points that were hard to perfect and finicky to get perfect. Tangency can be changed at the start and end of the curve. The plenum does not curve too early after the diffuser, increasing strength, clearance space for bolts and making the package look and function better aerodynamically. MAP sensor placement is currently right above the bellmouth inside the plenum, which is optimum since it needs to be as close to the injectors as possible since it dictates fuel info. In the image below note the tighter angle right at the start of the plenum for bolt clearance. Since increasing edge bend radius, gives the same bolt clearance problem, this will be tweaked in the near future by moving bolt locations. As before this tight bend would be where boundary layer control could be added to control flow separation at this point if Ricardo wave results show that works. To test it, a small bypass duct will be added from the restrictor to the start of the diffuser to see how volumetric efficiency is affected. The current plenum uses a sheet sweep command and then a thicken command, meaning adjustments easier. If need be this can be replaced to make the plenum thicker in places if need be to increase strength and not increase weight too much. Interior profiling can be done too to make the curve angle less severe inside the plenum at the expense of weight increase and volume reduction (see below).

This will be applied in the fine-tuning state after physical testing commences. Since there is no smooth transition (the design instead has a diameter "discontinuity"), a flange could not be used like before.

Tabs

              The intake in its current form went through several slight iterations, mainly updating its overall location in the car. Tab placement was much for complex than the lower intake since there were not many points to mount them, with some locations such as on the roll hoop not being allowed for tab placement according to FSAE rules. Nevertheless, the initial intake had a decent tab placement with one mounted to the engine, side roll hoops, and a chassis piece by the headrest. However, it was found that the intake had to be 350mm (~13.7in) above the ground, a problem since the intake sat low as it was coming down from above. After it was adapted to this constraint, tab placement was nearly impossible to mount them in a way that triangularly "locked it" in place. Thus the intakes were moved again farther back in the car to get the diffuser flange in a place where a tab could be mounted to the roll hoop support. Doing this while keeping the ground clearance above 350mm and preventing it from getting too close to other chassis components was difficult since it was already mounted higher. However, after 12 hours of work, it was set without affecting sizing. The tabs, in this case, would have to be 4-6 inches long and bent, which in the case of using aluminum would not put it up to spec for strength. This is especially the case when trying to mount them off the plenum flange, which is the section of the intake that is the heaviest. Earlier FEA imagery above shows the large stress values on the tabs, pointing to the necessity of a different, stronger tab design. The runners needed to not be in a condition where they were acting as a load member or had concentrated forces on them during acceleration and cornering as this could lead to failure as its the weakest point of the design (due to the nature of single-cylinder engines) Thus, for tab design, a circular aluminum plate was designed and mounted on the diffuser side intake flange to remove and install easier. The plate will be bolted to this flange and is 0.125 inches thick. Three tabs then come out of this plate, with one going to the roll hoop support and two being angled up and down, meeting at the exact location (but with different heights) to the roll hoop. The way this was accomplished was by using construction lines for the flange sketch, shown below. The angles are the same magnitude but opposite of each other (see image below). All bending and tuning were done with the thick plate, allowing stress to be concentrated to this thicker and more "locked-in" location. These bend angles are currently set at an arbitrary 30 degrees since this looked the best visually, and strength-wise. FEA testing will be used to nail this angle down perfectly if the current setup is shown to not work. The tab was designed to concentrate failure on the diffuser and not the plenum or the smaller and weaker runners since the diffuser is a much cheaper part of making (it can even be made in-house) compared to the 1000+ dollar plenum and runner section. Finally, the tab housing on the throttle body was made longer so that there would be no interference with the tab (see the last image in this section).

Intake Whiffling


dimple design main images

The second addition is dimpling to both the diffuser and the start of the plenum. Both location's dimple geometry is the same for right now but since the flow speed (and potentially direction) will be dramatically different, this will probably change if this idea makes it into the final design. It should be noted that this still needs to be tested in Ansys (will start with just the diffuser since that's the easiest model) to fully validate it, as sizing and locations for right now have just been determined by reading literature. ANSYS Fluent results run just on the diffuser last year (while I was working on the E85 case study), showed that a significant boundary layer formed along the diverging section, negatively affecting volumetric efficiency, pressure, and mass flow rate.  The third image is from this test, which was performed and the very low end of flow speeds the intake will encounter, note how large the viscous region is with the rearview. The other images come from other tests done at higher speeds. The first is from the 2018 report showing ANSYS results at a higher speed. Note the much larger boundary layer. The supersonic (very high choke flow RPM conditions), also has the condition after the normal shock in the nozzle. Therefore, if something was in place that could make this pipe flow viscous section smaller, it would increase the overall efficiency of the diffuser. The design for the dimples is based on the fact that a rough surface causes the flow to turn turbulent, which delays its flow separation and boundary layer development since it keeps higher kinetic energy flow closer to the surface. Vortex generators have been tried to be used for this purpose but they have, due to their design, decreased mass flow rate.

For the diffuser, the initial dimple size was set at 0.05 inches wide by 0.01 inches deep. The dimple also includes symmetrical edge bends both on the inside and the outside to give it a smooth finish. These are of a 0.0275 radius. The way that this feature was made was by setting an array of points on the surface face. Thus for the pattern to work correctly, all features for a single dimple must only depend on this point. This sizing was calculated roughly and will be subject to change as more research takes place. For the diffuser, it was initially thought to put dimples along the entire diverging section of the diffuser but looking at the Study of Leading-Edge Dimple Effects on Airfoil Flow Using Tomographic PIV and Temperature Sensitive Paint, it was found that only putting dimples on the first section helps to reduce excessive drag. For this report the initial section was just the leading edge, however, for the diffuser, this value was chosen with previous CFD results above. It was elected to only put dimpling on the first 25 percent coming out of the restrictor. Another report shows pressure distribution around a simple setup with varying size dimples from 0.125mm to 2mm (not that these dimples were sticking out and not the other way around. This report concluded that small dimples had a large effect on flow, medium-sized (~1mm) did not do much more, but then as the size increased to 2mm, the flow pressure dropped due to the presence of recirculation areas.

Another report looked at the actual dimple shape as opposed to the size, which is shown in the image below. Note that TKE stands for turbulent kinetic energy. As can be seen, the standard dimple has higher TKE compared to the smoother teardrop-shaped dimple, but have a higher drag with them. If the teardrop-shaped size is applied it would be for the diffuser only as the changing flow speed and direction in the plenum would necessitate asymmetric dimple shape for them to work adequately.

While diples on the diffuser are mainly a result of skin friction drag and a small amount of pressure drag, the dimples on the plenum would act more like golf ball dimples due to its sharp angle change. While this will hopefully change slightly once the size is better known, the geometric constraints, rule constraints, and sub-team integration have meant that the intake in this configuration will need to change sharply in direction. This will adversely affect the flow and efficiency through the plenum especially since the ideal shape is a cylindrical tank (like what is on the dyno design). Thus mitigating this will be pivotal for the effectiveness AND ease of tuning/sizing of the plenum. The testing plenum is simple for design reasons and the ones on Ricardo are 1D simulations only so making this complex geometry act more like a simple cylindrical tank will align its performance more to the testing models. Update April 27th, 2021:

Building off of the research done above, once again a new design for the intake was drafted up. The goal of this design was to give a geometry configuration that the previous two could not, a large plenum volume (8-10x displacement) while allowing for short intake runners (actual runner length at around 3.4 inches for 8500 rpm). It should be known that there was a slight mistake in the runner length calculations, in which things were being converted to inches twice making everything short by a factor of 2.54. After this was fixed it showed that there was significantly more wiggle room with dyno calculations and intake designs than was thought. For the configuration, the overhead intake could not achieve this due to the constrained areas above the engine (increasing plenum size would cause it to hit the chassis) since it had to be routed closer than usual for optimal tab placement. The first design (which will probably not be used) could have a large plenum but needed a longer runner to match. The main issue with the large plenum short runner is that it has to be designed in a way to prevent the intake from getting too close to the engine both for clearance, subteam integration (fuel and cooling), and to prevent the intake from losing strength due to the increased temperatures. To balance all of these variables, an overhead design was considered. It would be similar to the outgoing R6 design but would be jogged over to one side and then up to save vertical height (to increase rear wing performance). Aero gave a high of no more than the top of the headrest as to how high it could go. While no real constraint was given for how far it would stick out the side, this was to be minimized. This proved to be difficult since volume increased as it would stick out more, so a balance had to be found. 

initial drawings and dyno intake assembly which the new design is modeled after

Views of the new design. Note the use of the trumpet once again. The lines in the front view coking from the chassis are the boundary lines the intake can fit in as stated by rules. Max height is just under the top headrest point

The design was taken from the simple dyno-shaped plenum. This was done to increase the link between dyno results and how the car actually performed. Research also showed that its spherical tank design had better performance although this should be taken with a grain of sand since it was found on the FSAE forums. Of course, the dyno plenum had no chance of fitting in the car since it was designed with practicality and simplicity instead of size as a constraint. Still, the end goal for this latest intake was to make the plenum as similar in shape to the dyno one as possible. Initial sketches kept the same dyno cross-section but had the runner and diffuser exits angled on the sides, however, this still proved to be too big (see leftmost drawings). It was thought that there was enough room to have the intake "s bend" on its way up. This proved to be not the case as volumes found were not significantly higher than the last design. Thus the final design had it bending 90 degrees, and then 90 degrees again to make it almost vertical again (where the diffuser would be attached). These sharp angles were hard to do using the sweep command (geometry would come out messed up) and thus the number of cross-sections used was increased to four (two end pieces and two midsections). The two midsections allowed for the twists to be fully captured and guided in a nice aesthetically pleasing way. The final bend angle for the diffuser was also difficult to perfect as decreasing it (towards 90 degrees) increased volume but caused the height of the intake to increase. At the same time increasing it allowed for a smoother transition but decreased overall volume and caused it to stick out too far. In the cad, it was marked with a sketch of the maximum possible point it would stick out before it broke rules, which mandate that it not break the tangential line from the roll hoop to the top of the tires. Finally, the design could not majorly get in the way of any other subteam components, with the cooling lines being the main parts that needed room to come out of the engine.

highlighted cross-sections for the sweep, the position of plenum closer to the engine (could be an issue), and hole orientation to allow for easy access around other subteam parts/features

Perfecting all of these variables took time but was assisted by arguably the most top-down design out of all of the intakes. Both the runner and the plenum were controlled by a single spline that was a bridged curve between two very small splines. The small spline out of the engine was curved slightly to assist with optimal location, while the diffuser one was not. The location of the diffuser spline was dictated by a datum coordinate system. By moving this any which way the xyz location and angle of the diffuser could be adjusted with all of the other geometry the following suit. This was critical in getting the vertical height just right and location along the length of the car so that it didn't hit the chassis and so it stayed in the area defined by rules for the intake. After much trial and error, a base design was created and matured. Tab design has not been done yet but no major issues are suspected as the flanges are located in a way that makes tab placement easier. The plate/tab design described for the previous intake design will most likely be used again as attaching a tab to the back chassis piece will require it to be 5-6 inches.

Fuel injector position (vertical for easy rail placement), potential tab locations, and ribbing + edge bend to increase strength since the lower plenum/runner will be one piece

The plenum design is shown below with a current volume of around 9x displacement. This was the maximum size found to be sustainable after hours of trial and error configuration and measures but it could potentially be pushed closer to 10 if needed. The runner geometry had to be made specially to make sure the plenum did not impact or get too close to the engine. Another worry was to make sure there was enough room to allow a cooling line to pass out of the engine and under the plenum. The plenum as well as the flange was specifically designed to give it enough wiggle room. The flange was positioned in a way to prevent issues with cooling and to minimize the cost of 3d printing. One half of it curves one way while the other basically only curves the other way allowing supports to be hopefully placed in an optimal way. The bolt locations in the mid flange were placed so that they were as far away from the cooling line as possible to allow for easy installation and maintenance. The bolt holes on the diffuser flange were similarly placed around the structural support that adds strength to the initial section of the plenum as it curves substantially. These are basically much larger versions of the supports that were on the intake trumpet, which curves in a similar manner. The trumpet also makes a reappearance in the assembly as the vertical height is significantly increased compared to the last design. Plus it allows for the mouth of the intake to be facing forward. The trumpet may need to be tweaked still since it's not as high as PF20s intake so rockets still might be in danger of hitting it. Like the previous overhead design, the runners extend into the plenum and are contoured to match the main spline so they curve with the plenum so that the plenum can be large without it having to be longer. As of right now, the length inside is not quite as long as the last design but still includes a bellmouth. The bellmouth shape will be sized if this design is approved but as of right now it's just a placeholder cross-section. The diffuser side of the plenum also has an inner diameter larger than the diffuser exit diameter so that the volume could be maximized. This choice also has the added benefit of allowing the entrance angle to be set using chamfers, edge bends, or revolutions, getting rid of the headache seen with the previous design where it was up to the sweep commands discretion. The sweep was made using the sheet style and then thickened for simplicity.

discontinuity on the diffuser side (pre edge bend), and after with added chamfer angle to match diffuser

cross-section views of the plenum and the injector assembly. Note that injector angle may be made more tangential to runner to improve fuel spray pattern

The runners are very simple in design. It's similar to the last design but unlike it, there is no flange attaching it to the plenum. Rather, it will be integrated into the bottom half of the plenum. This was done as a space constraint since adding a flange would put it too close to the engine in its current state. This may cause problems with strength (will be tested with Ansys once tab placement is designed) and with cost issues, as a failure at the point where plenum transitions to runners, which is the weakest in the whole design, would cause major problems with repair both related to time and cost. The weird cross-section angle at the end of them was removed as this was only needed with the R6 engine. The inner and outer diamters remain the same although this may change with testing and later iterations. The injector placement is almost vertical allowing for easier fuel subteam integration. Its location also means it has a more direct spray pattern to the valves than previous designs. Runner lengths from 7000-1000 rpm are predicted to be able to be used with this design. Finally, the runner cross-section was increased to make it match the rubber gasket better. This should have been done earlier but tests with r6 runners in the gasket showed that they did not sit well inside it. Increasing it so the rubber + o-ring can act as a seal will increase the performance and strength of the design. The outer diameter was increased from 1.8 to 1.97 and the inner diameter was increased from 1.5 to 1.72 using measurements taken on the gasket in CAD. The old dimensions were from the R6 intake and had just been carried over. Along with this, the runner calculations script will be updated to take into account the increased cross-sectional area.

Overhead Intake Tabs, Ricardo testing, etc.:

For designing the tabs for this intake design, an extremely similar approach to the last intake was used when it came to the tabs coming off the diffuser. A plate was used that attached to all of the diffuser bolts to increase structural rigidy on the design. The plate contains 3 offshoots where tabs are attached to. Two of these offshoots are bent at 45 and 35 degrees, further increasing the strength of the design as the bends are concentrated on the most rigid part of the design. Unlike before the diffuser is much closer to the roll hoop section of the chassis so those tabs are much shorter than the single one going to the rear chassis section. This will probably be fixed/updated once Ricardo Wave tests on this plenum and runner shape are done. Once that is done, an ideal rpm can be picked (7000-8000rpm), and sweeps on plenum volumes and runner lengths will be done to determine which case has the highest volumetric efficiency. This will be used to update the model and since the plenum shape will most likely have to be changed (to fit and to maximize ideal tabs placement). This new plenum shape matching the previous case's ideal volume will be imported into Ricardo and the test will be run again. This iterative process will run until the previous case's geometry matches the current case's rpm with VE being maximized as well. The same approach will be applied to the previous intake design (see above) to tune it as well. Validation of Ricardo Wave results will be done on the dyno. The dyno tests that will be done before the summer will first test the ideal runner from Ricardo and the calculated runner length using the rpm used on Wave sweeps to see how each's torque and VE compare.

For the diffuser tabs, the tab going to the rear chassis section was angled upward to decrease the overall tab length (helping reduce bending). One of the tabs on the other side is angled downward (to lock in rigidity in this direction) while the other one straight with no angles. For decreasing moments, both angled tabs made around a 90-degree angle to each other when looking from the side, and the two smaller tabs make a right angle to each other when looking from the top down. These will prevent the intake from flexing during cornering and acceleration forces. The other direction (front/back) is not as locked as the current tab/intake design prevents this from happening without long tabs that would get in the way of other sub-teams components. However, a solution will be developed for this soon since this direction is pretty critical looking at how the intake assembly as whole wants to rotate. The last image above shows this with the red arrows showing rotation and the green arrow showing the gravitational force acting downwards at approximately the center of mass in this direction.

Additional tabs were added/planned for the intake. One comes from the center plenum flange which is angled to attach to the same chassis section as the rear diffuser tab. The end goal would be to make this tab 90 degrees from the diffuser tab as well to increase strength but as things are placed right now, this could only be done with an extremely long tab. It should be known that all chassis tabs use the same part that is welded to the chassis and includes a slotted design to accommodate imperfections in chassis manufacturing. This is done to not inadvertently preload the intake which would greatly increase the chance of intake failure. The other tab would attach from somewhere by where the intake runner starts from the plenum and would be attached to the engine as stated by fsae rules. This tab would not account for much structural support for the intake due to the location of the intake in comparison to the engine and the small number of places to attach a tab to the engine (most are out of the question as it is due to intake location). The previous design has this same issue and the tab made to attach to the engine was mostly there just to accommodate rules. The hole for this tab design is shown above, it was not modeled concentric to the edge bend angle (shown in red) as that would have put the hole too close to the edge of the ready small rib so the hole centered was moved in by around 0.05 inches. The tab is not modeled in NX yet since the attachment point on the actual engine is not known yet. There is a mounting point on the cylinder head I just need to go in and find its location on the actual engine. The design right now also uses a hole being made in one of the ribbed supports on the plenum which may change. The end goal would be to have the runner attached to the plenum with a flange-like the last design. There would be just enough room to fit bolts on and lots of edge bends to add strength on the small section of the runner that would be on the plenum side. The separate runner part would be then made from metal 3d printing to allow for better heat resistance and greater strength on the weakest section of the design. This design will be hopefully added once Ricardo can be used to find the optimal plenum and runner sizes given the intake's overall geometry, which will allow for less guessing with the design. It's better to actually have a quantifiable size goal for this intake when tuning the shape of it especially since doing so takes time. PLus the plenum is oversized (8.9 times displacement) so going slightly smaller would not be as much of a problem. Ricardo results also show multiple cases which give a similar high VE meaning we're not locked into one size configuration and if need be a case with a smaller plenum can be selected without adversely affecting performance (shown below).

All of the intake designs (especially this one) have "significant mass from the cylinder head." The intake rules state that only the engine intake tab has to be rigid and that the other tabs can include isolation to allow chassis movement to not load the intake. One good way of building this into the design while also adding strength to longer tabs, more rigidity in the non-normal tab direction, and decreasing weight is by using a rod end/carbon fiber rod design. This is already used on parts of the aero system on the car. As it sits right now the only tabs that would use this would be the two longer tabs and the engine tab since the other ones are too short, however, this will probably change as stated above. The engine tab would use rigid rod ends bolts to fit the rules (shown in the center above), while the other ones would use rod ends with built-in bearings to allow for movement, cushioning the intake if there's significant engine vibration or chassis movement. how much each rod end is screwed in would allow for ease of adjustment to the actual chassis location. To follow rule IC.2.3.2, they would include a jam nut to prevent unscrewing during competitions (shown in the first two images above). Finally, the lightweight and cylindrical shape of the rid itself would allow greater strength since its area is the same from every direction unlike waterjetted tabs which have most of their inertia in one direction.

Intake 3d Printing Material:

Another thing that will increase the structural performance of the intake is using a material this is less brittle, than what we currently have. Accura 60 has a good young's modulus but lower, impact strength. For all materials impact, strength is measured using the Izod Impact test which uses a cube shape of the material, and an apparatus that strikes the material at various speeds until it breaks. Usually, the material has a notch built in to concentrate stress and therefore failure to a single point. This gives a good representation of how intake will respond to handling mistakes such as dropping or being hit by the driver during egress testing. It also shows how it will respond to shocks like the car hitting a bump. There are also the criteria that measure elongation at which the material will fail, measured in percentage. This gives a good look at how the material will respond to preloading such as through engine or chassis vibration.

Material properties for Accura 60 are shown above. Looking at different materials Realize (who we use for printing) offers another material (Accura Xtreme) that offers much better elongation and impact strength. Accura Xtreme offers 2-3 times the impact strength and 1.5 times the elongation at break along with a higher glass transition temperature giving it an overall greater strength since the intake will be heated from the engine during runs (decreasing its strength). The lower Tensile Strength is not as much of an issue since the higher elongation means the material will be more flexible (lowering young's modulus). The plenum also has a circular cross-section meaning it's much stronger than the outgoing R6 plenum which has a rectangular cross-section (meaning loads aren't as well distributed). The R6 intake's wall thickness (0.22 in) was used as a baseline for the wr450f plenum and even though it was much larger (the first design), the plenum was still overdesigned at a wall thickness of 0.15 inches. Thus a lower tensile strength is not as much of a problem as before. Higher tensile strength/young's modules are also partially the downfall of Accura 60 as that means it's stiffer and therefore more suseptable to failure through the ways described above. 3d systems also have various resins on their website that offer ever better properties and more options. Their tough and durable line of resins is marketed to be better resistant to shattering by handling stretching, bending, and compression better. We don't want a material that is too flexible though as this will increase deformation, so the Durable and Tough 1500 are probably out. Tough 2000 is marketed as the stiffest one but it still has properties vastly better than Accura 60 in the places that are the downside of our current material. Out of all the options, this one seems like the best since it's not too stiff (looking at the plots below) but not too flexible. Free samples of these materials can be requested on their website so if we use these, we can test them in our own vehicle environment to see how each one compares and which is the best for our needs. These are shown below but it unknown if realize can use these with their printers or if they're only compatible with 3D system printers. Post-processing on these prints also increases the performance of these materials but involves the usage of another machine. Going this option (3d systems machines) would have the best results at an increase in cost and a potential switch in sponsors who have the required equipment that is large enough to print the intake so it might not be the best option as a whole. Testing (using material samples from 3d systems and realize for Accura Xtreme) and cost analysis will be done to see if going this option gives us the benefit we need compared to its overall cost.
