Purdue Formula SAE : Chassis Torsional Stiffness

Created by Dien Quang Nguyen on Dec 08, 2023

Definition: 

(Torsional Rigidity/Torsional stiffness) 

  • Defined as resistance to torque along the longitudinal axis 
  • Affects vehicle dynamics of racecar in steady state and transient events 

How Chassis Torsional Stiffness affects suspension:

1. Improved Suspension Geometry Control:

  • Explanation: Chassis torsional stiffness affects the control and consistency of suspension geometry during dynamic maneuvers.
  • Impact: A rigid chassis helps maintain the intended alignment of suspension components, preventing changes in wheel camber, toe, and caster angles. This is crucial for optimizing tire contact with the road surface and ensuring predictable handling.

2. Reduced Body Roll:

  • Explanation: Torsional stiffness influences the resistance of the chassis to twisting or flexing during cornering.
  • Impact: A stiffer chassis minimizes body roll by resisting the torsional forces generated during cornering. This allows the suspension to work more efficiently, keeping the tires in optimal contact with the road and improving overall cornering stability.

3. Enhanced Steering Response:

  • Explanation: Chassis torsional stiffness affects how quickly and precisely the chassis responds to steering inputs.
  • Impact: A rigid chassis provides a more direct and immediate response to steering inputs. This enhances driver feedback and allows for more precise control, especially during rapid changes in direction.

4. Optimized Weight Transfer:

  • Explanation: Torsional stiffness influences the distribution of forces among the tires during acceleration, braking, and cornering.
  • Impact: A well-tuned chassis helps manage weight transfer more effectively, ensuring that the suspension components work in harmony to maintain optimal tire contact. This is crucial for maximizing traction and handling performance.

5. Minimized Chassis Twisting:

  • Explanation: Torsional stiffness reduces the degree of chassis twisting or distortion.
  • Impact: Chassis twisting can lead to uneven suspension behavior and unpredictable handling characteristics. A stiff chassis minimizes such twisting, providing a more consistent platform for the suspension to operate.

6. Improved Ride Comfort:

  • Explanation: While often associated with handling, chassis stiffness can also impact ride comfort.
  • Impact: A balanced approach to chassis stiffness helps absorb road imperfections more effectively, reducing vibrations and harshness transmitted to the occupants. This is particularly important for vehicles intended for both high-performance and daily driving.

7. Dynamic Stability:

  • Explanation: Chassis torsional stiffness contributes to the overall dynamic stability of the vehicle.
  • Impact: A well-designed and stiff chassis enhances the vehicle's ability to maintain stability during sudden maneuvers, changes in road surface, and other dynamic driving conditions.

8. Suspension Tuning Considerations:

  • Explanation: Chassis stiffness is a factor considered in the overall tuning of the suspension system.
  • Impact: Engineers must carefully balance chassis stiffness with other suspension components (springs, dampers, anti-roll bars) to achieve a cohesive and effective suspension system. This requires iterative testing and tuning to find the optimal combination for the desired performance characteristics.


Chassis Stiffness and Suspension Tuning

Tuning the suspension system means adjusting variables that can be controlled to achieve desirable vehicle behavior and handling responses. Understeer and Oversteer and sensitive behaviors and can be adjusted via changing suspension spring rates in the front/rear. However, the effects of changing the stiffness of each axle depends on the stiffness of the chassis. A flexible chassis will simply dampen all responses and nullify the effects of changing suspension spring rates

Ideally, we want a close to if not linear correlation between front lateral load transfer distribution and front roll rate distribution. This means, we want to make sure that increasing the spring stiffness in the front axle will have a linearly correlating effect on the front lateral load transfer distribution. This scenario would be ideal as it enables us to effectively tune the behavior of the car based on driver feedback and testing results.
