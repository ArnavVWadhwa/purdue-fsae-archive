Purdue Formula SAE : Mega TowBar FEA

Created by Unknown User (dewey2), last modified by Panchal, Aarnav Nilesh on Dec 05, 2021

This page will mainly detail the setup process of the FEA for the uprights. Alongside the FEA, the bolted joints on the tow bar and the carbon rods should be analyzed.

  • Bolted Joints (on lower tow bar supports and tabs): Bolt Margin Calculator
  • Tabs: Lug Margin Calculator
  • Upper Carbon Rods: Two-Force Member Margin Calculator

If you aren't well-introduced to FEA, check out the FEA basics page: ANSYS Structural FEA Basics

Force Model / Loads

The force model is a determination of how much force must be applied to the tow bar tube (Fa) to lift the rear wheels off of the ground. For this, there is a force at the tow bar tube and the front wheel (as the rear wheel is not contributing anything), and the center of gravity is known. Technically, the car will be tilting forward as the tube is lifted which will decrease the force required at the tow bar tube (Fa), but for simplicity we just look at the initial lift required.

Where:

  • Fw = Weight of the vehicle
  • a = Horizontal distance from the tow bar tube to the CG
  • b = Horizontal distance from the tow bar tube to the front wheel (center)

The calculations of the tow bar force are in the drivetrain loads spreadsheet here: Z:\2021\6_Drivetrain\Design

FEA Model Setup

To begin, the entire tow bar assembly is imported into ANSYS and a static structural is used for the analysis. For the 2022 tow bar, all parts were suppressed besides the tow bar tube, inserts, lower supports, and the upper tabs as shown below. The carbon tubes will be modeled as springs.

Springs

Next, the springs will be inserted to simulate the carbon tubes.

The springs for the upper carbon tubes are body-ground with the body end connected to the lower mount and the ground end selected to be at the center of the two upper tabs (by selecting both holes). After applying the geometry locations, the spring will be placed in the center of the lower mount where in reality the rod end is on the outer side of it. To fix this, you will have to offset the spring location such that the spring location is at the center of the rod end. This will ensure the bending due to the cantilevered rod end will be captured. The mobile location is set to deformable as well. For the spring stiffness, the stiffness of the carbon tube was computed using k = EA/L (E = young's modulus of carbon, A = cross sectional area of carbon tube, L = upper support end to end). In reality, there is some steel and aluminum in the mix, but those sections are relatively short and this is a sufficient approximation.

The calculations of the stiffness are in the drivetrain loads spreadsheet here: Z:\2021\6_Drivetrain\Design

Similarly, the center brace spring is a body-ground with the body connecting to the left lower mount and the ground positioned where it is in CAD (coordinates pulled from CAD, it's offset from the chassis mounting point on the right lower mount).

With the springs defined, the upper tabs can be suppressed.

Contacts

Because of the tow bar tube inserts, there are 4 bonded inserts needed for the analysis. Some of the contacts could be frictional, but for simplicity and to obtain a solution quickly, bonded was used. Also, this area can be analyzed separately if needed since the loading is known at the bolt going into each insert (in reality since it's a big chunk of aluminum with low forces there won't be much happening here).

The contacts presented below are for the left side, but the right side works the same:

Contact 1 - Bonded between insert and lower mount faces.

Contact 2 - Bonded between insert and tow bar tube. This bonded is pretty accurate since it's going to be welded in anyhow.

Materials

The entire assembly in ANSYS is 6061 Al (other materials were investigated though).

Boundary Conditions

Cylindrical supports with free tangential (rotation) were used as the lower mount bolted connections. This is because the clamping force won't be keeping it from rotation, that's done with the springs.

The force application was placed on a section of the tow bar tube. The tow bar face was split such that there was a 4 inch section of the bottom half of the tube available for selection. This partitioning of the face was done in NX.

Meshing

Several meshing controls were used to ensure ordered mesh throughout the different parts in the assembly.

  • Swept methods were used for the lower mounts and tow bar tube
    • Lower mounts
    • Tow Bar Tube
      • 2nd swept control with same selection 
  • Body sizing was used to control the tow bar tube mesh size
  • Contact sizing was used for all 4 contacts to ensure proper meshing at these sites (showing 1 as an example)
  • A sphere of influence was used at the location of maximum stress to ensure mesh convergence

Results

The max equivalent stress occurred at the mounting point for the upper mount as shown here:

The region surrounding the hole can be analyzed via separate bolted joint calculations (in the spreadsheet). ANSYS or any FEA won't produce accurate results at the attachment sites for springs/constraints/boundary conditions usually. However, further down the fillet on the left side there is another high peak of stress which can be scoped to as the actual maximum stress.


Another item to look at is the total deformation of the assembly. Since this tow bar (2022) is so long, there is a decently high deformation which can't really be reduced without adding significant weight.

Other Suggestions

I don't think there are many other things to look to improve in this model. I think the main thing would be to replace the bonded contacts between the insert and the side mounts with frictional or other types to better represent that area.
