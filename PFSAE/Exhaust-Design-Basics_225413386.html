Purdue Formula SAE : Exhaust Design Basics

Created by Unknown User (dnocon), last modified by Unknown User (kinsellj) on Jul 03, 2022

Use this page for general tips. Keep detailed info to design notebooks.

The information here is very high level, and for high level education purposes only.

CAD:

FOR THE LOVE OF GOD ACTUALLY USE THE 'TUBE' COMMAND IN NX.

  • draw your exhaust header on several planes (if needed) using only lines and line fillets to connect everything... then tube command the entire thing all at once
    • only one tube command should be used for the entire header if you do it correctly (for a single cylinder) 
    • this way you can adjust your sketch and the header will automatically update
    • do this the right way once, and adjusting the exhaust will be the easiest thing to alter if something needs to change
  • when designing your header design the entire thing to use COTS parts from Burns Stainless and SPD
    • https://burnsstainless.com/#

Design:

Now... what gauge tubing should you use??

  • PF22 uses 1.5in OD 18 gauge (0.049in wall thickness) 304 stainless steel 
    • with 18 gauge be cautious about fatigue as the header can and will become a wear part if it is this thin
    • PF22 changed its exhaust header 2/3 of the way into testing so the comp header would be fresher.
  • For context, specialty lightweight race headers for circuit racecars are 20 gauge (0.035in wall thickness) and need to be replaced every season if not multiple times a season.
  • If you know the base strength of the material, the approximate pressure in the runner, and maximum temp, you can find max allowable stress given safety factor, weld, and temp knockdowns. Temp knockdowns can be found online but MMPDS is a good starting point. From here it's a hoop stress calc to get the desired wall thickness.

Other things to consider:

  • ID and exhaust velocity for scavenging
  • exhaust length for inertial supercharging effects (usually packaging will be valued higher than this)
  • expansion chambers
