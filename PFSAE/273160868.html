Purdue Formula SAE : PF24 Front Upright | FEA Documentation (guide?)

Created by Cameron, Alexander Grant, last modified on Nov 11, 2023

Overview

The PF24 upright FEA was a pretty big grind. Because of ANSYS being the way that it is, and the uprights being as complicated to model as they are (and requiring several varying cases to be studied due to this complexity), it took us a LONG time to get them set up and actually properly running. We do not believe that the previous year's model was set up correctly, and subsequently do not believe that last year's uprights structural analysis are, well, structurally sound. We are still not 100% that the methods we used for PF24 are perfectly valid, as we had to make a few simplifying assumptions (such as no bearing pressfit simulations) for the simulations to actually solve faster than your grandma's Civic. However, they should be within the ballpark of accurate, and with all the safety factors and estimations on the side of caution, completely overbuilt.Â On the fronts specifically, after getting the simulation properly set up, I went through about 26 different geometry iterations, mostly changing the brake caliper arms and related geometry, as the braking forces on the fronts seemed to pose a much larger risk than any of the other relevant upright forces.

Overall, this page aims to help next year's upright engineers not have to go through the same painful process that we did, so that they may pick up where we left off and hopefully design even better uprights. Also so they don't hate ANSYS with a burning passion for the rest of their lives like I now do.

Boundary Conditions/Contacts/Joints

Beams & Springs

We originally tried using springs to model all of the actuation arms, however we eventually realized that ANSYS likes to throw a hissy fit when springs are the only thing constraining an object. So, we swapped these over ANSYS generated beams (we also tried bringing in the actual actuation geometry, but this was completely over complicated and not very helpful).

You're probably noticing that the pushrod is also a beam, which doesn't make a whole lot of sense if you think about it, and you'd be right. Shut up. It also clips through the upright and contacts the LCA cup, instead of an inch or two inboard like it does in real life, and there's no rod end on the inboard end, which means that it has an associated bending moment, which is not accurate. However, this is the best that we could do before ANSYS would through yet another hissy fit. Fortunately, the LCA cup is (assuming a large spring force) essentially constraining the LCA outboard point in all 3 translational DOF, so this is a fairly valid assumption/simplification to make.

Spherical Joints/Rod Ends

To ensure that only translational forces we're being applied to the upright, we brought in the rod ends from actuation and applied spherical joints between them and wherever they attached to. For the inboard rod ends, we applied body-ground sphericals.

We also did the same thing with the LCA and UCA cups/sphericals, just set up slightly differently. Instead of applying a spherical joint from the spherical to the upright (or in this case the camber piece), we applied it from the spherical to the cup, which the beams subsequently connect to. We then applied a fixed joint between the spherical and the upright (camber piece). Ideally this last joint would have been a cylindrical joint, but ANSYS didn't like that, and because we already have a spherical joint, a fixed joint is an OK assumption to make.

Camber Piece Bolts

To connect the camber piece to the upright, we used two revolute joints to model the two bolts. These joints are pretty straight forward, and are probably the only joints that did not give us any issues.

Meshing

The meshing for the fronts was fairly straight forward. We used patch conforming tetrahedrons for the upright with 0.04 inch max element size for the upright, and 0.1 inch triangle elements for all the boundary geometry. We also simplified the inboard boundary geometry by setting them rigid.Â

Material Properties & Temp Environment

To keep things simple, we created a custom material file in ANSYS for 7055 Aluminum, and only inputted the relevant values. The front uprights are in a 220F environment, which, for 7055, has an ult factor of 88% and a yield factor of 90%.

Loadings/Cases

From TCP loads, these are the loadings we used for each of our cases:

The red cases are cases we did not model, because other cases were either a stronger version (Max Speed vs Max 3G) or a stronger version with additional loading at other points (Max Accel vs Combined 2). To calculate the braking force, we took the Fx of relevant cases (accel cases have an Fx component but it does not correspond to a braking force), multiplied by the tire radius and divided by the effective rotor radius (estimated in CAD). We then applied this magnitude to the braking force unit vector (also estimated in CAD) to get our braking force vector.

Above are the load values after we applied our 1.4 safety factor. These are the numbers we actually plugged into ANSYS. Combined 1 is highlighted in blue because it is the toughest case to pass. Reverse braking also proved to be a difficult case.

Running on the Purdue Scholar Cluster

Running on the Purdue Scholar Cluster can be daunting at first if you've never used any sort of terminal commands, but it's not too bad if you take it step by step.Â This confluence page is a great start to get it all set up, and here is a list of commands I found useful during the whole process:

In general, we ran 128 cores for every simulation and at ~1.4 million elements, it usually took around 17-20 minutes per case (start by requesting 40 cores - if the system is free enough, it will give you 128 due to the size of your input file). There was a weird bug that we weren't able to pinpoint, where the system would only give us 64 cores, and then fail due to lack of memory. When this happened, going back and manually requesting 128 cores seemed to work, though the solve time was still doubled.
