Purdue Formula SAE : Classical Lamination Theory

Created by Unknown User (mkotur), last modified on Apr 18, 2020

The objective of the classical lamination theory is to help us find the material properties of the laminates we use to make the sandwich panel. These properties are the Young’s Modulus (E) and the stiffness (k). Further expansion of the classical lamination theory can help us predict the force (W) a panel can experience before failing (we will discuss this later).

Consider the sandwich panel shown on Figures 1-3. The top and bottom sections represent the carbon layers also known as laminates (laminate = multiple layers of carbon stacked on top of each other to form a single sheet) and the middle section represents the core, which usually has a honeycomb structure to reduce weight and increase the second moment of inertia.


Figure 1. Front view of a three-point bend FBD of a sandwich panel

W represents the load applied to the panel, L is the span support length.

Figure 2. Side view of a three-point bending FBD of a sandwich panel

t is the thickness of the laminates, c is the thickness of the core, h is the thickness of the panel, b is the width of the panel.

Figure 3. Top view of a three-point bending FBD of a sandwich panel

1 represents the x direction and 2 represents the y direction

As shown on Figure 3, we have labelled two directions (1 & 2) which act as the representation of the x and y direction, respectively. It is important to label these directions because carbon fiber is an orthotropic material (orthotropic = the material properties are different in the x,y, and z directions) the labels will help us determine which direction the material property we use comes from.

For example: If we select a unidirectional carbon fiber and we want to know its Young Modulus, then we would need to look at its Young Modulus in the x-direction or E1 (E11 ­also works, where the first number is the face on which the young modulus sits and the second number is the direction. Think of it as an element particle) [AON1] and in the y-direction or E2 (E22 also works). It is important to make that distinction because E1 = 100GPa and E2 = 10GPa, approximately. As a result, we cannot say that the Young’s Modulus of the unidirectional carbon is 10GPa or 100GPa, it depends on the axis we look at.



Figure 4. Unidirectional Carbon


Moreover, if we were to rotate the unidirectional carbon, shown in Figure 4, at different angles θ, then the Young’s Modulus in the x and y axes would change. Therefore, before starting to use the classical lamination theory, you must know the orientation of the different plies in your laminate; it is good practice to make note of the orientations like so:

[0UD 45UD 90UD -45UD]

The notation above states that you have 4 plies of unidirectional carbon oriented at 0, 45, 90, -45 degrees with respect to the x-direction (or 1 axis in Figure 3).

Now that we know the orientation and the number of plies that our laminate has, we need to know the material properties associated to our carbon.

The Young’s Modulus in the x-direction (or 0° angle) – E11

The Young’s Modulus in the y-direction (or 90° angle) – E22

The Shear Modulus in the xy-direction (or 45° angle) – G12

The Poisson’s Ratio in the xy-direction (or 45° angle) – ν12


Figure 5. Woven Carbon (2x2 Twill fabric)

The same thing that will be used from the unidirectional carbon for the analysis is also used for the woven carbon, shown in Figure 5.

 [0 45 0 45]

The notation above states that you have 4 plies of woven carbon oriented at 0, 45, 0, 45 degrees with respect to the x-direction (or 1 axis in Figure 3). Notice how we only orient the woven carbon in the 0- and 45-degree directions. This is because the fibers in the woven carbon are oriented in the 0° and 90° angles to begin with. Therefore, it is not necessary to rotate them in the same way that we rotate the unidirectional carbon to make a quasi-isotropic layup.

 

Notice how we are only interested in the x and y direction. Since we want to keep this simple, we will look at laminates in two dimensions. For more complex problems, one may decide to use the z direction and we’d need more material properties such as E33, G23 etc. For our work, a 2D analysis is accurate enough to provide us with good results.

Once the stress for one ply is calculated, you can compare the values with the ultimate tensile and compressive strengths as well as the maximum shear stress of the ply. If the values obtained are lower than the previously mentioned stresses, then you can increase the force (W) until the stresses are above the maximum stresses that the ply can hold. This force will then be registered as the force at which your ply will fail and you need to do this iteration for each individual ply in your laminate. The ply with the lowest failure force will give you the resulting failure force for your laminate.
