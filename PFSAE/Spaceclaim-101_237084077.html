Purdue Formula SAE : Spaceclaim 101

Created by Aaltonen, Emil Johan, last modified on Jul 24, 2023

Intro

Spaceclaim is an FEA preprosessing tool. It is a helpful bridge between NX and ANSYS, and can be used to do things like remove unnecessary geometry, project surfaces for forces, supports, contacts, joints, or mesh sizing, make simple iterations, and prepare CAD for FEA by converting solids into shells or beams.

The strength of Spaceclaim is that it can make large modifications to geometry quickly. This is also its main flaw, there is no part navigator/outline where previous edits can be seen and modified. The only way backwards is the undo button, like in MS Paint, any edits simply become the geometry you are working on.

I will now go through the process you would go through to transfer your CAD from NX to ANSYS Workbench via spaceclaim.

Importing/Exporting Geometry

Spaceclaim can open NX files, but this isn't recommended. You will get the best results if you use Export as STEP file in NX. This way you can choose to only export bodies, so any reference geometry doesn't transfer over. To be clear, Spaceclaim will accept any faces that you do export, so if you want to use some of the faces you made in NX, export those as well.

To utilize these files, in spaceclaim, use the open button, and select, "show all supported files". After you open the step file, click save, to save the file as a spaceclaim file. These files can then later be opened in Workbench.

Removing Unnecessary Geometry

Since you may be analyzing several parts at once, you may have exported an assembly from NX. If the assembly has parts you don't need or parts you don't need right now, you can hide, suppress, or delete them from the part tree.

Hiding Parts

To hide a part, uncheck it from the part tree. This is like hiding in NX. It is still there, you just can't see it.

Voila!

Suppressing/Deleting parts

A suppressed part is still visible but it won't be visible in Workbench. To do this, right click it in the part tree and click "Suppress for Physics"

A deleted part is deleted... To do this, right click it in the part tree and click "delete". Optionally, you can also delete it by selecting the geometry and clicking the delete button on your keyboard. I suggest the part tree so you are sure the whole thing is gone.

Creating surfaces

Commonly in Workbench, it is useful to assign commands through a particular surface, like applying a force on a particular face. However, sometimes a force only affects part of a face, or only part of a face is in contact with another. In these cases, either the split or project tools can be used to create a new selectable face.

The Split Tool

The split tool works by creating lines in the direction of the coordinate axes on a surface in your part. If this tool will do the trick, it is easier to use than the project tool.

Tutorial

To use the tool, click on split in the design tab.

Next, select a surface. It will turn dark blue. If you mouse over the surface, it shows the lines that will be created if you click on any particular position.

After you click somewhere, lines are created in the direction of the global axes, with an intersection where you clicked. The lines highlighted in light blue above were just created using this method.

Tips, Tricks, and Use Cases

If you click on an edge on the border of the face, if the edge is aligned with the global coordinates (vertical, horizontal, etc.) the split tool can only create a line normal to the edge. This lets you partition the surface. if I wanted to apply a force along the end of the beam above, the split operation shown would let me just select the end of the beam.

The horizontal and vertical components above are touching, but only on the end of the horizontal component. I used the split tool to separate this area from the rest of the component, so my fritcionless contact will more accurately represent reality in workbench.

The Project Tool

The project tool projects geomtery along a straight line onto a face.

Tutorial

In the design tab, click "Sketch Mode".

Select the surface you want to sketch on, and sketch the geometry you want to project using the tools provided in the sketch tab. Once you are done sketching, click, "End Sketch Editing".

Your sketch will have created surfaces floating in the air.

Click "Project". On the top left, the cursor button lets you select geometry. Selected geometry is orange.

The direction button lets you select a direction in which your selected geometry is projected. If you select a line, the projection will be in the direction of that line. If you select a plane, the projection will be normal to the plane. Selected geometry is dark blue.

Finally, choose the surfaces to project onto using the target button. Selected surfaces turn a translucent pink and show the line you are projecting. Once you like what you see, click the checkmark.

Tips, Tricks, and Use Cases

If you need the same geometry on surfaces on opposite sides of a body (Like the contact patch for a nut and a bolt), and you already have the geometry on one side, you can select that geometry and project it across to the other side.

Select the target face(s) first. If you accidentally select a body and try to project it, at least Spaceclaim only needs to calculate how it would look on one face as opposed to all of them. This is the only operation I have found in spaceclaim that can take more time than a few seconds, and it is always a misclick.


Move Tool (WIP)

Selection

Within the move tool the top 2 icons are for selecting geometry. You will automatically have the first icon activated. This allows you to select any kind of geometry, like points, edges, faces, and even bodies (triple click). The second icon is the "select component" icon and it allows you to click on a body and it selects the whole thing, identiacally to triple clicking. Selected geometry highlights in orange.

Moving the anchor

The anchor is the coordinate system that you use to move the geometry. It is the center that rotations go around, and if you snap to a point(covered later), it is the anchor that does so, moving the geometry with it. The anchor can be moved to make placing your geometry easier. This can either be done by grabbing the yellow center of it on the CAD model and dragging and dropping wherever you want it, or selecting the "anchor" icon and clicking wherever you want the anchor to go on the geometry.

Turning the anchor

To allow your geometry to move in a direction that isn't global x, y, or z, you can select the "move direction icon" and select some geometry in your CAD. This will geep the center of the anchor in the same place, but rotate the axes according to the geometry you select. If you select a corner, the anchor turns such that one axis points directly out of the corner. if you select a side the axis turns such that one axis is aligned with the side. If you select a face, the axis turns such that 2 axes are on the plane and one is normal to the plane.


Directly tying your anchor to existing geometry

To move your geometry along a different line that already exists in your model, click the "move along trajectory" icon and then select a line in your CAD that isn't on the selected geometry. This will place the anchor on the line and allow you to drag your geometry along the line. As you can see below the line does not have to be straight. The geometry follows the line as if it were a train on tracks, turning with the line.


"Move radially about axis, and Fulcrum"

I can not tell any difference whatsoever when I press these icons and select geometry. You can do everything you need by placing the anchor on some geometry, rotating it, and moving along the given axes.


Snapping to geometry (MOST COMMON TECHNIQUE)

To snap the anchor to some other geometry in your CAD, place the anchor where you want it, click on the axis you want to move along, and then click the "Snap to" icon, finally, click the geometry elsewhere in your CAD you want the anchor to snap to. Your selected geometry will move such that the anchor is touching the other geometry (along the chosen axis only).

I will show a series of screenshots below to demonstrate. I want to make it so the center of one face of the big cuboid touches the center of one face of the small cuboid.

First I select the whole big cuboid. As we can see it is at an angle to the small cuboid.

Next, place the anchor on a corner by grabbing the yellow center of the anchor and placing it on the corner.

Then, select one of the axes on the anchor, click the "Up to" icon, and click a corner on the face on the small cuboid you want to touch with the big cuboid. Repeat this step with the 2 other axes until the cubes are touching corners. Once the corners are touching, the same procedure can be done with the orientation of the large cuboid, snapping the ange of the cuboid to the edges of the small cuboid. Once you are done you will have a result as shown below.

At this point, create a sketch on the shared plane of the two objects, place points at the midpoints of the sides of the cubes and align them to each other. Voila. You're done.
