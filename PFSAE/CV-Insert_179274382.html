Purdue Formula SAE : CV Insert

Created by Unknown User (dewey2), last modified on Apr 25, 2021

Purpose

The purpose of the CV insert is to distribute the load from the CV tripod roller onto the wheel hub without causing any plastic deformation. Without the CV insert, the wheel hub would have plastic deformation at the points of contact with the roller. The CV insert is a thin steel sleeve with the inside geometry compatible with the CV tripod.


If you aren't well-introduced to FEA, check out the FEA basics page: ANSYS Structural FEA Basics

CV Insert Geometry

The inside profile of the CV insert is made identical to the purchased RCV tripod housings. RCV has CAD files of their tripod housing which is where the profile was copied from (example here https://www.rcvperformance.com/rcv-fsae-drexler-inboard-tripod-housing-long.html).

The thickness of the CV insert is determined through FEA and was set to 0.050 inches.

Goal of Analysis

The CV insert FEA was performed to determine the maximum shear stress in the CV insert through a numerical model. These results will be compared to the analytical results from the Hertzian contact stress excel calculator to determine the legitimacy of both methods.

If both methods yield nearly identical results, then it should be fair to say that the maximum shear stress is being estimated accurately.

Force Model / Loads

The loading on the CV insert is derived from the maximum longitudinal acceleration of the vehicle (1.65g). Given the maximum longitudinal acceleration, wheel diameter, and radial distance from the center of the CV to the roller, the force on each roller can be determined.

FEA Assembly

A CAD model was made including the CV insert, wheel hub housing the CV insert, and a CV roller as pictured below. The loading on the CV roller is applied radially directly downward towards the CV insert.

The inside geometry of the CV insert matches the profile of the purchased, inboard RCV housings. The CV insert has a thickness of 0.050”. To reduce the meshing required for the problem, a quarter model was developed as shown below.

Materials

  • CV Insert
    • 17-4PH H900 Stainless Steel
  • CV Roller
    • 4340 high alloy steel (not specified by RCV)
  • Wheel Hub
    • 7055 aluminum

Boundary Conditions

Symmetry Conditions:

These restrict the selected surface to only move in planar motion. The inner faces all have symmetry conditions, while only the top part of the CV insert was selected for the top cut. This is because rather than restriction the wheel hub from moving from the top surface, a displacement constraint was set at the bottom of the hub to ensure no deflection comes from the wheel hub. A symmetry at the cut would result in the hub just bending away under the loading rather than truly compressing into the CV insert.

Displacement constraint (yellow):


Bonded Contact between CV insert and hub:

A bonded contact was used at this interface because there is no sliding motion occuring here and are tightly fitted.

Frictional Contact:

A frictional contact was used at the CV roller-insert interface to allow for deformation of the CV roller and insert independently rather than requiring mesh nodes to maintain contact in a bonded case. Many settings were changed as well from default (2nd picture).

The resulting contacts were all closed, which is an indicator that the simulation should run without issues. Open contacts typically will not have converged results.

Force Application:

One quarter of the total load was put on the CV roller since the model is only 1/4 of the total piece.

Meshing

Several mesh controls were used to ensure contact mesh alignment, sufficient mesh density at the contact location, and maintaining coarse elements far from the area of interest. Quadratic elements were used for the mesh.

Analysis Results

The analysis was run on the Scholar cluster (using 40 cores) due to the very complex problem and high mesh size, making it impossible for it to run on a lab/personal computer. The runtime on this was between 1-2 hours.

For info on how to use ANSYS on scholar, see the confluence page here: ANSYS Scholar Cluster Usage

Comparison of Numerical Results to Analytical Results

The numerical results were compared to the Hertzian contact stress calculator which calculates the MSS by simplifying the contact to a cylinder (roller) on a plane (insert). The spreadsheet template for the analysis is here: Hertzian Contact Stress Calculator

The analytical results (excel calculator) yielded a MSS of 65.18 ksi at a depth of 0.0062 inches.

The ANSYS results yielded a MSS of 67.04 ksi at a depth of 0.0061 inches. This is a 2.8% difference in stress and approximately the same depth.

The failure criteria for Hertzian contacts is MSS, where the safety factor is found with the following equation:

Given a CV insert material of 17-4PH H900, the yield safety factor on MSS is 1.27 with the ANSYS results, which is acceptable for the application. 

Final Conclusions / Design Outcomes

  • The final CV insert design as a 0.050 inch thickness and made of 17-4PH H900 stainless steel.
  • Because the ANSYS results were within a few percent of the excel calculations, any small changes to the geometry or loading can be updated in the excel to determine a new maximum shear stress rather than re-running the FEA.
  • The ANSYS mesh could be made a bit more efficiently by using a quadrilateral mesh, however it's difficult to implement as the element size becomes smaller at the contact and is bigger furthest from the contact. This element size gradient causes meshing issues which is why I ultimately used a tetrahedral mesh.


The FEA was reviewed with Jim Shaw with the suggestion of improving the meshing as previously stated, however he thought the analysis setup was good as a whole.
