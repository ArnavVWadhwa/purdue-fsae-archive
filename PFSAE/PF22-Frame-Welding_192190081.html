VR3 Order

Assigned tube numbers based on the number convention abb in order to meet VR3's 3 digit tube numbering request

  • a: the welding stage the tube is a part of
  • b: the order from the front that the tube is welded (usually) this was a little arbitrary, but having b be odd for left side and even for right side pairs was sometimes helpful

Weld Preparation

When dry-fitting, follow the weld schedule for placing jigs and tubes. Don't set up all the jigs at once, you will need to remove some of them inevitably.

Frame fully dry fit once the front plate was received from WJCI 09-11-21

After dry fit, masking tape indicators with tube # and direction (to the vehicle front and upwards) were applied to each tube.

This saved time and headache later on when jigging each successive welding stage.

Nonstandard jigs were also labeled with masking tape and this worked pretty well.

Swan Tab Jig

Rear wing swan tab jig was mostly successful but should be more careful with welding it. The base plate wasn't constrained and subsequently warped when the triangular tabs were welded on. Clamp it down to the table when welding it. When the jig was placed onto the main hoop, it was too far from the MRHB to weld. The solution implemented was to grind out the inside of the 

A better solution to this would have been to use spacers between the jig arm and the base plate to close said gap. 

Steering Column Support Jigging

The main takeaway from PF22 welding with respect to the steering column support was to avoid including the bearing sleeve itself in the manufacturing file. Consider only jigging the bearing sleeve support, then placing the bearing sleeve in the support backwards when dry-fitting the steering subsystem. This makes preparing for welding far easier since we can't weld the support with the bearings in situ due to warpage.

Headrest Tube Jig aka coat hanger

Was moderately successful. The jig only positioned the tube on one side, so someone had to hold the tube level while it was tacked/ welded (even though this was done the tube isn't visually level - maybe due to not letting the tube cool down enough between welds). I think a better jig could be made, but this jig was better than hand placing. 

Ergonomic Welding Considerations

Welding with a mirror is possible with practice but is very challenging. I found it worked well on tabs with edges that mostly ran parallel to the table. This includes suspension tabs and floorpan tabs. I found it much more difficult to move the torch away from me (vehicle Y direction) while maintaining the appropriate distance from the puddle and torch angle.

Additional Suggestions

I suspect most of the error in tube placement came from the interface between the PF_Arm4's and the PF_Towers. After many uses, the roll pins in the PF_Arm4's are getting bent out of shape which spells bad news for tube accuracy. 

One way to improve this in the future could be to replace the roll pins in the PF_Arm4's or remake them to have a threaded hole in the bottom.
