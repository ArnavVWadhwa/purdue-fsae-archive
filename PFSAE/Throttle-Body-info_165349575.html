http://nopr.niscair.res.in/bitstream/123456789/23129/1/IJEMS%2020%285%29%20350-360.pdf

This paper is very relevant because it contains research on designing a throttle body for a single cylinder, four stroke engine.

Contains important information on:

  • Throttle body diameter sizing
  • Butterfly valve shape
  • Throttle angle vs flowrate
  • Idle bypass

As shown by this graph, the angle that the throttle body is open does not directly scale to the amount of airflow into the engine because of how throttle bodies are designed.  Because of this, we must design a throttle cam that makes it intuitive for the driver to precisely control the throttle.

Methods For Finding Throttle Body Diameter:

  • Equations in above paper
    • Having trouble finding values for vacuum pressure of engine at wide open throttle (p in Vt equation)
  • Ricardo Wave testing
    • Design intake system in Ricardo Wave and run tests with different diameter throttle bodies until flow is not limited by the throttle body
  • Mass flow rate of throttle body equal to mass flow rate of restrictor
    • ρ v A = ρ v A 
    • Find area of throttle body then add ~15% 
    • Difficult because we do not know the pressures and velocities at both the restrictor and the throttle body. Also, these values depend on the cross sectional area of the throttle body

We want the smallest throttle body that does not limit airflow more than the restrictor. This will allow for greater throttle response and control.


Throttle Cam Design:

(from https://www.sae.org/publications/technical-papers/content/2003-01-2278/)

  • The size of the throttle cam will be determined by how much the throttle cable moves from the pedal being fully depressed
  • We can use S = r θ to calculate the required arc length at different points on the cam to correlate with the correct amount of airflow


Once we validate the size, we can begin the CAD of the throttle body and throttle cam.

Double Spring Redesign:

Requirements:

  • The rulebook requires the throttle body to have 2 return springs

Currently, there are 2 torsion springs mounted between the throttle cam and the throttle body. They sometimes interfere with each other and cause issues.

Idea 1:

One torsion spring remains in the original location. An additional extension spring attaches to the cam arm and the throttle plate between the throttle body and the restrictor.

  • Issues: Mocking it up in CAD, it was difficult to find a spring for purchase that had the required minimum and maximum length for the application while having it not interfere with the other spring

Idea 2:

Extends the shaft using a shaft that is keyed to the cam on one end. The extension is not threaded and a screw passes through and threads into the main shaft. This fulfills the purpose of holding the second torsion spring and holding the cam to the shaft.

  • Issues: The length of the extension might create a large moment on the shaft which could increase friction on the shaft bushings

Brainstorming for Switchover to Off the Shelf Throttle Body:

The initial decision was made to switch to an off-shelf throttle body since most of our issues with manufacturing and tolerances came in this region. This spring, it proved time-consuming and cumbersome to manufacture the throttle shaft and the water jetted throttle plates were far from perfect with the tab present. Looking at the throttle body that came with the R6, it was decided to custom order one with the cost outweighing the minimization in cost, development time, and headache. The goal will be to have the simplest setup that allows for the most options (potential custom throttle CAM) and ease of install (preferably having a flange + screw holes at the bottom for easy install even if the intake used needs the TB to be upside down for packaging). For now, we will be sticking with a cable-operated system but switching to an electric throttle body by 2023 will be preferred. Electric options will still be listed below though. Some initial companies have been found and are shown below each with its benefits and drawbacks. The goal is to find a company and have it modeled in CAD by the 27th. Modeling in CAD will be easy as we just need the screw hole locations and base sizes for everything to make sure it fits and to adjust the trumpet and the diffuser accordingly. When sizing it we will be sticking with the 1.15 - 1.5 inch sizing, which in millimeters is 29mm - 39mm. Testing in Ricardo will follow soon (tested with ideal intake described on the other page) to see what gives the best overall throttle response, torque curve, and volumetric efficiency. This will allow us to better hammer down the ideal size we will need, but for now, we will stick with this range. If an option is found without a base flange, an adaptor similar to the one used on the 450f intake (black rubber piece) will need to be used (shown below). Prior art by other teams is also shown below. 

Adaptor Example. This could be 3d printed by us

Option 1: Ecotron

https://www.ecotrons.com/components/tb/

These are sized right in the area we are looking for, are manually controlled, and have a flange at the base. They come in 28, 34, and 40 mm sizes that we could use although no custom sizing is available. They include injector mounts that would not need to use and also include a full cam setup with an included throttle position sensor. The throttle cam does look more difficult to replace with our own though, which is the downside of ordering a manual throttle, especially since most of these cams were designed for the short through operation of motorcycles.


Option 3: Bosch

https://www.boschautoparts.com/en/auto/throttle-devices/throttle-bodies

https://www.summitracing.com/parts/bch-0280750148

Bosch electronic throttle bodies come in a variety of sizes.  They come assembled with all the necessary components to run it out of the box (actuator, throttle plate, TPS) integrated together.  

32 mm (right where we are sized for the R6 right now) shown above for sale on summit racing. Would need potential housing adaptor

Option 4: AT Power Single Throttle Body (NOTE Sold in Europe)

https://www.atpower.com/single-throttle

Their 36, 38, and 40mm options would be in our range. Again this is a manual throttle body but it comes with a much larger cam that looks easy to take off and modify. Different throttle position sensors can also be added or none can be added allowing us to install our current one. Sizing and drawings are given in full detail for each one. The base of the throttle body housing is very similar to what we currently have meant we would not need much design change to the diffuser. Unfortunately, their smallest size is at our top end (1.41 inches).


Option 5: Jenvey SFS

https://www.jenvey.co.uk/support/faqs/formula-student/

Custom-made to order throttle bodies made specifically for FSAE that come in standard sizes between 40 and 50mm, and can also order other sizes.  Can mix and match bore diameter and lever specifications.  Linkage kits and TPS also available that are made specifically for them.  One of the smaller options.


Option 6: Koso North America

https://kosonorthamerica.com/product/34-mm-throttle-body-for-honda-grom-and-monkey/

Very similar to Keihin design but is shorter, which would help with packaging. Does not come with TPS sensor and looks harder to install. The throttle cam is also very small. Does not stand out but still added for reference. 34 mm shown but offer other ones as well.

Throttle Body Wave Testing

Throttle body testing consists of sweeping throttle position and rpm simultaneously for different throttle diameters and looking at results for volumetric efficiency. The name of the game is not to have the highest, but to have the linear section of the curve last for as long in the rpm range as possible. For example, a large throttle body may let a good amount of air in, but the maximum air the engine can use may happen at 50% throttle position for a certain rpm making 50% throttle feel like a full throttle. The goal is to not do that, creating the most linear response possible. The ideal diameter will be found using VE plots shown below and then a small factor of safety of maybe 1.2-1.15 will be multiplied. The current one also has a factor of safety but the factor is way too much, which has lead to lots of issues getting adequate throttle response

Case 6: 1.4 inch throttle body

Next, all cases for volumetric efficiency were exported into excel and graphed together. Since both rpm and tb angle is being varied, a trendline was made for each one for easy comparison. The equation of this trendline (depending on the size chosen from ecotron) will be used to make the throttle cam allowing for a linear throttle response if a modification to the et throttle body can be made. The results match up with a theory (except for the 1.25 inch one which had a different number of cases) and show that the smallest throttle body (1 inch) leads to the least amount of the throttle time at full ve for the engine. the 1-inch case tops off at around case 130 out of 169, meaning that 76 percent of throttle would be full power. This is compared to 59% throttle being full throttle for the 1.4-inch throttle body. The restrictor diameter is 0.787 inches so the tb diameter should not go below that. Next, a 0.9-inch throttle body will be tested to see if this improves response even more. Note that the final tb will be slightly bigger than the ideal case as a safety factor will be built in.

0.9 inch throttle body results

Final Choice

In the end prior art as long with wave simulations results helped to get the final choice. A chunk of other teams used a 27mm throttle body and AT Power, which is a popular supplier for teams. They make a throttle body and diffuser which a lot of teams use on their intakes. After the 27mm choice was made (1.06 inches) we contacted AT power about ordering just the throttle body minus the diffuser (since ours is better and it would not work with our intake package). This throttle was really short in size (perfect for our constraints) and had really good tolerances consisting of a shaft less design, which was something we had struggled with in previous years. However, it would have to have been shipped overseas taking around 8 weeks and was very expensive (300 dollars per unit). Another issue was a lack of a screw flange for easily attaching it to the diffuser of the car. This proved to be a deal breaker due to one intake design having the throttle body upside down and another having it stick out in the air. Fabricating a press or squeeze fit adaptor like the intake on the engine has was studied but abandoned. In the end we purchased an AT power 28mm throttle body, containing a screw flange. This throttle body is rather tall so we could modify it’s height if need be. Changing the throttle cam will most likely not be possible, but this is an easy accommodation to make. We could add a cam like design to right where the pedal is as well to get the same results without potentially allowing the cable to escape its housing. 1 throttle body will initially be ordered for dyno testing.
