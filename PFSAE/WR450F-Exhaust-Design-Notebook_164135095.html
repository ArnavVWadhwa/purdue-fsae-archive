Design Elements

  • Routing
    • Due to a single-cylinder powertrain, we don't have to worry about multiple colliding fluids coming out of different exhaust valves and we can avoid problems such as backpressure. The single-cylinder powertrain also allows us to aim for a more simplistic and lighter routing setup. 
  • Muffler Selection 
    • noise test - http://fsaeonline.com/content/Noise%20Test%20Speeds%202015.pdf
    • at idle must not surpass 103 dbc and at 7000 rpm must not surpass 110n dbc.
  • Rudder
  • Exhaust Flange
  • Pipe Length
    • Although we have some freedom with the geometry of the exhaust system due to the one exhaust valve, we aim to optimize the performance of the exhaust system by calculating and designing the best exhaust length to use in the WR450F. 
    • Utilizing the Bell and Smith equations we are able to calculate different pipe lengths.
    • P - Pipe length (ft), A - exhaust open period in degrees, S - Stroke length (in), D - Cylinder bore (in), d - exhaust port diameter (in), B - degree of exhaust opens before BDC, R- Target RPM. 
    • The Bell equation is very helpful for calculating many different pipe lengths at target RPMs, we could use that equation to devise a strategy to have exhaust performs the best at certain RPMs that the car spends the most time in.
    • Or utilizing the Smith equation we can find a Pipe length that works generically well across all RPMs.
    • We believe the best method is to use both equations for Pipe length; however, lean more towards the bell equations estimate for higher RPMs because that's where the car spends the majority of the race. 
    • To maximize flow we are going to coordinate with intake to find the right offset RPM to use for our exhaust equation to maximize flow.
    • We are also going to utilize an equation derived from tabulated data from the Bell equation that gives us a pipe diameter at target RPM.

Muffler - Design

  • Muffler types
    • Reactive
      • use of baffles and reverse flow to reduce noise and gas flow.  The use of too many reactive elements will reduce the flowrate and have a huge loss to performance
      • Absorbative 
      • uses acoustic elements to suppress noise. A lot of Absorbative materials use fiberglass on the outside of the pipe to reduce noise. They also significantly reduce high-frequency noises. 
      • Reactive - Absorbative
      • Combines reactive and absorptive elements into one muffler  

Research from ASHRAE suggests that the best setup is a reactive muffler followed by an Absorbative muffler; however a due to space constraints a Reactive/ Absorbative muffler might be our best option.

Two reactive mufflers in series produce too much "self-noise." 

  • Muffler components 
    • Baffle - utilizes baffles to disrupt the gas flow and reduce noise - ( 38% flowrate compared to a straight pipe)
      • Pros - little patience, easy to produce, reduces a lot of noise 
      • Cons -  Back Pressure, reduction of horsepower due to huge reduction in flow, holes tearing due to heat and water,
    • Straight Through- is a straight pipe with acoustic elements to reduce noise - (92% flowrate compared to a straight pipe) 
      • Pros - Great flow, increase performance, less weight,
      • Cons- loud and probably would be louder than noise limit,  
    • Reverse Flow 
      • Pros - reduces noise well
      • Cons - increases pipe length, heavy, large muffler body, reduces horsepower

Updates on non-steel options:

Titanium is reported to fail after long-term exposure to high temperatures, thermal capabilities are inversely proportional to weldability and formability across grades of titanium. Commercially pure titanium, when in excess of 700°C, loses its strength and corrosion resistance, and becomes prone to cracking. Look farther into: Exhaust XT, Ti-1.2ASN, Ti-1Cu-0.5Nb. It appears that welding titanium to stainless may not be realistically possible. Welding the two metals together results in brittle inter-metallics are formed and the welds are prone to cracking. It's possible to avoid this by using a third metallic filler, however this is closer to braising than welding and creates weak joints with low melting points. Strength testing of the welded joints shows failing at the interface between the filler metal and the parent metals. I do not advise attempting to manufacture an exhaust pipe made from sections of both titanium and stainless steel. (Dissimilar metal joining of stainless steel and titanium using copper as transition metal). "Applications of titanium alloys are often limited to temperatures below 550◦C,e.g.,400◦C for Ti-6Al-4V"(Brice, Samimi)

Exhaust XT: "a low-alloy content titanium alloy suited for automotive and motorcycle exhausts as well as other industrial applications. Weight savings in excess of 40% have been realized by substituting TIMETAL® EXHAUST XT™ for stainless steel in automotive and motorcycle exhaust applications." (TIMETAL). Density of 4.51g/cm^3, and maintains oxidation resistance at high temperatures much better than commercially pure titanium (see picture). Its intermittent service temperature is 540°C, which is far below the necessary 900°C (Matweb). Because it is a patented alloy made by Timet®, it appears there aren't any suppliers readily available.

Ti-1.2ASN: Can be used at temperatures up to 800°C (kobelco). Offers oxidation resistance up to 700-800°C (titek). Does not meet our requirements for thermal capabilities.

Ti-1Cu-0.5Nb: Excellent strength characteristics up to 800°C, however quickly loses oxidation resistance above ~700°C (see picture). 10CSSN was developed to be superior to Ti-1Cu-0.5Nb.

https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/pdf/10.1002/9781119296126.ch307


10CSSN (Ti-1Cu-1Sn-0.35Si-0.2Nb): Does not appear to have ever been tested at temperatures exceeding 800°C. Information for performance above 800° is not available. However, in the images (above), it can be seen that at 800°C, the performance of 10CSSN is not significantly better than that of Ti-1 Cu-0.5 Nb, which is not a suitable material.


Titanium and Titanium alloys conclusion: We should not use titanium nor any of its alloys to manufacture the PF-22 exhaust system. We will have temperatures at the header up to 900°C, and there is not a titanium alloy that maintains satisfactory oxidation resistance at temperatures that high. For this reason, these alloys are typically commercially used in mufflers, where the exhaust temperatures are lower. Titanium and titanium alloys tend to become brittle as they oxidize, which leads to cracking and pitting, while austenitic stainless steels (such as 310) actually form an oxide layer that protects the metal from further oxidation to an extent. It is not until temperatures exceed 900°C that this oxide is detrimental to the material's performance, as it forms CrO3, which is an acidic and volatile compound (Simms). Should we chose to use a titanium or titanium alloy, aside from having working and manufacturing problems, and a price much higher that of stainless steel, we run the risk of the material oxidizing and degrading to the point of failure, especially closer to the exhaust port. Should that happen during testing, we would at least need to replace the exhaust system, and should it happen during competition, we would almost certainly fail the noise test. The only real advantage of a titanium alloy over stainless steel is weight reduction, however that is worthless if the material fails. 310 stainless steel remains our best option for manufacturing an exhaust system. Because we also have not tested the engine on the dyno, we do not know for sure how hot the exhaust header may actually get, however based on the values that I have found through research, I believe that we should aim for an intermittent service temperature of over 900 degrees. That is a condition that 321 ss does not satisfy, and 310 does.

Nickel-Iron-Chromium alloy INCOLOY 800HT alloy is designed to resist high-temperature oxidation and has a higher tensile strength at high temperatures than 310 stainless steel (103 mPa  at 950C vs 50mPa at 950C). Similar density and melting point to 310 stainless, and retains weldability and formability, not requiring any special equipment. TIG welding is possible, and the alloy is prone to work-hardening (sandmeyer steel resource has a lot of good information). Once a pipe diameter and design is finalized the choice will likely come down to cost and availability of the materials.

Muffler Conclusion

Our best option for a muffler is the Lexx MXe. It's within our budget, much lighter than stock exhaust, has an adjustable noise level, claims to increase horsepower by up to 4, and is all around the best option. Most other options are either way too expensive or too loud. The Lexx muffler also has a 304 stainless midpipe so it can easily be welded to the exhaust pipe.


Additional Exhaust Silencing Research

After concerns arose regarding the silencing capabilities of a slip-on muffler, I'm looking deeper into muffler theories and exhaust silencing. It looks like the safest option for a functional exhaust silencing system is going to come from something along the lines of two slip-on mufflers in-line on the exhaust pipe. Mufflers can be divided into two categories: reactive and dissipative. Reactive mufflers reflect the pressure waves so that they interact with each other and cancel out. These include things like resonators. They "are essentially sound filters and are most useful when the noise source to reduce contains pure tones at fixed frequencies or when there is a hot, dirty, high-speed gas flow" (Kumar). Dissipative mufflers are chambers with ducts or perforations that are filled with sound-deadening materials. They tend to be best for higher frequencies, however, can degrade if the exhaust flow is particularly hot, corrosive, or dirty. These include things like glass packs. The safest choice seems to be something along the lines of an exhaust pipe with some kind of inline silencer like a resonator or glass pack style muffler, then additional piping to a muffler such as the Lexx MXe at the end. Since it's not possible to know exactly how exhaust setups will perform on our specific setup, we will need to experiment with the engine and see how they all perform.
