Created by Fava, Nicola Bernardo, last modified on Aug 07, 2022

Project Scope

  • Maintain proven manufacturing practices to create geometrically accurate and structurally sound aerodynamic devices
  • Improve overall package finish
  • Be cognizant of mounting locations and devices for ease of component access and to minimize aerodynamic losses
  • Optimize layup schedule using simulations, corroborate with classical lamination theory and hand calculations
  • Document and validate through physical tests​
  • Continue to reach out to sponsors to subsidize costs and look for lower alternatives

Stakeholders

    • Powertrain
      • Rules compliant with necessary parts (firewall, engine cover, catch cans, etc.)
    • Chassis
      • Rules compliant with necessary parts (body panels, floor pan, etc.)
      • Lightweight, robust, ergonomic seat
      • Additional parts (steering wheel, footholds, etc.)
    • SIQ
      • Sensor mounting and positioning
    • Suspension
      • Mounting locations for body panel sizing

Design Requirements

  • Leading edge seam flange leftovers ~<.05"
  • Trailing edge straightness ~+- 3 degrees 
  • Maintain positional accuracy to 1/16" for all components in all dimensions
  • Keep all wings aligned to within 1 degree of designed angle (keep wings parallel to each other/perpendicular to endplates)
  • Max allowable deflection 0.17in
  • Max allowable strain of 0.75%. Strain targets are set based on a safety factor of 2, taken from NASA-STD-5001. This accounts for analysis error and manufacturing defects.  
  • Design and manufacture structurally sound parts to maintain driver safety

Manufacturing

  • Carbon fiber is highly process dependent
    • Material properties and quality of components varies widely
    • Layup type
    • Fiber/resin used
    • Operator skill
    • Mold quality
  • Primary concern:
    • Need to match desired shape
  • Secondary concerns:
    • Easy/fast to machine
    • Defined edges to part
    • “Bonus area” for backing ply overhang
    • Few large vertical surfaces
  • Lay plies onto mold
    • Number determined by structural requirements
    • Mold holds plies in desired shape before curing
    • Lay backing plies onto carbon
    • Enclose mold in bag, compresses plies onto surface
    • Apply vacuum
    • Introduce resin, cure

Analysis

FEA

Hyperworks

  • Preprocessing tool
  • Create laminates (ply direction, thickness, etc)
  • Create mesh to export to ANSYS (allows for easy parameterization of mesh sizing)
  • Integrated composite stress tool box
  • Perform classical lamination theory calcs

CFD

  • CFD pressure exports also require a surface. As pressure acts on an area, you must give STAR a pressure surface to export rather than a volume.
  • Pressure loads will be mapped using star-ccm
  • Pressure loads saved as a .csv file to be brought into ansys as APDL commands.

Ansys

Overview

  • Postprocessing tool
  • Use shell meshes
    • Constant thickness, single surface parts which makes shell meshes ideal from a solution time standpoint.
    • Shell meshes are also required by our CFD pressure export and ACP.
  • Outer surface of the composite part (not the midsection) should be used (similar to manufacturing).

Deflection acts in the same manner as with conventional FEA. For stress and strain, results can be given by layer or ply. The layer option will provide results for the entire laminate (most useful for strain, as it is consistent throughout the laminate thickness), while ply can be used to select a specific ply to obtain results for (useful for stress, as this varies by ply). Classical Lamination Theory

https://wiki.itap.purdue.edu/x/pZmRC

-Objective: The objective of the classical lamination theory is to help us find the material properties of the laminates we use to make the sandwich panel. These properties are the Young’s Modulus (E) and the stiffness (k). Further expansion of the classical lamination theory can help us predict the force (W) a panel can experience before failing.

-Overview

  • Forms stiffness matrix using parameters for each ply in a layup
  • Allows for computation of laminate and per-ply strain from loads applied
  • Used for both simple cases (hand calcs) and for complex loads or geometry (FEA)
  • Typically use strain instead of stress
  • Stress varies per ply, strain is uniform across entire laminate
