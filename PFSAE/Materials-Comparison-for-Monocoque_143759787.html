Purdue Formula SAE : Materials Comparison for Monocoque

Created by Unknown User (mkotur), last modified by Unknown User (napolia) on Apr 24, 2020

Below, the different components of a monocoque are discussed in terms of materials selections. The tables show what varying characteristics of each component material does for the final product and discusses the materials tested to date. 


Core

CharacteristicTrend
MaterialWe only tested aluminum and would generally recommend staying with aluminum because we found that in testing with ANSYS models, that is what will work best, but a switch to Nomex could be done. The thickness and density of the material would likely need to increase compared to the same panel with aluminum core, however.
DensityHigher density (wall thickness) will lead to higher energy absorption for 3-point-bending
Cell Size

Smaller cell size is stronger and keeps carbon skin flat. Large cell size will have the carbon dip into it and loses crushing strength (perimeter shear).

Large Cell Size (left) vs Small Cell Size (right)

ThicknessWe found 1-inch core to be too thick and not deflect enough for aluminum honeycomb. ½-inch core has proven to be much better, but if I different material is used that is less stiff than aluminum, then a thicker core may be necessary. Look into changing density first, though.
Materials TestedComments
5052 Aluminum, 3/8" cell size, 1/2" thick,
5052 Aluminum, 1/8" cell size, 1" thick, 98 kg/m^3
5052 Aluminum, 1/8" cell size, 1/2" thick, 98 kg/m^3
5052 Aluminum, 1/8" cell size, 1/2" thick, 160 kg/m^3Used only in ANSYS model (still needs to be tested). This core passed SES (with reasonable layup schedules, first to do so.)


Carbon Fiber

CharacteristicTrend
PrepregUse prepreg carbon for structural carbon because the resin/matrix ratio is set and optimized. If other methods are used to layup the carbon
Woven vs UnidirectionalWoven fabric passes rules; unidirectional fabric may not due to the current SES rule (as of 2020). These rules have schedule bias weighted by weight of the carbon, so unidirectional skews the bias a lot and must be used sparingly. Use it to strengthen the 0 degree direction, but this will only pass rules if there are sufficient woven plies as well (around 10, but this is easy to check in section F.4.3 of the SES document for the specific carbon being used).
Young's ModulusA lower Young’s Modulus is likely preferable because the panel will deflect more, but in SES, we have always had the most trouble passing the buckling modulus of the composite chassis to the tube chassis, which is dependent on the Young's Modulus of the panel an the geometry of the cross-section. For this reason, you still need to be mindful of having a high enough Young’s Modulus, because it is better to change that than to have to change the chassis geometry (which would mostly come in the form of thickening the core).
Ultimate Tensile Strength (UTS)A higher UTS is better, and it is more important to have a high UTS than a high Young’s Modulus.
DensityIt is better to have a lower density carbon, because that will lower overall weight, but this should be the last priority for characteristics on this list. In addition, if there is a sponsor providing carbon, then you often do not get the choice of density if you are specifying other characteristics as well. 
Materials TestedComments
Grafil TR_50S (Unidirectional Prepreg)For layups, you need to be careful handling this, it is much more fragile than the woven prepreg and can get damaged easily when taking the protective coating off.
T300 2X2 Twill 3K (Woven Prepreg Carbon Fiber)It's much easier to work with woven carbon. NOTE (for all woven carbon): it is easier to stretch a 45 degree ply without damaging the carbon than a 0 degrees ply (useful to know this for wrapping carbon around edges and curved surfaces).
T700 2x2 Twill (Woven Prepreg Carbon Fiber)Used on in ANSYS models (still needs to be tested). This has the same Young's Modulus as T300, but a higher UTS. We theoretically passed SES with this carbon).


Inserts

CharacteristicsTrends
Young's ModulusThe Young's Modulus of the insert should be as close to the value of the Young's Modulus of the panel. This is to avoid a large discontinuity in E from the panel to the insert. The larger the jump, the higher the stress concentration at the point, and the more likely the panel will break at that point.
Tensile Yield StrengthThe tensile yield strength should be higher than the interlaminar shear strength of the panel. This means that the panel will fail before the insert. This situation is ideal because the insert remaining intact could help protect the part that is attached to it if the panel fails. In addition, the inserts need to be able to withstand a higher force than the panel in places like the roll hoop connections to the monocoque.
DensityThis value should be the lowest possible to save weight, but Young's Modulus and Tensile Yield Strength are more important.
Operating TemperatureCheck the cure temperature of the carbon, it should be higher than the operating temperature of the insert material or the material will melt in the oven.
ManufacturabilityYou would think that this one would be obvious, but it is important to check. We believed that the G-10 plastic would be cut well by the waterjet. This proved to be false. For new materials, double check.
Materials TestedComments
G-10 Plastic

This is what happened when we cut the G-10 on the waterjet. It started to delaminate. This material cuts best with diamond tooling. Do not use this material unless we gain access to diamond tooling. This is however, the industry standard for inserts.

Molds

CharacteristicTrend
Coefficient of thermal expansion (material)This value for the mold should be as close as possible to that of the carbon, otherwise there will be warping of the part as it cures and portions of it may become out of tolerance. The best mold material would carbon fiber with a higher cure temperature or fiber glass (also with a higher cure temperature). Metal can also be used, but this is harder to model because, while making the mold, you need to account for expansion and shrinkage. Fiber glass is most used.
Cure temperature and melting temperatureBoth should be much higher than the cure temperature of the carbon fiber being used.
