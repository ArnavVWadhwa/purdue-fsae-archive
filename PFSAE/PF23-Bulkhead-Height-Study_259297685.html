Purdue Formula SAE : PF23 Bulkhead Height Study

Created by Zhang, Chenkai on May 08, 2023

Goal:

Get 10 comp points with raised bulkhead.

Geometry sets:

Baseline Geometry:

The baseline geometry utilized PF22 bulkhead and nosecone.

FW-5-8 was used for the base geometry. It was a front wing that gained around 15-18 Cl*A points compared to PF22 front wing. And it was designed to clean up flow to the undertray.

PF22 rear wing was used in the base geometry. Due to the fact that baseline geometry had a good aero balance, rear wing optimization was not needed.

Matt LaMear’s first iteration of full undertray was used. In conjunction with FW-5-8 and PF22 rear wing, this undertray resulted in the best overall performance we saw with PF22 bulkhead.

Radiators were moved back to the location where the catch cans currently sit. This radiator position ensured good mass flow both in yaw 0 and yaw 15 case.

Figure 1 Baseline Geometry Set

Bulkhead key changes:

Bulkhead was raised by 3 inches, and side length was reduced by 1 in. PF22 bulkhead dimensions were 13.5 in x 13.5 in, and the new bulkhead dimensions are 14.5 in x 14.5 in. A new nosecone was designed to fit the current impact attenuator, and a temporary chassis respecting the current chassis “bulge” on the side was modeled to connect the new bulkhead and the front roll hoop. The figure below shows the difference between the new bulkhead and nosecone combination vs. that of PF22’s.

Figure 2: Bulkhead Comparison

Front Wing:

The front wing designed for the raised bulkhead (CZ-6-2) features an additional main element flap which makes use of the extra space provided by the raised bulkhead. The new flap has a reduced angle of attack and a reduced chord length to ensure flow attachment despite a pressure buildup under the nosecone. The inner endplate was also cut. Due to the fact that the lowest flap now spans the entire front wing, a much smaller endplate could be used to reduce blockage and provide better performance.

Figure 3: Front Wing CZ-6-2

Rear Wing:

PF22 rear wing was the primary wing used during for the bulkhead study, though some less-successful rear wings were also tested. PF22 rear wing still provides the best rear wing performance. A four element rear wing (four elements in sequence) is being tested to improve UT-RW interaction.

Undertray and Side Wings:

Due to the flow field change caused by the bulkhead and subsequent front wing modifications, undertray needs to be re-designed. Several small and large undertrays were tested, with the small undertrays providing the best overall performance. Smaller undertrays did negatively impact rear wing on earlier iterations.

This iteration of front wing added strakes to contain vortex breakdown. This gained a significant amount of front wing performance. The front wing performance gain could be sacrificed to improve UT performance, as one of iterations has already shown.

This iteration also ran with outdated UT since it was queued before Chenkai left for Michigan June Competition. It has been proven by other simulations that ML-ES-4 and ML-ES-10 gained significant performance in term of both undertray and rear wing, to the point where an iteration with FW-CZ-6-10, ML-ES-9, and RW-PF22 could be the highest downforce iteration to date.

While L/D gain in this iteration could not be ignored. Comparing to an iteration with FW-CZ-6-2, ML-ES-2, and RW-PF22, this iteration gained 7 points in L/D, proving this front wing design could be the path to higher efficiency and more competition points gain.

Future Plans:

Current issues:

  1. Aero balance. Due to significant FW downforce gains, the aero balance has shifted too far forward.
  2. While large downforce gains resulted from the bulkhead change, we also saw large drag increases that diminished competition points gained.

Keep iterating on FW-CZ-6-10 to sacrifice FW performance for UT performance gains to achieve a more balanced aero package.

We believe a 4-element rear wing has potential in terms of both UT and rear wing performance. Since the undertrays we ran are all short undertrays, this interaction between rear wing and undertray makes sense. RW-CZ-3-1, a 4-element design, ran with FW-CZ-6-2 and ML-ES-2, gained 24 points of UT performance. Although RW-CZ-3-3, another 4-elment design with similar performance, only gained 6 points. This effect needs to be investigated further and better utilized to extract the maximum out of undertray.

Matt LaMear is still iterating on undertray designs. He has found solutions to small undertray upwash issues. With further development, we could see significant UT and RW performance gains.
