Background:

This year, the team decided to go back to a 4-cylinder platform, meaning the intake must be redesigned again from the PF22 platform. The PF20 intake will be used as a baseline, but the core shape of things will change for structural, tuning, and packaging reasons. The final design is down the road, but the general shape for the plenum that I am shooting for looks like what is pictured below. The diffuser will follow off the plenum with the exit diffuser diameter depending on the packaging and the final plenum volume. The throttle body will be COTS once again with a 28mm baseline (since lots of teams use this size). Runner length is significantly longer than before due to the nature of a 4-cylinder. We will most likely select two or 4 RPMs to tune for. This will make the physical design easier since we will not be fighting to make all four runners the same length (making plenum design easier). We can either make the runners symmetrical (selecting two RPMS) or offset the plenum to one side making all four runners different lengths. The latter of the two will be great for overall packaging since it creates an "s bend" intake but will be hard for runner packaging. Runner tuning is much more coupled to exhaust runner tuning than we thought before so we can go with any of these configurations for runner tuning. I have no idea which one will be the best, but luckily we have WAVE at our disposal to test all of these to find the best package. The goal is to have the intake sizing done on paper before any CAD is done to keep the smoothest design timeline possible avoiding overdesign, analysis paralysis, and focusing on stuff that does not matter (getting too down in the weeds). This will be critical because we know a ton from PF22, but we need to make sure we have the fundamentals for a 4-cylinder intake before we design the "fun stuff". A flow chart has been provided below detailing the chronological steps I want for the design. If you see anything wrong or that needs to be added please let me know. 

What do we want: The best functioning and looking 4-cylinder intake design at competition, validation for everything on the intake down to the fillets on the plenum (structure, packaging, assembly, airflow, etc.). Hand calcs for as much intake design as possible (mainly structures), avoiding garbage in garbage out simulations, analysis paralysis

What do we have right now: WAVE, previous PF20 intake, design lessons learned from PF22.

What do we need to get there: 

CAD doesn't necessarily have to wait. Once we get a general sense of size with runner calcs, plenum research we need to start by laying out different packages in the car to Spaceclaim space and to see which one offers the most modularity and looks the best. This will be an iterative and a guess and check process. Work I did with PF22 should help (did this for a year straight). This should be low fidelity until the glasses "clear up" with more sizing data.

Throttle Body Overview:

For sizing a throttle body, one needs to look at mass flow and the effective area that the air sees as the throttle plate opens. A driver wants the easiest throttle pedal to use (i.e. one where you get max power at max throttle). If it is sized too big, max throttle will come at 40-50% throttle (PF20) meaning any input past this point will do nothing since the cylinders are already getting the maximum mass flow they need. m_dot = rho*V*A, so one needs to find the m_dot needed at redline and then solve for Area, giving a rough estimate of how large of a TB you need. If it is sized too small your engine will not be getting the flow it needs for max performance at high RPM ranges, reducing torque and creating a power falloff at high rev ranges. 

We had a great throttle body from last year that can be reused if the math checks out (EcoTron 28mm Cable TB). The company was slightly sketchy, with the throttle body potentially off the market. We will be sticking with a drive-by cable throttle body. Another good company is AT power which is what a bunch of teams use. It comes with a diffuser that we will NOT be using, so we would have to take it apart. The TB is short meaning it packages well, but we would have to build a press-fit piece with a flange on it to fixture it to the diffuser we make.

Intake Diffuser/Venturi:

Especially true for the R6, which redlines at 16k RPM is the fact that the airflow will choke at an RPM through the restrictor (~ 10000-11000 RPM). This means that airflow through the restrictor hits Mach 1 and will not exceed this valve. m_dot from here on out is limited since the air is now flowing faster at which information (pressure) can move upstream of the flow. There will be a small normal shock in the diverging section of the diffuser which shocks the flow back down to the subsonic. We actually want this because this means that the intake is operating at its maximum conditions. Past this point torque will stop increasing because airflow cannot increase to match increasing fuel and RPM. Fun fact, this is how rocket nozzles work past the choke point, the flow will speed up until it hits the normal shock. Rocket thrust (ISP) is basically how fast fuel and exhaust is being thrown out of the end of the engine and with Newton's third law, causes the rocket to rise.

The goal is to keep using old restrictors from PF20/22 since they are such a pain to make. If need be we can use Xometry and make a GDT drawing calling out a really tight profile tolerance (I have experience with this). Like what is said on flow chart we can do CFD on different configurations after the plenum is locked down, but a ton of work and papers have been published on this so this is a low priority. Will be made from Accura 60, unlike rest of the intake so that inside can be sanded smooth, reducing drag. If diverge angle to the plenum is large, we can leave the bottom part of the diffuser with a rough surface finish (or maybe whiffle it) to give a golf ball effect. The effect of this is small but helps with the transient filling time of the plenum.

Plenum:

Plenum sizing is the greyest part of the intake, as there exists no good equation or paper detailing it. The best course of action is to guess and check and use what prior teams have done as a starting point. The airflow is being minimized by the restrictor, thus the larger the plenum the better, giving the cylinders a reserve of air to pull from without having to "wheeze" through the diffuser restriction. The limit to this is packaging and also throttle response. If you have a massive plenum and floor the engine, it takes a lot of time for the larger amount of air inertia to get moving meaning your time to redline increases with plenum volume. This can be corrected with good engine tuning as it was with PF22. PF22 had a plenum 9.5x engine displacement which is massive. This flattened the torque curve up to redline due to air reserve but needed tuning to get the response time down. 

Intake / Exhaust Runner Selection Using WAVE:

There exist hand calcs out there for runner length. These fall into two categories: Helmholtz and ram theory. Helmholtz is what makes bottles play a note when you blow over them. The geometry (neck, volume, area) filters out all frequencies except for one which is what you hear. The sound comes from the oscillatory motion of air coming in and out of the neck. For a car if we can time this pressure wave of air flowing in and out with the intake valves opening and closing (corresponding to an engine RPM), the boom we have a little pressure wave allowing for more air the ambient into the cylinder. Thus the nickname it gets is inertial supercharging. We have a really good spreadsheet that can calculate this from PF20, which has been validated with physical testing.

Good CFD video visualizing the effect:

Match this frequency up with the calculated intake valve opening frequency f(RPM)

The other published phenomenon at play is the ram charging theory, first developed by Chrysler in the 50s states that when the airflow reaches the closed intake valve its pressure increases (stagnation point) causing a gradient to form leading to the air racing back up the runner until it reaches the stationary air in the plenum. Here the same thing happens and the air changes course once again, ending up at the valve where the cycle repeats. The pressure wave loses energy for each cycle due to losses so the goal is to make the runner length such that when the air comes back to the valve at the end of the first cycle, the valve opens letting the air into the engine. The values at work here are the speed of sound of the air, the cross-section area, and the length of the runner. The way I like to visualize this is with a ball or a spring bouncing back and forth. When it hits the ground, velocity goes to zero, and pressure increases to a maximum, pushing the ball back up.

These both are good, but which one is the more dominant effect at play. The answer is.... tbc. It turns out that sizing for the intake and the exhaust runners are coupled, i.e. the phenomena in one affect the other one. What makes this possible comes when looking at the CAM profile of the engine. During the exhaust stroke, as the piston comes up the intake valve will be open. Thus before intake air starts being sucked into the engine, a small amount of exhaust air gets shot up the runner. The question still is, how much effect does this have over the intake air going in, because generally, the overlap is small. The mass flow of air is also halved since both valves are open at the same time. See the image below. There will be a time period as the piston comes up during the exhaust stroke, where both the intake and exhaust valves are open...


Typical Valve Profile. One can see that there are about 45 degrees of rotation where the intake valve is opening and the cylinder is still going upward

Goal: Get a list of optimal runner configurations that we can pull from when doing config testing. The ideal case is we plug in values from this matrix to test intake/exhaust runner combinations (see image above)

After some more discussion, the design plan for using WAVE will be as follows and will follow a factorial method. First, a list of four intake runners will be selected a four different RPMs. Ideally, these runners will have the most harmonics available, so the most RPMs can be modeled. However, the primary driving force in selecting these is the packaging. With PF22 we found that the shortest runner we can do is 5 inches, and with some research, the longest we can do is 10-12 inches. Therefore the first list of 4 is all between 5-11 inches. Having the primary selecting force being packaging prevents a loop from forming as we will adjust diameter down the road, we will come back to this step since the runner area affects tuning RPM. The way that this work is a length will have a primary RPM that it works for, but also secondary ones. The primary will have the pressure wave come up the runner and back just once, whereas secondary and tertiary ones will have the wave come up and back two and three times respectively. If a length is selected that packages with the most harmonics, then the runner has the most modularity and efficiency at the most points. 

After this list is selected, we will validate these hand calcs in wave and tweak these values based on the values we get for length and RPM. Next will sweep the exhaust length to get a list of runner length for each runner length. Next will be the intake and then the exhaust diameter. The order of these are set by hypothesised effect on volumetric efficiency; we want the ones that will have the greatest change first. Going this factorial route will hopefully give us the maximums. If this was a 2d optimization (it isn't), optimizing one variable and then optimizing the next would give us a rough value for maximums for a function that isn't defined numerically, like what we could do on the plot below. I also would like to explore the newton raphson method for this as well (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_method), because we will have lots of test to do. Thus, we want to eliminate paths that won't give us the ideal value at all costs to reduce time.

To select the range of RPMS to tune for, the choke flow RPM of the intake needed to be found. At higher RPMs, the mass flow required by the engine will exceed the choke flow rate going through the restrictor. We shouldn't tune the intake runners past choke flow because m_dot will stop increasing meaning their effect won't be seen. Intake volumetric flow can be easily found with RPM; the engine will need x amount of air volume per minute based on RPM and the cam duration. M_dot choked is an easy isentropic flow calc. Discharge coefficient of the diffuser is assumed to be 0.9 (it is shaped and sized like a venturi) and VE of the engine at the choke flow RPM is 0.925 (assumed). This math depending on cd and VE gets a choke flow RPM of 11500 - 12500 RPM, which was expected. From this, a matrix of runner lengths vs RPM vs harmonic number was found and plotted. A list of lengths that had the most harmonics and packaged in the car was chosen. See below.

VE = 2 * RPM * cylinderVolume - in in3/minute since there are two intake strokes per revolution

We also want to see if looking at values like runner and exhaust diameter is even worth it. Modifying the ports will be time-consuming, difficult, and impossible to reverse if we get it wrong (costing us a ~1k dollar engine). Therefore, if we go that route, we want to ensure that it greatly affects efficiency. If it doesn't, then it isn't worth it. Reducing the diameter increases the velocity, but the mass flow stays constant, so while VFR increases, m_dot does not, which really matters for engine performance. Therefore, before going head-on into this test matrix, we will run a small pathfinder test on one runner length. We will take an intake and exhaust length and vary the diameters (two factorial runs at a constant rpm). We will then look at the % change in VE with varying the diameter. If it's lower than 5%, then my vote is that it is not worth the cost and time to do the math and the modifications.

Notes from Scientific Design of Exhaust & Intake System

Chapter 1 - Basics

The point of the exhaust is to guide the out flow in an efficient way. Optimally, there should be no exhaust gas present in the cylinder after the exhaust stroke. 

Both volume and temperature of remaining exhaust gasses hurt performance.

Overlapping timing is used to help achieve this. Fresh air passes through both the intake and exhaust valves.

If the exhaust manifold is designed poorly, high pressure from one cylinder's freshly opened valve could impact the last phase of an adjacent cylinder's exhaust stroke:

Chapter 2 - Simple Problems

The volume of the exhaust manifold should not be too small. This would cause pressure to be built up too easily.

Design exhaust to absorb high pressure events when valves are opened and allow smooth flow.

Average pressure in the exhaust system matters, BUT the most important thing is exhaust port pressure near the end of the exhaust stroke. We want maximum 'scavenging.'

Basic fluid dynamics must be considered. Boundary layers, major and minor losses (ME308).

The exhaust can be tuned to utilize pressure waves and time the low pressure to arrive at the port at the end of the exhaust stroke, increasing scavenging.

For multi-cylinder engines, this effect is smaller, but more opportunities for gains are possible if the exhaust kinetic energy from one cylinder can be used to amplify the low pressure at another.

Notes From Investigation of Intake Concepts for a Formula SAE FourCylinder Engine Using 1D/3D (Ricardo WAVE-VECTIS) Coupled Modeling Techniques:

This paper offered tons of good information on transient intake design. It explores the three most common intake designs: Top mounted (PF20), asymmetrical side mounted, and conical spline intake (what we want to do). It first looked at overall VE and runner length tuning. An important takeaway is that there is a greater impact on VE with runner length tuning at lower RPMs vs higher ones. This is most likely due to intake choking that occurs at higher RPMs. Another good thing with tuning is that there is no noticeable impact when the conical spline runners are twisted or left straight. These WILL have to be twisted to make it into the cylinders. Finally VE for all four cylinders is compared, with the conical spline intake coming out way on top. The asymmetric one is a mess as predicted.

The next thing explored was with choking in the intake diffuser. This even at redline RPMs does not happen constantly, only at certain crank angles where the intake is pulling air in. The frequency/% of time the intake is choked will increase as RPM goes up since overall VFR increases with RPM. 

Intake choking has an impact on the pressure loss in the diffuser since the normal shock causes flow seperation, these results are shown below. The drop in total pressure at the diffuser exit part of the plot is due to the normal shock created.

The plots show the difference in diffuser behavior at different crank angles even at 14,000 rpm. This leads to highly unsteady flow. If we want to take a stab at this in ansys we need the pressure and m_dot BCs at different crank angles to look at each of these snapshots of the flow.
