Purdue Formula SAE : Steering Assembly Notebook

Requirements: 

  • Meet steering assembly rules (FSAE_Rules_2021_DRAFT_for_comment.pdf)
  • Meet factor of safety requirement of 1.4
  • <,=0.5 degrees of play (Allowable steering system free play is limited to 7 degrees total)
  • The top of the steering wheel can not be higher than the front roll hoop (at any angular position)
  • The steering wheel must be attached to the column with a quick disconnect
  • All moving parts in the car must be covered for safety (steering rack)
  • The steering system must have positive steering stops that prevent the system from locking up
  • The driver must be able to take off the steering wheel while seated in the car


Things to consider:

How repairable is the system

How durable is the system (especially steering column support)

Due to taller drivers, there will be a larger force downwards on the wheel

IIT alternatives analysis

"A note in passing: One of the tests we sometimes conduct during engineering design judging is to lift the front of the car off the ground, and check for steering wheel effort. The best cars will be able to twirl the steering wheel from lock to lock with one little finger…" - Cockpit Control Forces


"Most FSAE/FS cars have the steering wheel positioned 300mm to 450mm (12” to 18”) away from the driver’s chest (mid-span of your total forward reach). It is reasonable to conclude that any well constructed FSAE/FS car should be able to withstand a minimum of 100-135 Nm (75-100 ft.lb.) of torque/force applied at the steering wheel with no steering system failure." - Cockpit Control Forces

"Steering system lateral (radial) forces are relatively small when the car is on the track, running at speed. However, that does not relieve you of the responsibility to mount your steering wheel & column in a workmanship like fashion. Any steering wheel/column should be able to withstand at least a 660 N (150 lb.) lateral (radial) force without failing." - Cockpit Control Forces

"Steering System Torque – The steering system should be able to withstand a minimum of 100 Nm (75 ft-lb) force, applied at the steering wheel, before failure" - Cockpit Control Forces

Initial Design Review:

Presentation - Steering Assembly.pdf

Takeaways:

  • Gearbox geometry doesn't make sense with the current set up, would be more complex, would likely be much heavier
  • Single U joint and double U joint should both be able to get us to the steering wheel perpendicular as desired

Version 1 of Reference Geometry using a Double U-Joint:

In this first version, we were able to get the steering wheel at 0º with each joint of the Double U-Joint operating at around 33º.

The process we took to complete this is as follows: 

  • We created the line segments for the rack a pinion standoff, the lower column, the mid-segment of the Double U-Joint, and the upper segment of the column.
  • We then set the upper segment to 6in which is roughly as long as the upper segment on the previous years' designs which held the quick release and bracing for the column.
  • We then set the mid-segment of the Double U-Joint to a constant value.
  • We also both angles representing the ends of the U-Joints equal to each other.
  • We were then able to find the resultant angles for the U-Joints which were both under the max operating angle for both U-Joint Brands.

We are currently working on deciding how long the top steering column needs to be.
