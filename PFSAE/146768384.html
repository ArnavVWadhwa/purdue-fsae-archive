Stress Elements

At the critical cross-section, you can look one step further and find the critical element. This is local on that critical cross-section that will fail first because it is under the most stress (if that stress is above the amount that the material can withstand, which will be discussed later in Failure Theories). To determine the critical element, different kinds of stresses can be looked at on the cross-section, and, similar to how we found the critical cross-section from the shear and bending moment diagrams, we can find the areas of maximum stress on the cut face. The stress element can be seen as a cube with very small side length. In a plane stress situation, the stress element can be seen as a square.

Combined Stresses

In general, any cross-section of the beam will have both shear and bending acting on it.  This results in a general stress element as shown, where

Failure is most likely to occur on a cross-section where V or M are maximum.  On the cross-section failure due to pure bending may occur at the top or bottom of the cross-section, and due to pure shear may occur at the neutral axis.  Wide-flange or other non-uniform cross-sections may have principal stresses or maximum shearing stresses at the web-flange intersection or other points of change in cross section width that exceed other stresses on the cross-section.[1]

To get the complete stress element, all the kinds of stresses applied to the beam need to be evaluated at the cross-section, then the value of each at a point should be added to get the combined stresses. For example, if there is an compressive axial force and a bending force, the part of the beam in flexural tension (positive axial force result) would be added to the compressive axial force being applies (negative axial force) to get the combined stress there.
