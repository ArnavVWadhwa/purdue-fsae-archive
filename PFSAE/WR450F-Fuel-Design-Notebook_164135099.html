Week 2 (11/29-12/5)

Fuel Tank Preliminary Design

The fuel tank will be located behind the driver, mounted to the bottom of the chassis. This helps provide a lower center of gravity. The central location also prevents changes in weight distribution as fuel is consumed.

An initial design sketch was created. This initial design is a simple trapezoidal shape, formed by only 2 bent cuts of 1/16 inch aluminum. We will need to check if the desired bent sheets can be made using the brake.

A hole in the top will be cut for the installation of two baffles. The baffles prevent fuel slosh, while leaving room for the fuel mat underneath. Holes will be needed for two lines and the filler neck.


The filler neck will be made of thin aluminum tube and will be routed later to be as short and simple as possible. A sight tube will be used. This saves weight and meets the requirements easier compared to a clear filler neck tube. 


Note on the HydraMat: although some of the bottom of the tank is not covered by the HydraMat, we can be confident that fuel pressure will not be lost. The factor of safety for the fuel tank size means that the fuel will not be low enough so as to not be in contact with the mat. The mat does not lose functionality even if it is in partial contact with the fuel. 

https://fuelsafe.com/holley-hydramat/#:~:text=Since%20the%20area%20of%20the,filter%20before%20the%20fuel%20pump.

"Surface tension and fluid wicking allow the HydraMat to continue to draw fuel from the tank or cell even with part of the mat uncovered"

We will still look into modifying the shape to allow gravity to pull the fuel towards the mat. Loss of fuel pressure is a critical fault.


CAD for the fuel tank, including baffles, Hydramat, cutout for installation, and holes for the filler neck and two lines was completed.

Feedback from Dom: Aluminum welding sucks.

How to improve design for manufacturing: Make welded sections overlap. It is much easier to weld an overlapped joint than just end-to-end.


The concept of an "adjustable volume fuel tank" was mentioned, but was deemed too complex and heavy to further investigate at this time.

Updated Fuel Usage Table

19% of 2019 teams use 1 cylinder, yet 6 of the top 10 positions were won by 1 cylinder teams. The majority of top 15 were also one cylinder. 

This table gives information on the top 1 cylinder teams, plus the lowest rank 1 cylinder team to complete all events plus 11 laps of endurance. The addition of this team shows that low ranked 1 cylinder aren't using a bunch more fuel.

2019 Fuel Usage of 1 Cylinder Teams (With Other Info)

Average fuel usage from table: 0.939 gallons. Average fuel usage of E85 from table: 1.178 gallons. Average fuel usage of 93 octane from table: 0.832 gallons.

These results show that if the team wants to use E85 fuel, a larger tank is needed. If not, the current fuel tank sizing will suffice, although it could be adjusted to 1.1 gallons to be extra safe. If E85 usage is planned, the tank will need to be a max size of 1.5 gallons. According to Carson, a good rough estimate for E85 usage is about 30% more. 


E85 Research

The top 4 ranked teams used E85 in 2019. Approximately 17% of teams use E85 (from 76 teams with efficiency event results). The percentage is probably less, since the teams who don't have efficiency event results likely don't use E85.

Considering all teams regardless of their engine usage, the table below shows average fuel usage per fuel type.


                                                                                                                                             


When researching the possibility of E85, a thesis, aptly named Utilizing the Thermodynamic Properties of E85 to Increase the Specific Efficiency of a High Specific Output Single Cylinder Formula SAE engine was found. The paper is fairly recent being from 2014, and is a good beginning resource to utilize in the early research. The engine used for his tests was the CRF450X, which, albeit a bit older, is comparable to the 450F. One notable difference was that the CRF450X didn't come with EFI until 2019, so it can be assumed that the one used was converted by OSU's team.                         The paper can be found here ->               DuncanDerekN2014.pdf

Because of ethanol's different stoic rating than gasoline, any flow-related parts have to be able to flow about 30% more fuel. This includes, but is not limited to:

  • Fuel pump
  • Fuel injectors
  • TUNE

Some things such as the fuel pressure regulator, lines, and the pump wiring are technically optional when transitioning to E85; their viability can be assessed based on later decisions. 


Fuel Tank Design Update

Some Updates to the fuel tank were made:

First, a sloped base on the fuel tank allows gravity to pull fuel towards HydraMat at low levels. This ensures fuel pressure is not lost.


Second, the design was adjusted so that all welds are made on overlapping joints. This makes the welding easier, decreasing the occurrence of leaks as well.



Third, the filler neck is currently a work in progress and will be updated more as more CAD is completed. Tentatively, the plan is for an angled aluminum tube to come from the tank, leading to a near vertical tube along with the sight tube.

Part Selection

Walbro GSL392 Inline Fuel Pump. This is the only inline pump Walbro offers. It provides flow that exceeds the needs of our system, even at pressures above 100 psi (See graph). One thing to keep in mind is the current draw of the pump, shown by the dotted line. 

AEM 25-302bk Fuel Pressure Regulator. This FPR provides a consistent pressure differential across the fuel injector, is a rubber hose is installed connecting the regulator to the intake. Adjustable, providing 20 psi at lowest setting, to max pump pressure at highest setting.

AEM 30-2130-100 Pressure Sensor. This sensor measures the fuel rail gauge pressure. This means pressure readings should be consistent for differences in atmospheric pressure. The readings from this sensor should fluctuate, as the regulator adjusts the fuel pressure depending on the pressure inside the intake. 

The Fuel line size will be AN6. Although our system does not require more flow than the AN4 lines provide, the GSL392 pump provides fuel flow that would be very restricted by AN4. AN6 lines and fittings are also much more common.

Week 5 (12/20-26)

Further Fuel Pump Selection Research

  • In-tank vs in-line
    • In-line makes more sense considering that it's easier to service, and allows the use of the Hydramat.
    • The main issue with external pumps is they are way oversized for our application.
    • In-tank pumps would require a different tank design. Collection 'reservoir' at bottom of tank, fuel foam, and no fuel mat. Also would have to figure out a mounting solution that is leak-proof even when serviced. 
    • In-tank has good potential though, and should be strongly considered in the next design iteration. Low power draw, light weight, small, and requires less lines/fittings

At last week's meeting, a target value for current draw was set at 3 amps in order to give a little more wiggle room to run electronics. Therefore, the Walbro GSL 392 was thrown out in favor of the 393 from the same line. While it still doesn't reach the target of 3 amps for most psi, it does 2+ amps better than the previous selection, and uses less current than any other inline pump with sufficient pressure. If this current draw is still too much, we will be forced to switch to the in-tank pump style.

                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   

               


After searching through motorcycle fuel pumps with limited success (which seems mostly due to their preference for in-tank fuel pumps), a new pump with a lot of potential came up. The GSL414. Originally made as a direct replacement for a Cannondale 440cc ATV, it's the only pump we found that can support the desired pressure with a low current draw, maybe even under 4 amps. The only issue so far would be that it's made from aluminum, which doesn't play super well with E85. 

                         

Additionally, the graph above is at 13.5v. This means that the flow would likely take a slight hit (which is irrelevant, since our application requires max 6 or 7 gph) along with the power draw, which is good anyway.

Week 11 (1/31-2/6)

Most stuff found in CDR.

Fuel Flow Calculation

In order to validate the fuel flow needed, we calculated the flow that would replace an injector shot at 11,000 rpm with 8ms pulse width at 3 bar. From the 16M-XX-1500-X injector table, each shot at 8ms releases .1813 cc. At 11,000 rpm, there are 5,500 shots/minute (singe cylinder 4 stroke). This means that there is .01091 seconds between each shot. We now know that .1813cc of fuel must be replenished every .01091 seconds, or 16.62 cc every second. Converting to better units, that is 15.805 gal/hr. The GSL414 has 28 gal/hr fuel flow at 3 bar. This gives a safety factor of 1.77 for the fuel replenishment.

Filler Neck Calculation

the volume held by the large angled section of the neck is .14 gallons. Every inch, measured from the base, of fuel added to the section with the sight tube adds .013 gallons. At max capacity, the total fuel volume would be 1.34 gallons. This gives alot of more flexibility. Using average fuel of 1 cylinders (including e85 teams) from week 3 in this notebook, max capacity yields a 1.43 safety factor. 1.34 gallons is also the largest amount of fuel any 93 octane car used during endurance.

Pressure Loss Calculation (AN-4 vs AN-6)

A MATLAB script was created to calculate the pressure drop from the pump to the fuel pressure regulator. It is in 2021>powertrain>fuel. Hopefully it is clear what the inputs are and how to adjust them for the situation. I compared an-4 and an-6 with our current setup and got an over 90% reduction in pressure drop. An-4 had a ~12 psi reduction and an-6 had a ~1 psi reduction.
