Failure Theories

There are two main failure theories that we work with at this point (in mechanics of materials and for our applications). They are the distortion energy method and maximum shear stress theory. These theories are for ductile materials.[3]

Maximum Shear Stress Theory states that failure occurs when the maximum shear stress from a combination of principal stresses equals or exceeds the value obtained for the shear stress at yielding in a uniaxial tensile test.[17]

Distortion Energy Method is also known as von Mises – Hencky Theory says that a given material has a definite limited capacity to absorb energy of distortion and that any attempt to subject the material to greater amounts of distortion energy result in yielding failure.[17]

 

Distortion does not include hydro-static volume changes; so, some volume dV of a material expands or contracts equally in all direction, this is not included in distortion.

 

Distortion energy theory states that failure by yielding under a combination of stresses occurs when the energy of distortion equals or exceeds the energy of distortion in the tensile test when the yield strength is reached.

Factor of Safety

We use these criteria to determine a factor of safety to design our part to. This factor of safety is reflects the uncertainty we have in our calculations, material, use cases, etc. For example, a airplane often has a factor of safety of 1.5 because it is well engineered and we know a lot about the conditions it will be in. A screwdriver, on the other hand, often has a factor of 18 because they are not always used as they are meant to be used, and we still don't want them to break.
