TIRE NOTES:

  1. The tire is the most important component as it is the only link to earth and gives the driver their only feedback
    1. All other components and systems are there to link the tire to the driver and ensure the maximum use of the tire
  1. TRULY THE ONLY WAY TO INCREASE PERFORMANCE: Understand and integrate the tire with suspension
    1. Improvements: This is why tires need to be maximized
      1. Down force -> limited
      2. Engine power -> limited
      3. Weight reduction -> Pegged by regulations
  1. Friction coefficients: Differ along lateral and longitudinal axis
    1. Fx = mux*Fz
    2. Fy = muy*Fz
    3. The average of the 4 tires' coeff of friction is equal to the lateral acceleration in g's
  1. Forces on the tire
    1. Weight
    2. Lateral G
    3. Longitudinal G (braking/accelerating)
    4. Vertical Aero (downforce)
    5. Vertical G
    6. Longitudinal Aero (drag)
    7. Transversal Aero

    1. Tire Stiffness and Deformation (refer to tires presentation slides 46 to 54 for diagrams)
      1. Vertical deformation due to vertical load
      2. Vertical stiffness varies with inclination angle (INCREASES with pressure, DECREASES with inclination angle)
      3. Vertical deformation due to lateral deformation
      4. Torsional deformation
      5. Tire can be modeled by a spring
      6. Influence of tire stiffness on car
        1. Without suspension it is known the tire has its own vertical/lateral/longitudinal/torsional flexibility and dampening that will influence the car response due to road roughness and forces on the car
        2. Ex: lateral rigidity and dampening of all four tires influence frequency in yaw/ tire response time to steering/ lateral stiffness
      1. Weight transfer on lateral deformation (analysis tools for this slide 57 and 58 for longitudinal)
        1. Causes track to decrease as contact patch changes with roll and deformation

  1. Tire Construction
    1. Radial and Cross-ply Tires
      1. Advantages of radial tire:
        1. Life span (higher mileage)
        2. Greater load capacity (heavy vehicles)
        3. Lower rolling resistance
        4. Better aquaplaning properties
        5. Better wet braking behavior
        6. Transferable, greater lateral forces for a given tire pressure
        7. Greater ride comfort at high speeds
      1. Diagonal tire uses:
        1. Temporary spare
        2. Motorcycles (wheel inclination against lateral forces)
        3. Some race cars (lower moment of inertia)
        4. Agricultural vehicles (slow speeds)
      1. Radial tire description:
        1. Have plies at zenith angle of approx 90deg with stiff belts wrapped radially around the tire (work together to provide structural integrity)
          1. Note: cross ply relies solely on the plies for structural integrity
        1. Primary function of plies is to hold in air and support belt
        2. Casing is designed to flex laterally and allow the belt to provide pressure distribution across the contact patch
  1. Rim Design
    1. Minimum weight as un-sprung mass has a major role in handling
    2. Air tightness as they are tubeless
    3. Brake heat transfer to aid in cooling and ventilation (not an issue with a car yet but good to look into and analyze)
    4. Secure connection with hub flange
  1. Slip Angle
    1. Angle between the tires expected direction and real direction
    2. Derived from the deformation of the contact patch
    4. Calculating Slip angle
      1. Alpha = arctan(lateral speed/ longitudinal speed)
    1. Lateral Force vs Slip angle plot
      1. 3 regions
        1. Linear (elastic) Fy = C*alpha
        2. Translational
        3. Frictional
      1. Slope of linear region is N/deg and the cornering stiffness
      1. Function of Pressure, Camber, Vertical Load, Tire Construction
  1. Slip Ratio
    1. Ratio of distances traveled by external surface of tire and wheel center
    2. Tires tangential speed and vehicle longitudinal speed can be different
      1. Wheel Spin
      2. Movement of contact patch vs rim
      3. Longitudinal deformation of contact patch (hysteresis**CHECK IF TRUE)
    1. SR = (wheel speed - angular speed*tire loaded radius) / wheel speed
    2. Typically develop peak force at slip ratio of 8%
  1. Vertical Load
    1. Spring Forces (doesn't account for ARB and Damper)
      1. Spring displacement -> spring stiffness -> spring force -> Spring/wheel MR -> Spring force @ wheel
    1. ARB Forces
    1. Damper Forces
      1. Damper Speed -> coulomb friction and viscous damping constant -> Damper force -> damper/wheel MR -> Damper force @ wheel
    1. Load sensitivity!!! And varying lateral and longitudinal loads with vertical load
  1. Inclination Angle
    1. Used over camber angle as the tire sign convention is independent of a vehicle sign convention
    2. Camber Definition
      2. Varies withâ€¦
        1. Roll
        2. Heave
        3. Steering
      1. Why Camber:
        1. Negative camber must be added with radial tires to compensate for lateral flexing
        2. Evenly distribute the load with an optimized contact patch
        3. Note: Too much negative camber is bad as in a straight line all the load is carried by the inside shoulder
          1. Leads to extreme tire wear
          2. May fail the tire
          3. USE Tread temperatures to correct camber
            1. A large spread between inside and outside shoulders >15C is an indication that failure may occur
  1. Pressure
    1. Changes print size and affects force vs angle/ratio plots


  1. Conicity and Ply Steer (slides 102-104)
    1. Side Forces
      1. Cornering Force (Big)
      2. Camber Thrust (Medium)
      3. Cross Wind
      4. Road Condition (Camber, Bank, unevenness)
      5. Minor residual forces (small)
        1. Hysteresis
        2. Conicity
        3. Ply Steer
    2. Conicity and Ply steer cause lateral force and self-aligning moment to have non-zero values when straight ahead on a level road (zero slip angle)
      1. Conicity is determined by offset of center lines of belts away from longitudinal centerline of entire tire
      2. Ply steer relies on stiffness symmetry with respect to circumferential midplane
  1. Steering Geometry (LOOK AT TECH TIP EXAMPLE)
    1. As load changes the slip angle @ Max Fy varies
      1. Ex: As Fz increases the alpha @ max Fy increases (outside tire needs more alpha to produce max force)
      2. Need of anti-ackerman
      3. Opposite is also true but is pro-ackerman
    1. Ensure steering geometry compliments tire characteristics but also need to consider grip is extremely sensitive to temperature and the steering needs to enable the tire to work at its optimal operating temperature


















  1. TIRE MODELS: SAE J670e
    1. Analysis of tire is needed to achieve optimal performance
      1. Can help understand car behavior, and guide setup and design changes
      2. Can be used to coach driver to maximize car performance based on their feelings
      3. Determine the 3 forces and moments for any steady-state (SS) operating condition
    1. Tire model vs Tire data
      1. Model is easier computationally vs lookup tables
      2. Model better interpolates between data points
      3. Tire data has noise and differentiation will amplify this noise
      4. Tire data has hysteresis present
    1. IMPORTANT to know the models limitations that you are using
      1. Don't include effects of temperature and grip is very sensitive to temperature
      2. Cannot solely predict performance of car
        1. Need an accurate VD model to ensure the tire model is maximized
      1. Essential to VD sims but not the only part
    1. Common used model is 2002 Pacejka
      1. SS model that neglects transient behavior of tire
      2. Approximations of numerically fitted data
      3. Sign convention Adapted SAE


  1. Pure Lateral Model
    1. Simple graph but a lot of information:
      1. How tire feels to driver
      2. Steering geometry requirements
      3. Vertical load sensitivity
      4. Instantaneous cornering stiffness
        1. Stability vs Response characteristics ** LEARN MORE ABOUT THIS* MAY CONNECT TO YAW MOMENT
    1. Convention to use SAE J670e (left turn is positive Force and negative slip angle)
    2. Particular slip angle for max generated lateral grip
    3. Linear region occurs at low slip angles and the slope represents cornering stiffness
      1. Fy = C*alpha
      2. Where road cars operate
    1. Transitional region occurs as graph becomes non-linear as max Fy is approached
      1. Where contact patch starts to experience micro-slippage
      2. Sensitivity of Fy changes in slip angle is less than linear region
    1. Frictional region occurs when max Fy passed
      1. More contact patch slips and Fy = mu_d*Fz
      2. Mu_d is coeff of dynamic friction of tire and road
    1. Lateral force variation with load
      1. Generates more force at higher vertical loads but this is not directly proportional due to load sensitivity
    1. Camber Thrust
      1. Adding an inclination angle causes conicity of the tire producing a lateral force when slip angle is zero (offset )
    1. Be careful of inclination angle
      1. Doesn't only influence at zero slip, produces a full offset so one direction turn may produce max Fy at a larger slip than the other direction (asymmetric)
      2. Has major effects when Fz is greatest
    1. COMPARING TWO TIRES
      1. Observations:
        1. Different slip angles for maximum Fy
        2. Which has higher cornering stiffness
        3. Sharper drop in performance beyond optimal slip angle?
        4. Ask questions: What is application? How good is driver? How can the car be set up for the driver?
        6. Energy Absorbtion
          1. Higher slip angle operation means more energy absorption due to higher induced drag (proportional to sin(alpha)) and larger alpha means more distortion of rubber
          2. E = integral(Fy*dalpha)
        1. NOTE: INDUCED DRAG may create yaw moment
        2. Operation at higher slip angles causes
          1. Increased induced drag
          2. Increased tire temperature
          3. Increased tire wear
      1. PLOTS:
        1. Normalized Fy vs Slip Angle

Demonstrates load sensitivity as more load is applied the relative gain in Fy decreases; BASIS for steady state setup with regards to anti-roll distribution (Vary anti-roll distribution or TLLTD to move the loss of grip in weight transfer to front or rear for balance of oversteer/understeer)
          2. Constants: Camber, Given Speed, Given Ground, Pressure | Inputs: Various Expected Loads | Output: Tire load sensitivity, Max coeff of friction @ slip angle
        1. Fy vs Slip (varying inclination angles)
          1. Allows for the ideal camber of the car to be set for all points of a corner
          2. Shows that weight transfer causes camber to be more important for outside tire and compromises can be made for inside (maintain full contact patch for heavier tire)
