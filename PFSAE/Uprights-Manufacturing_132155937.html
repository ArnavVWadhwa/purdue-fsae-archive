Purdue Formula SAE : Uprights Manufacturing

Created by Unknown User (dewey2), last modified on Apr 26, 2021

2019 upright machining in the ME shop. Upright bolted to the sac block (fixture) while machining the second operation.

There are six main steps involved in manufacturing the uprights:

  1. Acquire stock
  2. Design fixtures and stock
  3. Prep stock and fixture for CNC
  4. Create Mastercam program and approve with John
  5. CNC mill uprights
  6. Bore out upright bearing pockets
  7. Press in bearings

1. Acquire Stock

Our main supply of aluminum is from Arconic. 7055 aluminum is preferable in our application due to its tensile yield and ultimate strength over 7075 or 7050. Typically we grab large blocks capable of machining 4 uprights from a single block (maybe 10-12 inches length & width, 4 inches thick)

2. Design Fixtures and Stock

Given the stock size obtained from Arconic, the stock can be designed. The stock has a few requirements/suggestions:

  • Stock must encompass the upright
  • Stock must have provisions for mounting locations around the part to secure it while not interfering with toolpaths
  • The upleft and upright stock should have the same outer dimensions for simplicity
  • If mounting locations can be shared, do it!

The process of designing the stock and fixture are described in this video:

The fixture typically extends 0.25" past the stock on all sides as this is a easy number to remember and achieve.

3. Prepare Stock and Fixture for CNC

After the fixture and stock are designed, they must be machined.

Prepping Upright Stock

The stock will typically start as a large block of aluminum in which 4 uprights can be made from. Let's say the dimensions of the block are 10 x 12 x 4 (LxWxH).

The first step will be to cut the block in half (on the bandsaw) through the height such that you have 2 blocks with 2 inches of height. This will ensure that all 4 upright stocks have the relatively same starting thickness to machine from.

The second step will be to cut each half-block in half again (bandsaw) length or width-wise (whichever is more appropriate based on the stock design). Let's assume cutting on the width is appropriate. Now you have 4 blocks of dimensions 10x6x2 which will be squared up and machined down to the exact stock size.

The process of squaring the blocks can be as follows:

  1. Place the stock length-wise into the vise with the bandsaw'd face pointing up.
  2. Face the top surface until the entire surface is flat.
  3. Face the left and right faces (the width) such that they are flat and are to the dimension specified in the drawing
  4. Remove the stock from the vise and re-insert width-wise into the vise with the cold-extruded face pointing up
  5. Face the top surface until the entire surface is flat and the upright thickness matches the drawing
  6. Face the left and right faces (the length) such that they are flat and are to the dimension specified in the drawing
  7. All faces should be faced and the dimensions should match the drawing created from step 2

Finally, the upright and mounting holes can be drilled per the drawing and as discussed in the video.

The fixture stock can follow a similar process, squaring up the stock and drilling and tapping all of the appropriate holes.

5. CNC Mill Uprights

Once John has approved the Mastercam, you should be able to bring in the fixture, stock, and mounting hardware to machine the uprights.

6. Bore out Upright Bearing Pockets

Once CNC machining is completed, the bearing pockets must be bored out to 0.001" under the bearing diameter to press-fit into the uprights. Consult John during this process, as it is easy to mess up and challenging to set up if never done before. Words or a video can't match actual experience for this.

Generally, this is the process:

  1. Mount the upright + fixture into a manual mill vise
  2. Insert a Jacob's chuck with a dial indicator and indicate in the center of the bearing pocket
  3. Remove chuck and dial indicator, insert boring head with boring bar
  4. Set up the automatic Z feed to stop at the appropriate depth
  5. Perform a first pass to just machine all of the bearing pocket to get a clean surface
  6. Measure the diameter of the bearing pocket as well as the bearing which will go into it. Determine how much material must be removed and remove the majority of it
  7. Check the diameter periodically until you reach 0.050" diameter larger than the target diameter (remember: the target diameter is 0.001" less than the bearing diameter)
  8. Use smaller steps from here until the target diameter is achieved (harder than it sounds)

Telescoping gauges + a micrometer are used to measure the upright bearing pocket diameter. A micrometer should be used to measure the bearing diameter.

7. Press in Bearings

Once the upright pocket is bored out, the bearing can be pressed in inside the room directly across from the shop. There is a manual press that can be used, be sure to align the bearing into the pocket and once it begins to go in, ensure it does not tilt significantly off. Once pressed it as far as it will go, the bearing is properly seated in.
