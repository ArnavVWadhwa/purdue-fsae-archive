Overview:

For this project, you will have to design an anti-roll bar (ARB). An ARB is a mechanical mechanism used to change the vehicles characteristics only when in roll. There are various methods to do this, but we will be focusing on 1: a tube in torsion. 

  • Fancy ARB w/ 3rd Spring :O

  • Full suspension system featuring a torsion style ARB (this is the one you'll be designing!!)

  • IndyCar ARB (Twisting blade style) (highlighted in red w/ droplinks in green)


Pre-requisites:

  1. Complete general onboarding
  2. Read Chapter 17 of Race Car Vehicle Dynamics, Milliken and Milliken, Chapter 17, Page 607-661
    • This section seems to do a good job of giving basic understanding of the components of a suspension, terminology, etc. Focus on content regarding SLA suspension.
    • https://drive.google.com/file/d/1beXnd3ZTY7-RCddXpm_gndgWSs6IfhwV/view?usp=sharing
  3. Read through this powerpoint on how ARBs work in the context of our car - Basics of ARB
  4. Watch youtube videos to supplement your knowledge on vehicle cornering behavior and ARBs (in tools below)

Steps / Process:

Step 1: Size your ARB Torsion Tube

  • For the most part, the general aspects of the design are determined for you: It will be a tube in torsion that reacts through an arm on each end. The point of this is to select parameters to hit a design target (50lb/in as per the powerpoint)
  • Reference Material Selection Guide to determine what properties you want
  • Keep in mind you cannot select whatever diameter and wall thickness you want, there are only specific discrete options available, check out this supplier https://www.aedmotorsport.com/, McMaster-Carr , as well as any other tube manufacturer.

Your goal is design an ARB with 5 stiffness settings (10, 20, 30, 40, 50 lb/in - Wheel rate equivalent)

Step 2: Size and Design Droplinks

  • Typical Suspension droplinks (not the actual torsion bar its-self) will use a tube (steel or composite) with an insert on each end. The insert fits into the tube (either welded or bonded) and has inserts that accept a rod end. The point of a rod end is to convey forces (ideally in the direction of the axis of the tube) but it allows for free rotation at either end
  • You can see the ARB Drop Link pictured above in the middle picture: this isn't what our suspension looks like exactly, but it's very close. 

Step 3: CAD

  1. After setting all the parameters of your ARB design, its time to model the components in CAD. Hopefully at this point you have finished your team-wide CAD training and practice. In case you need to reference that again, it is linked below in tools
    1. Take this opportunity to use and follow all of the team CAD practices and habits including
      1. Using a layout file for initial modelling, in the downloaded .zip, look for PF20_Sus_FrontARB_Layout.prt
      2. Saving (using Wavelink) all components to their separate .prt files with appropriate name convention
      3. You will have to model the ARB, ARB arm, Mounting for ARB to chassis, and Droplinks
        1. As you're modelling these components, keep manufacturability in mind. How will you use the basic processes (Mill, Lathe, Waterjet, etc) to manufacture each component?
      4. The necessary hardware for the assembly should be in the .zip file with the environment file
      5. Assemble those saved components into a subsystem .prt file, use proper assembly constraints
      6. If you're confused about these CAD details, be sure to reference some of the NX tutorial pages. Those guides explain how this team uses the software.
    2. The necessary environment file for your assignment can be downloaded here. You should be able to take this package and work on the CAD independently on your computer
      1. https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1LWHvGbizu1iZiLt3O3_8-9MrglLQ169L?usp=sharing

     4. Another detail to think about is how you will manufacture the components you design. As an example of this practice, the final step is to make a drawing of the ARB arm as if you were going to machine it in the shop. Using NX create a multiple-view drawing. See drawing resources below for reference and an example.
