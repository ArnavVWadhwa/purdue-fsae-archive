Workflow: 

  • Kinematics: there's no reason not to switch PF23 onto the newish 16x6-10 tires from our current 18x6-10. 
    • The first design is making a suspension with the exact same output parameters on the tire size. The reason for this is because our kinematics still operate in the linear range hence all we are doing is updating the static offsets. This includes:
      • F+R roll center heights
      • F+R camber gain
      • F toe vs steering curves: ackermann and total steering travel
      • F caster, kingpin inclination, offset: there will be slightly less mechanical trail with these held the same because of the smaller tire. Look at steering force change. We're probably happy with the lower steering force.
      • Bump steer: should be possible to reduce because lower control will be closer to flat (in past, bump steer and need for tierod to rack angle < 5 degrees caused conflict)

TTC Data:

TTC stands for the tire testing consortium. It's tire testing that's organized by the Milliken Research Associate Group (Guys who wrote Racecar Vehicle Dynamics) and done through Calspan. We bought into back in 2005 for $500 and you can register through your Purdue email. They will just verify your Purdue email and you can create an account. 


The way it works is that TTC does rounds of testing on different types of tires consisting of hoosier, good year, etc on different sizes, compounds, etc. This is purely raw data from the calspan testing. 


To post-process this tire data the use of tire models must be incorporated. Highly suggest reading the book Tyre and Vehicle Dynamics by Hans Pacejka. We have a sponsorship with Stackpole Engineering Services (SES) who gives us access to their tire-analyzer software. On the TTC website, there is a section for tire models where you can find their post-processed data that you can just download and use as inputs into the SES tire-analyzer software. This is the simplest way to go about using the data from TTC because they've done all the curve fitting for you and gotten the coefficients for the tire models figured out. If however, SES hasn't gotten around to creating models, going about doing it yourself in MATLAB would be the next best bet. I highly suggest at least going through this process at least once to understand the intricacies of tire modelling. 



Tire Modelling: 

Tire modelling is a very complex process which is why I highly suggest reading Tyre and Vehicle Dynamics and attempting it yourself in Matlab to understand what goes into it. I made a script that takes .dat files from the TTC data and makes into colorful pictures. 

 

There is no single data template that is used by TTC for all the rounds, hence programs to process the raw data have to altered just about every round. 


The Matlab script is broken into parts

  • Fetching the data and breaking up the data into the relevant columns
  • Elimination of unused data
  • Outputting scans of data for each slip, load, condition 
  • Performing curve fitting based on coefficients used
  • Surface fit to get plots
