Comments:

For 2020.10.09 issue, lambda was actually reading lean. this is because lambda sensor measure the combustion products. With the coilpack failing, there was no ignition so uburnt fuel and air were going through the exhaust. This would increase the lambda value (leaner). In general, we do see increase of lambda on throttle in with it getting up to ~1.5. With the coil pack issue, it would get to ~4.0 on slow throttle applications. On fast throttle applications, it would rail (10). This is because the engine would be sensing very lean exhaust, and thus increase fuel input. If it injected so much extra fuel that it fouled the injectors, then more cylinders would not have ignition, making the exhaust appear even leaner (even though there was a bunch of unburnt fuel in it). This creates a positive feedback loop, so power is lost until driver back out of throttle momentarily. Comparing plots of measured lambda vs target air fuel ratio shows that the car struggled to maintain the target ratio far more than usual on that testing day. We weren't logging closed loop fuel trim, but that would have clearly shown that closed loop was doing far more work to hit the target air fuel ratio. The spark plugs were also coated carbon deposit because of the very rich conditions.
