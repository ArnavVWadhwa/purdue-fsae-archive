Purdue Formula SAE : Intake / Exahust Up to Collector DCR

Created by Unknown User (jpoulse), last modified by Horton, Tyler Jason on Jun 21, 2023

Changes Since Last Review

Use knowledge from PF22 to create a more efficient intake, couple intake and exhaust design for 4 cylinder engine

The design will be similar to PF20, but with a circular cross-section plenum, lighter and more efficient exahust

Better structurally designed plenum

Fuel injectors that bolt into runner (like PF22)

Use gasket material instead of RTB or O-rings (can laser, WJ or hand cut this)

Better calculate bolt numbers on flanges, get number for torque (prevent cracking)

COTS throttle body

Restrictor, diffuser that has a higher Cd (like a venturi)

Project Scope

-Design a reliable, efficient intake and exhaust (up to collector) to maximize volumetric efficiency while adding as little weight as possible

-Package components into the smallest area possible to avoid interference with airflow over rear wing

-Optimize key design parameters using simulations, get within 80% of final value using research and hand calculations

-Use reasonable budget and look for lower-cost alternatives

Other Requirements

Packaging: Intake/Exhaust must be able to fit in car and avoid other components. Work with aero sub team to ensure plenum/throttle do not interfere with airflow too much. 

Weight: Not exact figure here, but we cannot make the system too heavy.

Manufacturability: We have to be certain we can obtain materials and manufacture/purchase what we need for our design within normal tolerance. Manufacturability should be on the mind during entire design process

Cost: All costs must be justified from a volumetric efficiency, points, manufacturing, or other perspective.

Design Overview

-Overall design goal: reevaluate all prior research and develop a complete understanding of how to tune and design intake and exhaust well. We might not be able to incorporate everything, but this knowledge is very important for design comp and to pass on to future goons.

TIME TO LEARN HOW TO READ BOIS

-Improve throttle response compared to previous years by better balancing our tuning parameters (diameters, lengths, plenum volume). These all have effects on performance in different RPM ranges and throttle response. We are thinking of moving toward more performance on lower RPM and good throttle response compared to 'putting all our eggs in one basket' by only optimizing peak RPM performance.

-Try to get lighter overall, especially the exhaust system. This will be done by evaluating material options (more on that later) and making the system smaller where possible. 

-Optimize inertial supercharging effects in intake and exhaust to increase VE. Done by using combination of simulation, testing, and hand calcs. Tune intake runner length to set resonance points to provide more power and improve throttle response.

-Test how much exhaust muffler and system as a whole restricts flow. How much of an effect does it have?

-Incorporate a purchased mechanical throttle body. This saves design time and will likely be better quality than in-house. We will use an air filter design (tea filter) similar to last year. Sizing will start at 28mm and we will run tests in WAVE since choke flow calcs won't work without complex Cd valves for the valve

-Simplify the design for second throttle spring by incorporating it directly with the purchased throttle if possible. This design can begin one we have narrowed down our specific throttle.

-The plenum will be circular with the runners departing in a square formation and snaking to their respective cylinders. Intuitively, this provides each runner with the same airflow compared to PF20 design. We also can tune each runner length individually by snaking the tubes (basically infinite freedom here with SLS printing). Results from WAVE will determine the route of this. This allows us to further balance our response instead of being tuned to heavily for one specific RPM. We plan on routing the intake vertically, but sideways routing is still an option if needed.

-One length's have been chosen, plenum will be sized as large as possible while still packaging (similar to PF22). Volume will be smaller than PF22 since it is a 4 cylinder vs. a single cylinder (smaller cylinders)

Inspiration pics:

-The exhaust design is still flexible at this point as more research is being conducted. The main goal of the runners is to increase scavaging of air out of the cylinder. The lower the pressure in the head when the valve opens the bigger the pressure gradient causing more air to flow in the cylinder. Valve overlap helps a ton with this. Exahust might go to a 4-to-1 collector almost like the reverse of our intake. Or, it might incorporate multiple 2-to-1 collectors. Lots of directions we can go with this in terms of material, how much we make in house, how complex/simple. We could even think about 3d printing options if we wanted to. The current plan is a 4-to-1 collector using a purchased collector and a variety of purchased straight sections and bends to weld the shape we want. More on this later.

According to SSDE&IS, and Engine Exhaust System Development & Optimization for FSAE Vehicle, the 4-1 collector is easier to tune for and less complex, but can cause issues with exahust flow flowing back up into another open valve, decreasing scavaging effects. Thus tube lengths should be long if we do a 4-1 to minimize this. A double branch exahust can minimize this if they are paired correctly (based on firing order). 

We also want to look at the effect of varring diameter are on intake and exhaust runner lengths. While this does NOT change mass flow, it changes volumetric flow rate, and a lot of F1 cars do this. We will use WAVE and research to validate doing this. SSDE&IS has a section on it that I am currently reading.

Hand calculations and research will be used to get close to the final value we want for things, with WAVE and Dyno testing getting us the last mile. Dyno testing will include variable plenum sizing and runner lengths. Fortunaltly, this has already been done back in 2021 with 1, 2, and 3 inch spacers made in CAD for the plenum, along with bottom runner pieces that allow tubing to be installed to simulate various runner lengths. We will need to also 3d print the bottom of the PF20 plenum with just nibs instead of runners that have the same shelf for sliding tubing in. All of this can be done on FDM printers or with Xometry on the cheap and quickly. Time for CAD should only be a day or two.

The throttle body will be COTS. Starting testing with a 28mm throttle body as a baseline


The exhaust will be made with COTS bends and straight sections. Still deciding between Grade 3/5 (grade 5 much stronger but harder to come by/more expensive) Titanium and 304 Stainless.

Possible custom bending option: https://russellstubeforming.com/

Stainless

Pros: More availability, cheaper, easier to weld

Cons: Heavy, heavy, not as cool, not as strong

8.0 g/cm3 density

16 GA thickness typical for exhausts (~1.6mm)

Titanium

Pros: Cool sound, lighter, stronger (grade 5 only)

Cons: Harder welding, fewer suppliers

4.4 g/cm3 density

20 GA thickness typical for exhausts (~1.0mm)


Total weight savings assuming going from 16GA to 20GA: 65%

Cost increase would be around 2x to 4x, some specific examples later. SPONSORSHIPS WOULD BE KEY

From a pure weight standpoint, we would save approximately 75 percent of the weight of a Ti exhaust. This should be plugged into the lap time simulator to see how many net points this gets us. We can use the PF20 weight as a baseline to see the approx weight of a Ti exhaust. This should be used to see if this outweighs the cost increase.

Analysis

Hand Calculations:

1) Hoop Stress (for exhaust tubing/intake runner diameter checks)

2) Bolt Joint calcs: used for determining # of bolts needed on 3d print bolted flanges, torque that bolts should be set to, washer area, etc.

3) Choke flow calcs: used for determining choke RPM of engine for finding runner length tune point

4) Helmholtz Calcs: used for both intake and exhaust length sizing. See the spreadsheets attached, same as 2018 intake design report (see confluence page)

5) Ram Charging effects (eqns from Scientific Design of Intake and Exhaust, and from engine airflow test bench guide used)

FEA:

Will be used for intake plenum, may use shell elements depending on geometry complexity
