Correcting understeer/oversteer with LLTD

Many factors can be tweaked to correct for understeer and oversteer. Changing relative spring rates of the suspension via tire pressures, tire compounds, shock absorbers and Anti-Roll Bars (ARBs) is one method. Another is to change the toe of the front or rear tires. In the Mercedes video above, Andrew Shovlin discusses some of the factors they tweak. A skilled driver can sometimes account for this via adjusting their racing line or by varying the application of the throttle/brake.

The main tool in our arsenal to correct for understeer and oversteer is adjusting the ARBs. By adjusting the ARBs, we can finely control the spring rates of the front and rear suspension systems. For example, if we are to stiffen the front ARBs, we can adjust the effective front spring rate all the way from 350 lb/in to 400 lb/in. This results in an increase in the Front Load Transfer.

When the bias of the load transfer distribution shifts forward (LLTD > 50%), the front outside wheel is loaded more. As shown by Figure (5) in Tune to Win, if the front outside tire is loaded more, it will produce a larger lateral force. This increase in lateral force aids in cornering, therefore correcting understeer. The same principle works in reverse for correcting oversteer.

Where Chassis Fits Into This

Our suspension calculations and models assume the frame is rigid. This is conceptualized by the front wheels connected to the front suspension, then the front suspension connected to rear suspension and subsequent wheels.
