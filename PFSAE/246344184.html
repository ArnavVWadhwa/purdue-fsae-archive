Purdue Formula SAE : 2. Pneumatics Hand Calculations & Component Selection

Design Requirements

Force Requirements

Force requirements for this were decided by physical testing with a crane scale. The crane scale was pulled on until the point at which the clutch was disengaged, or the next gear was selected.

Stroke Requirements

Stroke requirements were determined by recording video of the shifter and clutch actuation, and calling out the point at which the clutch was disengaged - or the next gear was selected. The moment arm was measured using calipers and the angular displacement was estimated from the video.

Margins of Safety

Force and stroke margins of safety were originally set to 100% based on sections 3.1.1.3 and 3.1.1.4 of NASA's Space Flight System Design and Environmental Test Standard, respectively (can be found here: https://www.nasa.gov/sites/default/files/atoms/files/std8070.1.pdf). This was the case until a visit from an engineer who works with pneumatic systems noted that this NASA standard explicitly includes "electromechanical motors and solenoids, phase-change and state-change actuators, and spring-energized devices, or mechanisms driven by these devices", not necessarily pneumatic systems. Because of this, a 40% force margin of safety was applied to the clutch lever, which has not caused any problems with engagement on the engine dyno.

Component Selection

Piston Selection

Piston selection was done using the spreadsheet at the end of this section. One important thing to note is the use of surface areas instead of bore diameters. Depending on how a cylinder needs to be packaged, and what it's being used for, the cylinder may use one of three modes. The first, known as single acting, is shown in the left image below. This is the cylinder extending. The second, known as reverse single acting, is shown in the right image below - this is pulling / retraction. The third is double acting, where the cylinder is set up to extend and retract.

Someone designing a pneumatic system needs to keep in mind the usable surface area when their cylinder actuates. Bimba provides a wide selection of round line actuators, and generates detailed CAD here (https://www.bimba.com/en/detail/original_line_cylinder). This is useful for finding the usable surface area as shown below, as well as for general modeling.

To use the spreadsheet, simply input your force requirements, measured moment arm, actuation angle, and margins of safety. The output cells are the minimum areas for a given set of requirements.

Solenoid Selection

Solenoids were initially selected as carryover from Alex Krueger's ventures into pneumatics during the 2021-2022 season. His book of knowledge was a good starting point for me, and can be found here: Pneumatics (and Cost). The only change was the addition of an Airtac 3V1-06 Solenoid to replace one of the two 4V110-06's on the clutch side. It was discovered in preliminary dyno testing that the 4V110-06 needed pressure to be applied in order to actuate. For the solenoid controlling the exhaust from the clutch cylinder (e.g. slow vs fast clutch), there is no buildup of pressure, so the 4V110-06 is not suitable. Instead, a 3V1-06 was selected for further testing.


Fitting Selection

SMC's push-to-connect fittings were originally used for Alex Krueger's test bench, but guidance from a contact who works with pneumatic systems in harsh vibration environments led to the change to compression tube fittings (https://www.hylokusa.com/hy-lok-tube-fittings/). These should perform better under the harsh track and engine vibration conditions, and can be visually checked by hysol staking the joint after leak checking, prior to any testing session.
