Definitions:

  • Factor of safety (FS): a factor used to account for uncertainty and load exceedances that are not known or predicted.
  • Fitting factor: a factor used to account for known uncertainty in a given type of analysis or manufacturing. For example, it is known that a weld has more uncertainty than a machined part. Rather than using multiple factors of safety, a fitting factor is applied for welds.
  • Margin of safety  / Safety Margin (MS): calculation below. Shows that design meets criteria if greater or equal than 0.
  • Limit load: the maximum expected load for the car.
  • Ultimate load /design ultimate load: limit load times the ultimate safety factor. For an ultimate MS of 0, failure is expected at this loading.
  • Yield load /design yield load: limit load times the yield safety factory. For a yield MS of 0, yield is expected at this loading.
  • Allowable: the part strength. Can be material property or loading. Examples:
    • bearing ultimate allowable
    • pressure allowable

Safety Factors

Safety factors are used exclusively to address uncertainty that is unknown. For calculations that are known to be suspect, highly sensitive, or dependent on inputs with high variance, an additional fitting factor should be applied. These values hold regardless of component classification.

Yield1.1Typical with 1.4 (1)
Bolted Joint Separation1.1NASA STD 5020

Composite Uniform Area1.5NASA STD 5001
Composite discontinuity Area (i.e bonds, bolted joints, etc.)2.0NASA STD 5001 

Fitting Factors

Fitting factors are for addressing known additional uncertainty. These are usually based on experience with a given type of calculations. For certain tools that are used team-wide, these are specified below. They may also be created for more analysis done on a subteam.

Analysis TypeFitting FactorSources
Bolted Joints1.15NASA-STD-5020
Lug calculations (not including weld)1.15Airframe Stress Analysis and Sizing
Lug weld calculationsN/A60% Nominal knockdown on weld strength capability to account for process control uncertainties

Notes:

  • Margins are being used because they work for all kinds of analyses while safety factors only work for linear analyses. Also some textbooks provide calculations for margin of safety. And it is easier to convert a factor of safety to a margin of safety than it is to do the reverse.
  • Stress could be replaced by any other kind off allowable. For example, allowable pressure and predicted pressure for fluids line.
  • FS is factor of safety. Any fitting factors are also multiplied in the denominator
  • Example: for an analysis of shear failure of a bolt, which is an ultimate failure:
    • "It holds a 0.08 margin with a 1.4 safety factor and 1.15 fitting factor on shear ultimate failure"
  • Margins can be expressed as decimal numbers or percentages. Above could be an 0.08 margin of safety or an 8% margin of safety.
  • For our purposes a positive margin of safety will always indicate that the part is of acceptable strength; however, in some textbooks, fitting factors are not used and there are instead requirements for margin of safety greater than 0.
  • Margin of safety may be abbreviated MS.
