Purdue Formula SAE : 1. Statics Basics

Created by Unknown User (napolia), last modified on Apr 28, 2020

We will briefly review some statics concepts that are necessary for mechanics of materials. First, we will look at whole systems and how to find the forces on one part of that system. Then, we will find how to determine the internal forces of that singular part.

Supports and Reactions

When looking at any member in a system, it is important to know how it interacts with its surrounds, i.e. how it is supported. Below is a list of common supports and reactions. Each kind of support allows or stops different directions of motion. 

[13]

The image below shows the reaction forces for each kind of support shown above. Any direction that there is not a reaction force in, the beam is free to move. For the roller, for example, there is a reaction force in the y-direction (let's assume that the y-direction is up-down and the x-direction is left-right). This means that the beam cannot move in the y-direction at the point where it touches the roller. It can move in the x-direction, however, and it can also rotate about the roller. 

These reaction forces are can be positive, negative, or zero. They are considered external forces, which will be examined in the next sub-section.

External Forces

Simple trusses are the easiest structure to investigate because we assume that:

  • Truss members are connected together at their ends only.
  • Trusses are connected together by frictionless pins. (ADD ANOTHER PAGE ON CONNECTIONS AND SUPPORTS. LINK HERE)
  • The truss structure is loaded only at the joints.
  • The weights of the members may be neglected. [5]

The basic building block of a truss is a triangle. You can see why in the illustration below. The rectangle truss is able to move because the joints are frictionless (they do not restrict motion in the shown plane). A triangular truss would not move in this way unless the material fails (breaks). [3][5]


The members (bars/parts) of the truss are two force members. This is because we assume that all applied forces occur at joints. When two or more forces are applied to the same point, we can use vector math to find the resultant vector. (See https://www.mathsisfun.com/algebra/vectors.html, https://www.mathalino.com/reviewer/engineering-mechanics/components-of-a-force for more a review of vector math).


We have the sign convention that positive is outward, away from the member and negative is inward,  into the member. Once the vector math is done, the truss member will be in compression (negative), tension (positive), or have no force acting on it.


Two-force members will ALWAYS have the forces acting on them be equal and opposite and in line with the joints. This is to satisfy the fact that they are stationary and in static equilibrium.


Curved members are still two force members if they only have forces acting on their joints. Remember that the two forces acting along a two-force member are along the line connecting the two points on which the loads are applied. [3][6]



You can use the Method of Joints to determine the forces acting on each of the members in the structure. This method uses the free-body diagram of joints in the structure to determine the forces on each member. Remember, the forces are equal and opposite from the member to the joint, and the joints are all in static equilibrium. We can look at the example below to see this. [3][5]

Internal Forces

Looking at determining internal loads, we know that:

  1. In any given body/part, you can make a 'cut' at any location. Doing this allows you to determine the forces acting on either cut face
  2. For determining whether a part will fail due to static loading (which is what we will look at in this review), we assume the part is in static equilibrium, i.e. all forces sum to zero.
  3. With this knowledge, we know that each half of the of the cut body must have forces that sum to zero. We can then calculate that the internal forces are.
  4.  force applied over an area is a stress. The internal force applied over the area of the cut faces gives you the internal stress on that cut face.
