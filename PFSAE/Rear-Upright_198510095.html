Purdue Formula SAE : Rear Upright

High Level

  1. Stock Prep
  2. Drill Tierod and LCA holes
  3. OP1 - CNC Mill for Inboard Side
  4. OP2 - CNC Mill for Onboard Side
  5. OP3 - CNC Mill for Inboard Side
  6. Bore out Bearing Holes (After Anodize)


1. Stock Prep

Use manual mill to prep stock. Thickness should be to the dimension of the part. Leave ~ 0.150" to 0.200" in X and Y directions.

2. Drill Tierod and LCA holes

First import the .stp file in Mastercam and place the upright in space correctly. Once done, this should always be in the same place relative to the origin. And each op should use the same 2 sides for XY datum to prevent mismatch between operations.

Use the measure tool in Mastercam to create a drawing for the location of those 2 holes. Pay attention to the depth of hole as that is likely critical as well. Setup block in a manual mill and drill. As with the other ops, make sure the edge find on the correct datums.


3. OP1 - CNC Mill for Inboard Side

This operation will have at minimum (you can do more pockets if you want)

  • Bearing Hole and through-bore between bearings
  • Pocket for UCA/Camber Piece Mount
  • Contour around the UCA mount to make room for surface parallel.
  • Surface Parallel Rough and Finish for that same region

4. OP2 - CNC Mill for Onboard Side

This cannot be stressed enough: When flipping for another operation, ensure that the part has not moved relative to origin in CAM, and use the same sides for the origin.

This should be one of the relatively longer programs. There are a lot of pockets to machine. Basically use your discretion, but anything that can only be accessed/should be accessed from this side needs to be machined in this operation. The upright wont be machined again on this side (aside from bearing bore out).

  • For pockets that go all the way through: You can pocket half way from each side, but that risks a line in the middle due to mismatch. You could try to pocket all the way from one side, but that requires a long tool stick out, and risks large tool deflections.



5. OP3 - CNC Mill for Inboard Side

This operation is going to require a fixture plate. The base has nothing to fixture onto with a regular vise, so a fixture will hold the upright by the center bearing bore and the UCA bolt holes. You'll need a spacer under the UCA bolt holes.

The center bolt is a 1/2-13 and the UCA bolts are 10-24. The center cap is recessed in the upright and the cap has a counter bore to recess the bolt head. This ensures nothing is protruding so you don't have to worry about the tools traveling over and crashing.


6. Bore out Bearing Holes (After Anodize)

Lightly toe clamp the upright to the mill table. Use the test indicator to find the center of the hole. Make sure to use the middle bore (not the surface the bearing actually sits in, but the feature between the 2 bearings). Using that face for boring both sides is more likely to create concentric bearing holes.

Use a boring bar and telescoping gauge to start taking passes.

Some notes to not mess this up:

  • Keep a chart of sizes as you step up. this will give you an idea of the boring bar behavior and make your passes more predictable.
  • Use the same micrometer for bearing and gauge measurements.
  • Sanity check with calipers if you aren't confident in your micrometer readings
  • For a ~2-3 in OD bearing, we want roughly a 0.002-0.003" interference when we press, ask John about this to confirm
  • Use a burr whip to slightly chamfer the edge before attempting to press in.



Other Notes

  • The easiest way in my opinion is to do Left and Right hand parts in a parallel process. Machine Left Upright OP1 first, then mirror the CAM for the Right Upright.
  • Leave 0.100 in diameter for the bearing hole in CAD. It is possible to do this modification in CAM, but that is more susceptible to error and forgetfulness.
  • Always export the .stl file to represent stock for the following operations. You should always have stock display for something this complex. This helps prevent errors and crashes.
  • Often times there are operations where blocks or chunks of the stock will come off. You need to be very careful of these as they could fly off, get caught in things, or break endmills. Be sure John is with you next to the machine if you are at all unsure.
  • The most critical detail of the fixture block is that it holds the upright flat and level in the Z axis. It doesn't matter of the part isn't perfectly square to the X and Y axis as the machine touch-off method can account for rotation about the Z axis.
