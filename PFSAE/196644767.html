Purdue Formula SAE : 4. Selecting Ignition Coilpacks (Dwell Calculator)

Created by Unknown User (dnocon), last modified on Dec 09, 2021

Modern engines controlled by computers use ignition coilpacks to send a high burst of energy to the sparkplug. This is commonly referred to as High Energy Ignition or (HEI). 

Older engines with mechanically timed ignition utilize a coil with mechanical system known as a rotor / points / distributor to send power to sparkplugs. This is commonly referred to as Capacitor Discharge Ignition or (CDI).

For all intensive proposes FSAE will use electronically controlled ignition and therefore utilize HEI coil packs to send power.  There are 2 types of coilpacks: 'dumb coils' and 'smart coils.' 

  • Dumb Coils: Do not contain an ignitor or a power transistor unit (PTU). Requires an External Driver.
  • Smart Coils: Contain both the ignitor and PTU in the same package.

Dwell time is the backbone to inductive ignition systems. It is crucial to understand dwell in order to fully understand how coilpacks work, and how to select the right coil.

Dwell time is the time the coil is "on" or "charging up."

This charging period is important to consider. If you are in high rpm's the exceed the limit of the coilpack, the coil will not have enough time to charge before firing. In addition, a reduction in supply voltage will cause a corresponding reduction in coil output. 

This can lead to melted components and possibly a ruptured coil casing.

Overview on Dumb Coils

Dumb coil's are exactly as the sound - dumb. They can not think for themselves and will require an external driver to control the closed loop dwell. The easiest way to determine if a coilpack is a 'dumb coil' is if it only has 2 wires going to it. If you do not control dumb coils with a closed loop ignition module, you will need to fully calibrate the dwell in your ecu. See the attached PDF on Dwell Calibration if you are interested. The amount of bench testing required is out of the time and scope of the team. 

We used dumb coils on the Yamaha R6 platform: PF15, PF16, PF17, PF18, PF19, PF20

PF20 Setup: 

This was the ignition module used. It is only rated for a max of 8000 rpm. PF20's redline was 12,500 rpm. Negligence in reading data sheets caused us to use this module out of it's operating range. Luckily the module was still able to work up to our redline rpm, at a detriment to coilpack life. WE SHOULD NEVER USE PARTS THAT EXCEED DATA SHEET SPECIFICATIONS.


Ignoring the RPM spec, this module was the brain behind the OEM Yamaha R6 Coilpack. The module has a PTU which prevents damage to the ECU against voltage/current during the coil discharge. The external driver also controls the closed loop dwell. The driver will monitor the coil current and adjust the dwell to ensure the target amperage is met, this accounts for for RPM, temperature, voltage, coil tolerances etc.


Overview on Smart Coils

On the other hand, smart coils can think for themselves. In short, it is a dumb coil and external driver paired together into a single package. The easiest way to determine if a coilpack is a 'smart coil' is if it has 4 wires going to it. We have chosen to run a smart coil on PF22 for the WR450F single cylinder in order to stop using the bosch closed loop ignition module that is out of spec.

Connecting the Coil to Sparkplug

Coil-On-Plug

  •  Coil-On-Plug is easy. The coil sits right on top of the sparkplug. 
  • Example: The Yamaha R6 Sparkplug: NGK CR10EK has a "Terminal Type: threaded stud terminal." The threaded stud terminal is common among motorcycle and powersports applications. The coil pack doesn't actually thread into the stud but instead grabs onto the stud. 



Remote Mount 

  • When the ignition coil is remote mounted, the ignition coil will need to connect to the sparkplug via a sparkplug wire. The sparkplug wire needs to connect to the coil side, as well as the sparkplug side. The coil side is not standardized so your sparkplug wire will need to match the coil. The sparkplug side should be standardized to "Terminal Type: Solid." However, as mentioned earlier, motorcycle and powersports engines sparkplugs do not come with a solid terminal. Luckily, you can buy spark plug terminal nuts to adapt the plug to the plug wire. See: https://www.ngk.com/spark-plug-terminal-types
  • The sparkplug wire will carry high power signals to the sparkplug. No part of the harness should come close to the sparkplug wire, as the electromagnetic interference will cause interference in the nearby wires. 
    • This was tested in Fall of 2021 with the CAM position wire getting significant interference, enough to lose engine synchronization.
