Design Goals

  • Exhaust Sound below 110 dBc at all speeds 
  • Exhaust noise below 103 dBc at idle
  • Minimal flow and scavenging loss. 

Research: 

The total sound field radiated by an engine is divided into two categories. The first one is the noise component that is linearly related to the combustion process. The combustion process can be best represented by cylinder pressure signals since cylinder pressure signals do not include contributions from the various mechanical parts of an engine. Thus, combustion-related noise is defined here as the noise component that is correlated with cylinder pressure signals. The second category corresponds to the remaining noise (i.e., the noise component that is not correlated with cylinder pressure signals). This noise component is considered to be radiated primarily by mechanical sources. 

Velocity of sound waves:  v = √(B/ρ) , B = Bulk Modulus.

Testing Ideas: 

Speaker Muffler Test

Concept is to use a speaker with a wide frequency range and loud enough amplitude to mimic exhaust noise and develop a set up to test differing muffler internal geometry. 


Pros: Cheap testing, multiple muffler configurations, fast testing, able to test wide frequency range, be saves to test annually. 

Cons: Testing will not capture thermal effects of hot gas and heated material. (Currently the severity on the sound and set up the heated gas and material is unknown and requires further research) 

Potential speaker: Turtlebox 


Dyno Testing

Concept: Run Dyno across RPM ranges and test muffler. 

Pros: Accurate reading to sound 

Cons: Dyno setup time, fuel, time to switch muffler.
