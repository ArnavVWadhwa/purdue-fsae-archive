Purdue Formula SAE : Three Point Bending

Created by Unknown User (mkotur), last modified on Apr 19, 2020

The main importance of the ANSYS 3-Point Bending simulation is that we are able to predict the material properties of our sandwich panels before testing them within a 15% error margin and estimate the predicted failure forces of these panels. This information is essential when filling SES and determining the layup schedule for each section of the monocoque.

To begin with, the sandwich panel is separated into three parts: the top laminate, the honeycomb core, and the bottom laminate. The following figures represent the way all three parts are made in NX 12.0. I will not go into much detail on how to CAD them because they are not complex parts. All parts are 500x275 mm as required by SES.

Figure 1. The Top Laminate

The top laminate is a surface and the “trim sheet” tool was used multiple times to get the division lines seen in the middle of the surface. These lines are important because the middle one represents the load line and the outer lines will be used to make a fine mesh in the center of the laminate. It is important to limit the number of elements. Since we know that the ply should fail in the middle, then we do not need to have a very fine mesh all across the laminates.

Figure 2. The Honeycomb Core

The honeycomb core is an extruded solid. In this case we have a CAD representation of a half inch core. Notice how in real life the core has cells, however, it is not possible to CAD a 1:1 scale core with all the cells because it would make meshing impossible and the part would turn out to be larger than 200MB (trust me I have tried). Just make sure to extrude towards the -z direction so that all the parts line up correctly.

Figure 3. The Bottom Laminate

The same practice used for the top laminate was used for the bottom laminate. The two outer lines shown in Figure 3 represent the two supports of the 3-point bending rig. Do not forget to offset the plane for the bottom laminate such that it is in contact with the bottom of the core (e.g if the core is 12.7 mm then offset it -12.7 mm in the z-direction).

Once all the parts are made, they are ready to be imported into ANSYS. Figure 4 shows the project schematic for the simulation. We will go through each module individually to make things easier.

Figure 4. Project Schematic for 3-point bending simulation

To begin with, in the ANSYS toolbox (on the left side of the screen) select three geometry modules in the components system section. For each module you can right click on the geometry with a question mark. Then go to import geometry and find your parts. For the honeycomb core no further action is required, however, for the laminates you must right click the geometry with a check mark box and select “Edit in Design Modeler”. 

Once inside Design Modeler, you will notice that your part is generally missing. If that is the case then right clock the “Import1” symbol and select “Generate”.

Figure 5. Generating your geometry in Design Modeler

Now that your geometry is generated, you should be able to see the transparent surfaces. The parts and bodies in Figure 5 should have also updated. You should have multiple parts and multiple bodies. However, we want 1 part with multiple bodies otherwise the individual surfaces will not be linked together when applying loading. To merge all the parts into one, you need to highlight all the parts, right click, and select “form new part”.

Figure 6. Merging all surfaces into one part

You should now see the same thing shown on Figure 6, above. You need to do this for the top and bottom laminate parts.

Now that the geometry is ready to be used, you need to import two “ACP (Pre)” and one “Mechanical Model” modules from the component systems section. The ACP (Pre) modules will be linked to the top and bottom laminate geometries, and the Mechanical Model will be linked to the core as shown in Figure 4, above. Additionally, it is recommended to link the “Engineering Data” of one module to all modules by left clicking and dragging the data box from one module to the next. That way changing the material properties of one module will change them in all modules instead of having to do it for each module.

This next part is involves adding the material properties of the carbon and the core, which can prove to be tedious since it is hard to sometimes find the components we want. Our main interest is the density, orthotropic elasticity, orthotropic stress limits, and ply type for our material.

Figure 7. Material Properties for T300 Woven 2x2 Twill 3k

Figure 8. Material Properties for Aluminum 5052-1/8in Cell size Core

Figure 9. Material Properties for Grafil TR50S 12K

 

Figures 7 to 9 each represent the material properties set up for the woven and unidirectional carbon and the honeycomb core that the team used in the year of 2019-2020. I will not go into more depth of how to add material properties.

Once the material properties have been set, it is time to move onto the meshing and setting up of the layup schedules. Let’s begin with the honeycomb core meshing.

Double click on the model box of the “Mechanical Model” module and wait for your part to open (it takes a bit of time). Expand the geometry portion shown in Figure 10, below, and click on the part shown. Under the part details, click on the material, which is set to steel as a default, and select the honeycomb properties.

Figure 10. Adding material properties to the part

Once the core has been given material properties, you will need to mesh the structure. In this situation, we know that the core should not fail before the plies. As a result, a fine mesh is not required for the core, but generate a mesh that is not too coarse as it may impact the contact stress between the laminates and the core, resulting in wrong numbers.

Figure 11. Meshing of the honeycomb core

In Figure 11, a body sizing tool was used to generate the mesh shown above.

Once the honeycomb core has been assigned a material and a mesh, it is time to move onto creating the layup schedules for the top and bottom laminates. I will go through the setup of the top laminate only, because the bottom laminate is nearly set up in the same way expect for one parameter, which I will mention later.

Double click on the model box in the “ACP (Pre)” module for the top laminate. Expand the geometry and click on the part. Assign any thickness and material property. Whatever is assigned for the thickness and the material property in ACP in the model box will not matter as ACP will disregard it and create the appropriate layup schedule. Your geometry should have a check mark and as said previously, the thickness and material property assignment can be anything as shown in Figure 12, below.

Figure 12. Geometry setup for the laminates

The meshing of the laminates is something important and should be done with thought put into it. Here is an example of how to mesh your laminate.

In Figure 13, below, a face meshing tool was used and the entire surface was highlighted to ensure that we would only obtain quadrilateral elements. Two edge sizing tools were used at the middle of the laminate and one face sizing tool was used for the rest of the laminate. The figures illustrated in Figure 13, below, show where each tool acted.

Figure 13. Meshing of the laminates

Once the meshing is applied to each section of the laminate, we are now ready to move onto the generation of the layup schedule. Close out of the “Mechanical Modeler” and double click on the Setup box in the ACP (Pre) module. Do not get overwhelmed by all the different options in this new ACP window. The first thing you want to do is expand the Material data as shown in Figure 14, below. You will see all the material data that your engineering database has. Next you want to right click and create a fabric.

Figure 14. Material Data in ACP

Figure 15, below, shows the interface for creating a fabric. Name your fabric something that makes sense. For example, name it T300 Woven, if it is for the woven carbon you are using. Under material, pick the carbon material that you want to use and then add the thickness to the material (make sure you check the units). In general, we have been assigning the thickness of the material as 0.19 mm, however it might be different for each carbon so see if you can get more reliable data. This number was found using a caliper on a fully made sandwich panel. Once you have finished setting up the fabric properties, click OK and create as many fabrics as you deem necessary.


Figure 15. Fabric Creation

Once you have created a fabric right click on the “stackups” symbol shown in Figure 16, below, and create a stackup. Keep the symmetry as the default and keep the default layup sequence as “Top-Down”. Next click on the grayed-out box under fabric and you should be able to select the material as well as the orientation angle of your ply. In Figure 16, a quasi-isotropic two-ply woven layup was created.

After having created the stackup, click apply and head over to the Analysis tab of the stackup window. In this tab you can see the material properties of the layup (E1, E2, G12) and the stiffness matrix of the layup by selecting an option in the “CLT Analysis” and checking the boxes of the material properties. For the two-ply layup, the following material properties should be observed using T300 2x2 Twill prepreg.



Figure 16. Stackup window and layup material properties

The Young’s modulus and Shear modulus at a 0° angle, shown in Figure 16, is what is used to compare with the experimental result outputted by SES. The next step involves creating the layup on the sandwich panel. To begin with, right click the “Oriented Selection Sets” symbol and create a set. This set will let the program know in which direction, which plane, and what geometry you want to stack your plies on. Select the “All_Elements” in the “Element Sets” symbol in the main window as the elements sets concerned with the orientation. Afterwards, you will need to add the direction as shown in Figure 17, below, to let the program know that you want to stack your plies in the positive z-direction. Finally, pick the default rosette in the “Rosettes” symbol in the main window and press ok.

Figure 17. Setting up the stackup direction, plane, and geometry

The oriented set should look like the one shown in Figure 17, above. Finally, it is possible to create the layup by right clicking the “Modeling Groups” symbol and creating a group. Name the group what makes sense and is easy to remember. Inside this new modeling group, you can create plies by right clicking your group and selecting “Create Ply”. The oriented selection set will be the one previously created in Figure 17. The ply material and ply angle depend on what layup schedule you are doing. Continuing with what was done previously, we made two plies with T300 2x2 Twill carbon at an angle of 0° and 45°. Note that if the ply order matters then you can create one ply below the previous ply by right clicking that ply and selecting “Create ply after/before”. Once you have finished adding your layup schedule hit the update button in the top right window (looks like a lightning symbol) and your part should update. The thickness shown in that screen should correspond to the number of plies you have added in your modeling group. It is a good time to also make sure that the units are correct because sometimes the units used are in mm or m. Figure 18, below, is a representation of what your updated part window should look like.

Figure 18. ACP (Pre) Part Window with thickness in m

If you are happy with your layup schedule than close out of ACP (Pre) and update your setup box. Now you would need to do the exact same thing for the bottom laminate, however, the only difference will be how you set up the oriented selection set. Figure 19, below, shows that the only difference lies in inputting your direction vector. This modification tells the program to stack plies in the negative z-direction. This is necessary so that the bottom laminate geometry does not overlap with the honeycomb core, which is right above it.

Figure 19. Modified oriented selection set for the bottom laminate

 

Once you have set up all both your laminates and your core, you can import a static structural module as shown in box D in Figure 4, above. You will need to link the “setup” boxes of the ACP (Pre) modules to the “model” box of the Static Structural module and make sure you import shell composite data. Importing solid composite data will not work. In the same way connect the “model” box of the Mechanical (Core) module to the Static Structural “model” box. When you have connected all three modules, double click the model box in the Static Structural module and wait for the program to open a new window (it can take a while). If everything was imported correctly, you should see the same thing as in Figure 20, below, as well as your part, with the top and bottom laminates and the core on the right side of your screen. 


Figure 20. Static Structural empty outline

The next step is now to add the boundary conditions to the sandwich panel as well as the force, which will serve as a representation of the real life 3-point bending test. Right click on the static structural title, hover your mouse on insert and select three displacement supports and one force loading condition. Select the first displacement support and set the X and Z components to 0. This will reference a pin joint by preventing the parts motion in two directions. Go to the bottom of your panel and select the left or right support line for your first displacement support. Figure 21 demonstrates how to select the geometry.

Figure 21. Setting the pinjoint boundary condition

Make sure that when you select the edge of interest shown in Figure 21, above, that you select the “edge” selection cursor shown on the top of Figure 21. Furthermore, to select the geometry, click on the desired edge, then click the empty yellow box of the geometry box and hit apply. The result should look similar to Figure 21. The second displacement support will reference a roller support, therefore, set the Z component to 0 and select the right edge as the line of action of the support similar to what was done in Figure 21. It should now make sense why the surfaces in Figures 1 and 3 were divided in to aid with the boundary condition setup.

The third displacement support is used to fix the panel in the Y direction because during the physical test, the part should not move in the Y direction. Therefore, set the Y component to 0 and select the front and back longitudinal faces of the core as shown in Figure 22, below. Do not forget to select the “face” selection cursor shown on the top of Figure 22 and use the “Ctrl” key to select multiple faces at once. The result should look like what is shown on Figure 22.

Figure 22. Fixing the Y-direction of the sandwich panel

Finally, the force loading condition must we set to finalize the modeling of the 3-point bending test. Rotate your part so that you are at the top and select the middle edge of your panel, without omitting the two small lines on the extremities of the panel. You should have 3 edges selected, as shown in Figure 23. Finally, change the definition of your force by clicking the “Define by” box and switching from vector notation to component notation. It is then up to you to apply an initial force. Remember that this force needs to be pushing into the panel and so you must add a negative sign to represent that the force is acting towards the negative z-direction.


Figure 23. Setting the Force loading condition

Your sandwich panel model is now complete and ready to be solved. The only thing left is to add the results which are able to tell give us information on the stiffness of the panel and failure force of the panel. Right click the “Solution” symbol and insert a directional and total deformation output, a Von Mises stress (Equivalent stress) output, and under the composite section, a composite failure tool. Figure 24, below shows what your solution outline should look like. The “Equivalent Stress” will give you a question mark asking which ply you want to analyze. You can choose to analyze a ply by expanding the imported plies in the outline and selecting a ply of interest. However, to get an overall view of the panel, under “Sub Scope by” change the selection from ply to layer to get the entire sandwich panel. Furthermore, click on the composite failure tool and turn on the “Maximum Stress”, “Face Sheet Wrinkling”, and “Von Mises” criteria to tell the tool what to look for. The datasheets only have accurate data for the stresses, and thus it is not possible to find accurate data using the other criteria. Also, delete the “Inverse Reserve Factor” and insert a “Safety Factor” by right clicking and hovering over insert for the composite failure tool. A safety factor is generally easier to use.

Figure 24. Solution outline

It is now possible to run the program by clicking the Solve button on the top bar of the screen. It will take a few minutes to run so please be patient. Once the solution has been solved, you want to start by looking at the deformation of the panel and making sure that the deformation looks symmetric and that the panel has an arc like shape, as shown in Figure 25. During the real test, the panel deforms in a shape similar to what is shown on Figure 25, which proves that the pinjoint and roller supports were a good representation of reality. Furthermore, check the directional displacement in the Y-direction to make sure that the displacement is negligible in comparison to the displacement in the Z-direction, as it should be in real life. Finally, to get the stiffness of the panel, simply input the displacement and force results for two points in the model and input them into the composite tab in SES.



Figure 25. Deformation of a 3-point bending sandwich panel in m

Next, if you select the composite failure tool safety factor, you will be given the factor of safety for your panel, as well as the mode of failure within each element. In terms of notation, “s” means stress, “t” means tension, “c” means compression, “1” refers to the x-direction or 0° direction, “2” refers to the y-direction or 90° direction, and “12” refers to the xy-direction or 45° direction. S1 and S2 refer to the normal stresses and S12 refers to the shear stress. There is no S3 or S13 and S23 because ACP creates 2D models. However, it is ok to analyze a simple 3-point bending test as a 2D stress problem due to the simplicity of the loading.

If you were to zone into the top laminate, you would see a result similar to what is shown in Figure 26, in such a way that the middle of the panel will have the lowest safety factor and as you move out of the force’s line of action, the safety factor increases. In this scenario, the tool is saying that the part is past failure and the mode of failure is due to shear. However, using engineering judgment based on the history of tested panels, the failure is usually due to the normal stresses. As a result, the panel should not fail when looking at the safety factor of the “S1c” elements. The objective is to reach a safety factor that is as close as possible to 1. Once this is achieved, the inputted force that resulted in that output can be used as the predictive failure force of the panel. This is therefore an iterative process and the answer depends on engineering judgment from passed experience of panel testing, as well as how close the safety factor is to 1 before a force is considered a failure force. If you want to have a conservative result, I suggest aiming for a safety factor of 1.15 to account for the estimated 15% error.

Figure 26. Safety Factor using the composite failure tool
