Purdue Formula SAE : Yaw Sensitivity Study

Created by Zhang, Chenkai, last modified on Jul 10, 2023

Method

Created a super dumbed down lap sim to study yaw sensitivity by evaluating lap times under different downforce/yaw sensitivity configuration. For each configuration, 360 laps were ran with wind from 360 degree of angles. Average, fastest, and slowest laps were recorded. A wind speed sweep of each configuration were ran, and a weighted average of lap times were achieved through by analyzing probability of each wind speed.

Assumptions

  • All the assumptions that goes into quasi steady state lap sim
  • Forget about all the complicated stuff with tires and suspension, tires now have combined friction coefficient of 1.56
  • 2019 Michigan track
  • Rolling resistance has a nonlinear component that is almost entirely made up to prevent the car from hitting top speed on straight (which it doesn't in real life). It follows the equation 0.000025 * fn^2 + 0.1 * fn - 250 N
  • Cl and Cd are read from yaw maps, values lower and higher than what's capture in the map are linearly extrapolated with two end points. The rest are linearly interpolated
  • Chassis slip angle follows a somewhat made up function of 0.15 * exp(-0.08 * speed [m/s] + 0.3) radians
  • Discretization error is a thing, since the track is divided into linear pieces
  • Drivability is a not factored into this
  • Aero balance shift is not factored into this

Yaw Sensitivity Curves

The target of the yaw sensitivity curve is to create something that we could possibly see as one of our end products. Decrease in yaw sensitivity usually means a decrease in yaw 0 performance as well, since yaw 0 needs to be sacrificed to make higher yaw angles work. Above shows the Cl vs. yaw angle plot of all the configurations tested. The base yaw sensitivity (purple) was obtained by running CZ_043 through yaw 0, 5, 15, 25, and 50 in CFD, and curve fit it with a 4th degree function. Although Cl is possibly too high for yaw angles between 25 and 45, it's a decent representation nonetheless. The rest of configurations were achieved by blending the base curve with a flat line at Cl of 4.2, a configuration we know is possible since that's roughly the performance characteristics of PF23 PDR aero package. The blending factor can be anyway between 0 and 1, with 0 returning the base curve and 1 returning the flat 4.2 Cl curve.

Above is a table of wind angle sweeps at fixed wind speed with different configurations. Here are a few takeaways,

  • Lap time is a function of not only wind speed, but wind direction. Some directions of wind can be extremely useful and speed the car up across a lap, and some do the opposite. The difference between the fastest and slowest lap at a fixed wind speed decreases as yaw sensitivity decreases. In other words, if we produce a yaw sensitive aero package, we need to bet on good luck more.
  • At a wind speed of 7 mph, the average at MIS in May, the lap times got slower ever so slightly as yaw sensitivity decreased. The difference between the base package and the 4.2 Cl flat line is less than half a tenth
  • At a wind speed of 1 mph, the base curve is on average 0.62s faster per lap than the insensitive. However, the trend reverses at 14 mph, with the base curve 0.66s slower.
  • At 80% insensitivity and above, average lap times actually got faster with an increase in wind. Which make sense since the higher the wind, the higher the total pressure.

Conclusion

After this study, I think we've been too worried about yaw sensitivity in the past to the point where it limited our overall performance, since we sacrificed yaw 0 Cl so much. Going forward, if there is easy yaw 0 performance on the table, we should take it, even if it is slightly worse for yaw sensitivity. 

However, the biggest limitation in this simulation is that driver confidence and drivability of the car is not taken into account. For driver confidence, we should still aim to reduce yaw sensitivity as much as possible. Gusts are also not modeled, which you would image picks up as average wind speed increases. Gusts will also present its own issue if the package is too sensitive. A gust in mid corner can at best mean corrections on steering wheel or getting out of power, at worst mean a cone hit or missed gate, which can easily outweigh the benefits of a theoretically fast aero package.
