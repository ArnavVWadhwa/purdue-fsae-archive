Purdue Formula SAE : Making Sense of Squiggly Lines | Notes

Chapter 1: Introduction

  • Graph FL and FR together above RL and RR in alphabetical order
  • Graph G Force Lat, G Force Long, and G Force Vert together and in alphabetical order
  • Channel Colors
    • FL, RL (Reds)
    • FR, RR (Blues)
    • Speed
    • RPM
    • Throttle
    • Steering
    • G-Force Lat
    • G-Force Long
    • Gear
  • The logging rate depends on how rapidly a channel changes value
    •  If the logging rate is too low, important values such as maximums can be lost
  • If a graph has anything other than time or distance on the x-axis it is called an X-Y plot (ex. G-G diagram)
    • Colors can be used on each data point to display an additional channel


Chapter 2: Speed

  • Speed can be calculated using wheel speed or GPS
    • GPS does not update fast enough → It is best to use WSS and use the GPS to check that data
  • To measure tire circumference mark the ground and tire then do a revolution, mark the ground again and measure the distance between the two marks
    • The tire radius shrinks when the car is on the ground
  • Ground Speed = Rolling/Front Wheels | Drive Speed = Driven/Rear Wheels 
    • Ground speed is the best option for vehicle speed outside of braking
    • In corner outside will read faster → An average is most accurate but will suffer inaccuracy from a single wheel lockup
    • Because of the above using the fastest is the best option: "The advantages in accuracy from averaging do not outweigh the reliability and consistency of selecting the fastest
  • Corrected Speed (Corr Speed) is where MoTeC makes use of the IMU to correct for total lockup for the most accurate speed measurement possible
  • GPS have a time delay associated with them being logged, a correction factor is necessary in i2 to offset this
  • Be aware of whether Time or Distance is on the x-axis as distance will distort the image and not show portions where vehicle speed is 0
  • When comparing two laps they should be stretched/shrunk to the same distance (not including out/in laps they will never line up as the variation is too large)
    • To check this is the case make sure minimum cornering speeds line up position-wise
  • Transition of tire contact patch from static to kinetic friction is called sliding in cornering, slip in acceleration, and lockup during braking
  • Small and repeated lockups of a given set of tires (F/R) typically indicate a brake bias problem
  • During acceleration rolling circumference decreases significantly, slightly offsetting the wheel speed measurement
  • Limited slip differential allows for lockup during acceleration and open diff during cornering
  • The most speed can be gained by improving high-speed corners


Chapter 3: RPM

  • For engine-related channels, an x-axis of time is preferred to distance so that times when the car is not moving do not disappear
  • Heat soak occurs when coolant, oil, etc. is heated while the car is stopped and higher temperatures are read across temperature sensors
  • Clutch slip can give the appearance of traction control problems in the RPM trace → Verify wheel slippage with wheel speed sensor readings
  • The RPM trace can provide better insight into slight speed variations between two drivers or laps
  • Over Rev = Any time the engine RPM goes above its intended limit
    • Typically caused by a damaged part
  • Put a horizontal line on the RMP limit in the RPM channel
  • X-Y Plot of RPM vs. Speed can help show if the driver is always shifting when they should
    • Overlap at 100% throttle can be caused by incorrect shift timing, short-shifting to save fuel, or for a cornering-specific reason
  • It is easier to find optimal shift points using horsepower (save the calculation to it)

         

         


Chapter 4: Gear

  • In general, a car accelerates faster in the lowest possible gear for any given speed
    • Exceptions to this are:
      • Driver trying to save fuel by driving at a lower RPM
      • Certain corners can be taken faster in a higher gear as the car is less sensitive to throttle input
      • Downshifts can delay braking and upshifts can delay acceleration (time gains due to gear change must outweigh the time lost during the gear change)


Chapter 5: Throttle

  • Throttle position is best shown as a percentage of maximum
  • The goal of throttle blipping is to match the engine revs required for the next lower gear
    • Reduces the impact of letting the clutch out a makes it possible to downshift without the clutch
  • Time between throttle blips should be longer for each consecutive downshift (greater difference between gear ratios)
  • Amount of throttle blip should increase for each consecutive downshift (greater difference in RPM with greater difference between gear ratios)
  • Over Run Fuel Cut = Stopping fuel injection when only the car's momentum is keeping the engine turning
  • Downshift throttle blipping should have no effect on brake pressure
  • Generally, constant radius corners are best suited for steady throttle and changing radius/banking ones are best taken with multiple lifts
  • There should be no throttle lifts on corner exit
  • Throttle position trace should be smooth (minimal handling upset)
  • Balanced Throttle = The small amount of throttle that keeps the car at constant speed (for max traction)
  • End of Corner Exit = Point where engine power is not strong enough to cause rear wheel slippage such that there is no concern that WOT will exceed the grip level of the drive tires
  • Cross-sectional area of throttle increases rapidly in the beginning (<30%) and falls off towards WOT (>60%)


Chapter 6: G Force

  • Accelerometer data must be filtered due to many small vibrations transmitted through the chassis
  • Stiffer chassis (ie. monocoque) will transfer more vibration through them
  • There are three ways to filter an accelerometer:
    • Low pass filter built into the sensor that filters the channel before it gets to the logger (increases delay and decreases resolution)
    • Anti-alias filter done by the logger in real time → Reads the sensor at a fast rate and applies a center-weighted average filter then logs at a slower rate (Requires more processing power and creates time delay equal to half the logging rate)
    • Filter applied in the analysis program (Requires very high logging rates up to 60000Hz)
  • G-Force Long can be used to see braking points by noticing a drop
  • Stiff suspension transfers weight more quickly (quick application of brakes with soft springs will lockup fronts before weight has transferred)
  • Turn in point can be found by looking at G Force Lat (should be near 0 along a straight)
  • G-G plot's largest use is showing the driver's trail braking skill graphically
  • It is helpful to color the dots of a G-G plot by speed for cars with high downforce


Chapter 7: Steering

  • Steering angle pos/neg should be the same as G Force Lat (+ left)
  • Steered angle should measure the angle of the outside tire (most steering is transmitted through the outside)
  • Radius of a corner can be calculated using speed and steered angle
  • Increased steering in an attempt to counter oversteer simply decreases the grip on the front tires and makes the situation worse (already on the decline of the tire traction curve)
  • Oversteer is indicated by a reduction in steering angle or a reversal of steering direction
  • Detecting oversteer requires an analysis of the equation G Force Lat = Speed x Speed x (tan(Steered Angle) / (-1 x Wheelbase))
    • If Speed increases so must G Force Lat unless Steered Angle is decreased
    • If Steered Angle Increases so must G Force Lat unless Speed is decreased
      • An increase in steering angle should be accompanied by an increase in G Force Lat, anywhere this is not the case the car experiences understeer so long as speed is not decreasing
  • Steering reference laps are the quickest way to spot oversteer/understeer (major differences in steering angle between the traces are indications)


Chapter 9: Section Timing

  • The fastest rolling minimum lap is the fastest lap if the start/finish line could be moved anywhere
  • The fastest theoretical lap (eclectic) is essentially an SAT super-score for lap times but is often not possible because of the influence different sections of a track have on each other
    • Made possible by grouping parts that influence each other into larger single sections
    • Useful when there is traffic to get an idea of what a good lap time could be
  • Within the section timing chart sections should be color-coded by how close they are to the fastest completion time for that section
